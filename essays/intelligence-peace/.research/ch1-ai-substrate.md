## Section 1: The Bull Case — EA Thinkers on AI's Transformative Potential

### 1.1 Holden Karnofsky — "Most Important Century" (2021)

The single best articulation of why AI matters at civilizational scale. Karnofsky (co-founder of Open Philanthropy, former co-CEO of GiveWell) argues:

- For 99% of human history, the global economy grew under 0.1% per year. Since the Industrial Revolution (~1800), growth has exceeded 2% per year. This growth rate *cannot continue indefinitely* — sustaining 2% growth for 10,000 years would require turning every atom in the galaxy into an economy as large as Earth's today.
- This means we are living in a *fundamentally unusual* period. Either growth slows back down (stagnation) or some transformative event occurs (explosion, then lock-in).
- AI is the most likely trigger: once we develop "PASTA" (Process for Automating Scientific and Technological Advancement), we get a productivity explosion that could compress centuries of progress into decades.
- Timeline estimate: >10% chance of transformative AI within 15 years (by ~2036), ~50% within 40 years (by ~2060), ~2/3 chance this century.
- The critical implication: this is humanity's last window to shape how the transition happens. The decisions made now — about AI governance, supply chains, international order — will reverberate for millennia.

**Source:** Karnofsky, Holden, "Most Important Century" series, Cold Takes (2021). Also available as consolidated PDF and discussed on 80,000 Hours podcast #109. **[Tier 2]**

### 1.2 Nick Bostrom — Superintelligence and the Substrate (2014)

- Bostrom's *Superintelligence* (2014) established the intellectual framework: once machine intelligence crosses a critical threshold, it may rapidly surpass human cognitive capabilities across all domains.
- The "substrate" metaphor originates here: intelligence can run on biological neurons or silicon — what matters is the *computational substrate*, not the biological form.
- Key insight for this essay: if intelligence is substrate-independent, then the *physical infrastructure* that produces AI chips becomes the most strategically important industrial system in human history.
- Bostrom's strategic analysis: the entity or coalition that first develops superintelligence gains a "decisive strategic advantage" — which is why every major power is racing to secure AI capability.

**Source:** Bostrom, Nick, *Superintelligence: Paths, Dangers, Strategies* (2014), Oxford University Press. **[Tier 1]**

### 1.3 MacAskill & Moorhouse — Intelligence Explosion Framework (2025)

- William MacAskill (Oxford, *What We Owe the Future*) and Fin Moorhouse argue that once AI can meaningfully substitute for human researchers, we should expect "a century's worth of progress in a decade" — even without recursive self-improvement, even if compute scaling stops.
- The mechanism: AI that accelerates research creates a feedback loop where each improvement enables faster subsequent improvement.
- MacAskill states he "takes seriously the idea of human-level artificial intelligence within the next three to five years, and takes even more seriously the idea of an intelligence explosion."
- Grand challenges from an intelligence explosion: new WMDs, AI-enabled autocracies, races for offworld resources, and digital beings worthy of moral consideration — alongside opportunities to dramatically improve quality of life.
- Critical argument: AGI preparedness is not just about alignment. We must prepare for the full range of developments an intelligence explosion would bring.

**Source:** MacAskill & Moorhouse, "Preparing for the Intelligence Explosion," Forethought Foundation (March 2025); arXiv:2506.14863. **[Tier 1/2]**

### 1.4 The EA Consensus

The EA community has been thinking about transformative AI longer and more rigorously than any other intellectual tradition. Across Karnofsky, Bostrom, MacAskill, Ord (*The Precipice*), and the broader EA ecosystem, there is a shared understanding:

1. AI is not just another technology — it is potentially the *last* general-purpose technology, because it can improve itself.
2. The transition period (now through ~2040) is the most consequential window in human history.
3. The physical infrastructure that enables AI (chips, supply chains, energy) is therefore the most strategically important industrial system ever built.
4. How we govern this infrastructure will determine whether AI leads to flourishing or catastrophe.

This series takes these premises seriously and asks a question even EA thinkers have largely neglected: *could the structure of the AI supply chain itself be a source of peace?*

## Section 2: Mainstream Confirmation — Institutional Economic Projections

The EA community's bull case is increasingly confirmed by mainstream institutions that approach AI from a more conservative, data-driven perspective. They see current technology and project forward — arriving at strikingly similar conclusions, albeit with less theoretical depth.

### 2.1 Convergent Projections

**Goldman Sachs (2023, updated 2025):**
- Generative AI could raise global GDP by 7% (~$7 trillion) and lift productivity growth by 1.5 percentage points over a 10-year period.
- AI investment globally forecast to approach $200 billion by 2025.
- Goldman forecasts AI beginning measurable impact on US GDP and labor productivity in 2027.
- **Source:** Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research, "Generative AI could raise global GDP by 7%" (Briggs/Kodnani, 2023); updated 2025. **[Tier 2]**

**PwC (2017, still widely cited):**
- AI could contribute up to $15.7 trillion to the global economy by 2030 — a 14% increase in global GDP.
- Regional breakdown: China gains 26% GDP boost; North America 14.5%.
- **Source:** PwC, "Sizing the Prize: PwC's Global Artificial Intelligence Study." **[Tier 2]** [VERIFY if updated post-generative-AI]

**McKinsey Global Institute (2023, updated 2025):**
- Generative AI alone could add $2.6–4.4 trillion annually in enterprise value.
- Total AI economic potential: $15.5–22.9 trillion annually by 2040.
- Automation midpoint accelerated to 2045 — roughly a decade earlier than previous estimates.
- **Source:** McKinsey, "The economic potential of generative AI" (June 2023); McKinsey State of AI 2025. **[Tier 2]**

**Convergence point:** Goldman, PwC, and McKinsey all project AI economic impact in the trillions-to-tens-of-trillions range. They are normie institutions — not EA true believers — which makes their convergence with the bull case all the more striking.

### 2.2 The Honesty Gap: Current Measured Impact vs. Projections

This is essential for intellectual credibility. The projections are enormous. The current measured reality is modest:

**Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Jan Hatzius (January 2026):**
- AI investment spending contributed "basically zero" to US GDP growth in 2025.
- Of 2.2% US GDP growth in 2025, only ~0.2pp came from AI investment — offset by heavy reliance on imported semiconductors from Taiwan and South Korea.
- **Source:** Goldman Sachs Economics Research, widely reported (January-February 2026). **[Tier 2/3]**

**Goldman Sachs Earnings Analysis (March 2026):**
- "We still do not find a meaningful relationship between productivity and AI adoption at the economy-wide level."
- However, in two localized use cases — software coding and customer service — companies report ~30% productivity gains.
- Only 10% of S&P 500 management teams could quantify AI's impact; 1% quantified impact on earnings.
- **Source:** Goldman Sachs equity research, reported in Fortune (March 3, 2026). **[Tier 2/3]**

**Framing for the chapter:** The gap between projection and measurement is the *signature of a general-purpose technology in its early deployment phase*. Electricity took 30+ years from commercial availability (1882) to measurable productivity impact (1920s). The internet was dismissed as economically irrelevant through much of the 1990s. Karnofsky explicitly anticipates this pattern — the Most Important Century thesis doesn't require AI to be transformative *today*, only that the trajectory is clear. ## Section 3: Military Integration — AI-Enabled Warfare Is Now Real

### 3.1 Operation Absolute Resolve — The Maduro Capture (January 2026)

The US military operation to capture Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro was the first major military operation where AI played a central, publicly acknowledged role:

- **Scale:** 150+ aircraft and drones, integrated space and cyber effects, multiple intelligence agencies.
- **AI involvement:** Anthropic's Claude was used via Palantir's platform for planning and analysis. The Maven Smart System (AI-powered image processing and tactical support) was deployed.
- **Drone operations:** RQ-170 Sentinel stealth drones maintained near-continuous AI-assisted surveillance over Caracas, mapping facilities, movement patterns, and defensive layouts.
- **Significance:** This was not a future scenario — it was an AI-enabled military operation that successfully captured a head of state.

**Source:** DefenseScoop, Fox News, TRT World, ExecutiveGov (January 2026). **[Tier 3]**

### 3.2 The Iran Strikes (February-March 2026)

AI-enabled warfare at industrial scale:

- Joint US-Israeli offensive: nearly 900 strikes on Iranian targets in the first 12 hours alone.
- **AI acceleration of the kill chain:** AI systems accelerated the entire process from target identification to legal approval to strike execution, operating "faster than the speed of thought" (Fortune).
- **Systems used:** Anthropic's Claude (via Palantir), Maven Smart System, autonomous drone systems.
- **Pentagon confirmation:** Secretary of War Pete Hegseth confirmed the US is deploying "autonomous systems and AI-enabled technologies" in the campaign.
- **Nature article:** "Iran war heralds era of AI-powered bombing" — academic recognition that AI-enabled warfare has arrived.
- **Debate triggered:** Questions about fully autonomous weapons, Anthropic's usage policies, and international law compliance.

**Source:** Nature (d41586-026-00710-w), Fortune, CBS News, New Republic, Pentagon statements (February-March 2026). **[Tier 1/3]**

### 3.3 Pentagon AI Budget and Programs

**FY2026 Budget:**
- $13.4 billion specifically for AI and autonomy — the first dedicated DoD budget line for these capabilities.
- Breakdown: unmanned aerial ($9.4B), maritime autonomous ($1.7B), DARPA ($1.7B), underwater ($734M), autonomous ground ($210M), CDAO ($45M).
- CDAO awarded $200 million in contracts to Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and xAI in 2025.
- **Source:** Pentagon FY2026 budget documents; Defense One; CDO Magazine. **[Tier 1/2]**

### 3.4 China / PLA: Military-Civil Fusion

- Official 2025 defense budget: ~$230B. Real estimate: $330-450B.
- Military-Civil Fusion strategy: majority of AI-related military suppliers are now civilian companies and universities, not traditional defense enterprises.
- Analysis of PLA procurement contracts reveals investment in unmanned systems, ISR, decision-making assistance, cyber operations, and information campaigns.
- **Source:** DoD Annual Report on PRC Military (December 2025); Georgetown CSET. **[Tier 1/2]**

### 3.5 Other Nations: UK, Israel

**United Kingdom:** Strategic Defence Review 2025 positions AI as "core enabler." GBP 400M Defence Innovation Fund. 10% equipment budget for novel AI/autonomous technologies. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Israel:** Most operationally aggressive user of military AI. AI-assisted targeting systems ("Lavender," "The Gospel") reportedly used in Gaza. New Mafat AI/autonomy research unit (January 2025). **[Tier 2/3]**

### 3.6 Revealed Preference Summary

The Maduro capture and Iran strikes are not future scenarios — they are *facts*. Every major military power is now treating AI as foundational to warfighting, not optional. The Pentagon's $13.4B budget line, China's MCF strategy, the UK's SDR, and Israel's operational use all confirm the same thing: AI-enabled warfare is the new norm. This is the strongest form of evidence about how seriously states take AI — they are betting their security on it.

## Section 4: AGI Timelines — The EA Forecasting Ecosystem

### 4.1 AI 2027 — The Scenario That Changed the Conversation

- Published by the AI Futures Project, a nonprofit founded by Daniel Kokotajlo (former OpenAI governance researcher) and Eli Lifland (ranked #1 on RAND Forecasting Initiative all-time leaderboard).
- Presents a detailed, concrete scenario for how AI could reach and exceed human-level capability by 2027.
- Written iteratively with two endings ("slowdown" and "race"), informed by trend extrapolations, wargames, expert feedback, and experience at OpenAI.
- Widely discussed and debated — Gary Marcus called it into question, MIRI engaged seriously, 80,000 Hours produced a video breakdown.
- **Significance for this chapter:** AI 2027 represents the most detailed attempt to make near-term AGI concrete and testable, not vague.

**Source:** Kokotajlo, Lifland, Larsen, Alexander, Dean, "AI 2027" (ai-2027.com, 2025); 80,000 Hours video breakdown (July 2025). **[Tier 2]**

### 4.2 Epoch AI — The Empirical Backbone

Epoch AI provides the most rigorous empirical tracking of AI capability trends:

- **Training compute:** Frontier AI models' compute has grown 5x per year since 2020. Since 2010, 4.4x per year.
- **Cost escalation:** Training frontier models has grown 3.5x per year since 2020. By 2027, the largest models could cost over $1 billion.
- **Concentration:** As of June 2025, 30+ models from 12 developers have exceeded 10^25 FLOP. Extrapolating: ~200 models will exceed 10^26 FLOP by 2030.
- **Infrastructure:** Total NVIDIA compute capacity growing ~2.3x per year. Power requirements growing >2x per year, on trend to reach multiple gigawatts by 2030.
- **Capex:** Combined capex at Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft, Oracle growing 72% per year, nearing $500B in 2025.

**Source:** Epoch AI (epoch.ai), "Compute trends across three eras of machine learning"; Epoch AI 2025 impact report; data insights. **[Tier 1/2]**

### 4.3 METR — Measuring Autonomous AI Capabilities

METR (Model Evaluation and Threat Research) provides the best empirical benchmark for AI autonomy:

- **Time horizon metric:** Measures the length of software tasks AI agents can independently complete. This has been doubling every ~7 months for the past 6 years.
- **Latest results (February 2026):** Claude Opus 4.6 achieved a 50%-time horizon of 14 hours 30 minutes — meaning it can autonomously complete software tasks that take humans ~14.5 hours, at a 50% success rate.
- **Trend:** Exponential. If the 7-month doubling time continues, AI agents will be able to complete multi-day tasks autonomously within 1-2 years.
- **Task suite:** Expanded from 170 to 228 tasks, including 31 tasks estimated to take humans 8+ hours.

**Source:** METR (metr.org), "Time Horizon 1.1" (January 2026); model evaluation reports. **[Tier 2]**

### 4.4 Ajeya Cotra — Biological Anchors

- Cotra's "Forecasting Transformative AI with Biological Anchors" (2020, updated 2022) is the most methodologically rigorous AI timeline forecast.
- Uses multiple "anchors" (evolutionary compute, lifetime compute, neural network scaling) to estimate when enough compute will be available to train transformative AI.
- Updated estimates: 15% by 2030, 35% by 2036, 50% by 2040, 60% by 2050.
- The biological anchors framework has been widely adopted as the baseline for serious AI timeline discussions in the EA community.

**Source:** Cotra, Ajeya, "Draft report on AI timelines," Open Philanthropy (2020, updated 2022); Cold Takes summary by Karnofsky. **[Tier 1/2]**

### 4.5 Grace et al. — The Largest AI Researcher Survey

- 2,778 respondents who published in top AI venues (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, AAAI, IJCAI, JMLR).
- Median estimate for human-level performance: 10% by 2027, 50% by 2047.
- The 2047 median is 13 years earlier than the same question in 2022 — a dramatic acceleration.

**Source:** Grace et al., "Thousands of AI Authors on the Future of AI," arXiv:2401.02843 (January 2024). **[Tier 1]**

### 4.6 Other Key EA Voices on Timelines

**Benjamin Todd (80,000 Hours co-founder):**
- "The Case for and Against AGI by 2030" — identifies four drivers of AI progress (larger base models, teaching models to reason, increasing thinking time, agent scaffolding) all set to continue until 2028-2032.
- Key insight: "At current rates, bottlenecks will likely start around 2030, meaning we'll likely either reach AGI by ~2030 or see progress slow significantly."
- **Source:** Todd, Benjamin, "The case for AGI by 2030," 80,000 Hours / benjamintodd.substack.com. **[Tier 2]**

**Rob Wiblin (80,000 Hours):**
- "What the hell happened with AGI timelines in 2025?" (recorded January 2026) — explores how sentiment swung after reasoning models (o1, o3), then blew back out further than before when scaling plateaued in H2 2025.
- **Source:** 80,000 Hours podcast (January 2026). **[Tier 2]**

**Peter Wildeford (co-founder, Institute for AI Policy and Strategy; Rethink Priorities):**
- Ranked #1 on RAND Forecasting Initiative all-time leaderboard. First place in Astral Codex Ten – Metaculus tournament 2025 (out of 2,975 competitors).
- Views 2025 as "make-or-break" year for AI trajectory signals.
- Co-founded the Institute for AI Policy and Strategy to bridge forecasting and governance.
- **Source:** Wildeford's Substack; Metaculus; podcast interviews. **[Tier 2]**

**Metaculus Community (February 2026):**
- 25% chance of AGI by 2029, 50% by 2033.
- Dramatic timeline compression: over four years, the mean estimate dropped from 50 years to 5 years.
- **Source:** Metaculus question #5121. **[Tier 2]**

### 4.7 Framing for the Chapter

The EA forecasting ecosystem — AI 2027, Epoch AI trends, METR benchmarks, Cotra's biological anchors, Grace et al., Todd, Wiblin, Wildeford — provides the most rigorous and comprehensive picture of AI trajectory available. Mainstream institutions (Goldman, McKinsey) see current capability and project forward; EA thinkers understand the deeper dynamics (scaling laws, feedback loops, intelligence explosion) that make the trajectory potentially explosive.

Whether AGI arrives in 5 years or 25, the convergence of expert opinion toward shorter timelines means the substrate thesis is conservative — it describes what is *already happening* (AI as economic and military foundation), not what might happen if AGI arrives. But the plausibility of near-term AGI raises the stakes enormously.

## Section 5: The Framing Gap

### 5.1 Current Discourse Landscape

**Frame 1: Existential risk / alignment.**
- Focus on whether superintelligent AI could be aligned with human values.
- Key voices: Anthropic, MIRI, Future of Life Institute, Bengio, Hinton.
- International AI Safety Report (2025) structures risks as: malicious use, malfunctions, systemic threats.
- **Source:** International AI Safety Report 2025; 80,000 Hours AI Safety Reading List 2025. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Frame 2: Disruption / socioeconomic harm.**
- Focus on jobs, inequality, bias, misinformation, deepfakes.
- **Source:** General discourse. **[Tier 4]**

**Frame 3: Geopolitical competition.**
- Focus on US-China AI race, export controls, semiconductor supply chains.
- Key voices: CSIS, CNAS, Georgetown CSET, RAND.
- **Source:** Think tank publications. **[Tier 2]**

### 5.2 The Missing Frame: Could AI Make the World Safer?

**What is almost entirely absent from discourse:**
- Could AI reduce the probability of great-power conflict?
- Could the economic interdependence created by AI supply chains function as a peace architecture?

**Evidence of the gap:**
- Cambridge University *Data & Policy* paper (2024): "peace remains a missing piece in conversations about AI" — a "blind spot that grows more urgent in the context of rising conflict and violence."
- UN Security Council open debates on AI focused almost entirely on risks and regulation, not conflict reduction.
- SIPRI Yearbook 2025 chapter on AI frames the question primarily through "how AI could create or exacerbate threats."
- Even MacAskill & Moorhouse, who identify "opportunities to dramatically improve quality of life and collective decision-making," do not systematically explore whether AI could reduce great-power conflict risk.
- **Source:** Cambridge *Data & Policy*; UN Security Council proceedings; SIPRI Yearbook 2025. **[Tier 1/2]**

### 5.3 Positioning the Essay Series

This series fills a specific intellectual gap:

1. It accepts the substrate thesis (AI as foundation of civilization) — this is Chapter 1's job.
2. It asks the question almost no one is asking: given this substrate architecture, could it *reduce* the probability of catastrophic conflict?
3. It draws on IR theory (deterrence, interdependence, collective security) rather than purely technical AI safety frameworks.
4. It takes the EA understanding of AI's trajectory seriously while applying it to a question the EA community has largely neglected.
