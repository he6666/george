**Series:** The Intelligence Peace Thesis

## Section 1: AI Economic Integration

### 1.1 Convergent Projections from Major Institutions

Three independent institutional analyses converge on the conclusion that AI will be the most significant economic force of the coming decades, though they differ in magnitude and timeframe:

**Goldman Sachs (2023, updated 2025):**
- Generative AI could raise global GDP by 7% (~$7 trillion) and lift productivity growth by 1.5 percentage points over a 10-year period.
- AI investment globally forecast to approach $200 billion by 2025.
- Goldman Sachs Research forecasts AI beginning to have a measurable impact on US GDP and labor productivity in 2027.
- **Source:** Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research, "Generative AI could raise global GDP by 7%" (Briggs/Kodnani, 2023); updated in "AI is showing very positive signs of eventually boosting GDP and productivity" (2025). **[Tier 2]**

**PwC (2017, still widely cited):**
- AI could contribute up to $15.7 trillion to the global economy by 2030 -- a 14% increase in global GDP.
- Of this, $6.6 trillion from increased productivity; $9.1 trillion from consumption-side effects (higher-quality, more personalized products driving demand).
- Regional breakdown: China gains 26% GDP boost; North America 14.5%. Together these account for ~70% ($10.7 trillion) of global AI economic impact.
- **Source:** PwC, "Sizing the Prize: PwC's Global Artificial Intelligence Study." **[Tier 2]**

**McKinsey Global Institute (2023, updated 2025):**
- Generative AI alone could add $2.6--4.4 trillion annually in enterprise value, with 75% concentrated in four areas: customer operations, marketing and sales, software engineering, and R&D.
- Broader AI (including agents and robotics): ~$2.9 trillion in annual US economic value by 2030 (midpoint automation scenario).
- Total AI economic potential: $15.5--22.9 trillion annually by 2040.
- Automation timeline accelerated: half of today's work activities could be automated between 2030 and 2060, with a midpoint of 2045 -- roughly a decade earlier than previous McKinsey estimates.
- **Source:** McKinsey, "The economic potential of generative AI: The next productivity frontier" (June 2023); McKinsey State of AI 2025. **[Tier 2]**

**Convergence point for the chapter:** All three institutions project AI economic impact in the trillions-to-tens-of-trillions range. They disagree on timing and exact magnitude, but the directional consensus is striking: AI is not a sector contributing marginal GDP -- it is projected to become the single largest driver of global economic growth.

### 1.2 The Honesty Gap: Current Measured Impact vs. Projections

This is perhaps the most important nuance for intellectual credibility. The projections above are enormous. The current measured reality is modest:

**Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Jan Hatzius (January 2026):**
- AI investment spending contributed "basically zero" to US GDP growth in 2025.
- Of the 2.2% US GDP growth in 2025, only ~0.2 percentage points came from AI investment -- and even that was offset by heavy reliance on imported semiconductors and equipment from Taiwan and South Korea, which boosted *those* economies rather than America's.
- Hatzius noted "there's been a lot of misreporting of the impact that AI investment had on GDP growth."
- **Source:** Goldman Sachs Economics Research, widely reported (Tom's Hardware, Yahoo Finance, Fortune, January-February 2026). **[Tier 2/3]**

**Goldman Sachs Earnings Analysis (March 2026):**
- "We still do not find a meaningful relationship between productivity and AI adoption at the economy-wide level."
- However, in two specific, localized use cases -- software coding and customer service -- companies report a median productivity gain of ~30%.
- Only 10% of S&P 500 management teams could quantify AI's impact on specific use cases; a mere 1% quantified impact on earnings.
- **Source:** Goldman Sachs equity research, reported in Fortune (March 3, 2026). **[Tier 2/3]**

**Survey Evidence:**
- 70% of firms actively using AI, but ~80% report no measurable impact on employment or productivity (survey of ~6,000 executives across US, Europe, Australia).
- MIT and RAND Corporation research suggests 70-85% of AI initiatives fail to meet expected outcomes.
- **Source:** Various, aggregated in Goldman Sachs and ISG reporting. **[Tier 2/3]**

**Framing for the chapter:** The gap between projection and current measurement is not evidence against AI's transformative potential -- it is the signature of a general-purpose technology in its early deployment phase. Electricity took 30+ years from commercial availability (1882) to measurable productivity impact (1920s). The internet was dismissed as economically irrelevant through much of the 1990s. The question is not whether AI's current GDP contribution is small -- it is whether the trajectory of investment, adoption, and capability improvement supports the projections. ### 1.3 Corporate Adoption and Investment Trajectories

**Enterprise Adoption Rates (2025):**
- 87% of large enterprises have implemented AI solutions.
- 65% of enterprises use generative AI, with 72% using it at least weekly.
- 88% anticipate generative AI budget increases in the next 12 months; 62% anticipate increases of 10% or more.
- **Source:** ISG State of Enterprise AI Adoption Report 2025; Menlo Ventures State of Generative AI in the Enterprise 2025; Kong survey; Wharton/GBK 2025 AI Adoption Report. **[Tier 2]**

**Venture Capital Funding:**
- Global VC investment in AI: ~$114 billion in 2024, surging to $211 billion in 2025 -- an 85% year-over-year increase.
- AI now captures 53% of global VC deal value (up from 32% in Q3 2024).
- Five companies alone (OpenAI, Scale AI, Anthropic, Project Prometheus, xAI) raised $84 billion in 2025, representing 20% of all venture capital funding globally.
- Geographic concentration: US accounts for ~75% ($194 billion) of all AI VC deal value.
- **Source:** OECD "Venture capital investments in artificial intelligence through 2025"; KPMG Venture Pulse; Crunchbase year-end 2025 analysis. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Hyperscaler Capital Expenditure:**
- Top 5 hyperscalers (Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta, Oracle) capex: ~$256 billion in 2024 --> ~$443 billion in 2025 --> projected ~$602 billion in 2026.
- ~75% ($450 billion in 2026) directly tied to AI infrastructure (servers, GPUs, data centers).
- Goldman Sachs projects total hyperscaler capex 2025-2027 will reach $1.15 trillion -- more than double the $477 billion spent 2022-2024.
- Capital intensity now reaches 45-57% of revenue -- historically unprecedented levels.
- Individual projections for 2026: Amazon ~$200B, Alphabet ~$175-185B, Meta ~$115-135B, Microsoft ~$120B+, Oracle ~$50B.
- **Source:** Goldman Sachs, IEEE ComSoc Technology Blog, Futurum Group, CreditSights. **[Tier 2/3]**

**Total AI spending (Gartner):**
- Worldwide AI spending forecast at $1.5 trillion in 2025.
- **Source:** Gartner forecast, reported in multiple outlets. **[Tier 2]**

## Section 2: Military Integration

### 2.1 United States: The Pentagon's AI Bet

**FY2026 Budget:**
- The Pentagon's $1.01 trillion FY2026 budget request includes $13.4 billion specifically for AI and autonomy -- the first time DoD has allocated a dedicated budget line for these capabilities.
- Breakdown: unmanned/remotely operated aerial vehicles ($9.4B), maritime autonomous systems ($1.7B), DARPA ($1.7B), underwater capabilities ($734M), autonomous ground vehicles ($210M), CDAO ($45M).
- **Source:** Pentagon FY2026 budget documents; Defense One; CDO Magazine. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office (CDAO):**
- FY2025 budget: $139.9 million.
- In 2025, CDAO awarded $200 million in contracts to commercial AI providers including Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and xAI -- signaling that the Pentagon is building its AI capabilities through commercial partnerships, not just internal development.
- **Source:** ExecutiveGov; Defense One. **[Tier 2/3]**

**Replicator Initiative:**
- Launched to field thousands of small, cheap, autonomous drones and counter-drone systems.
- Reality check: by mid-2025, DoD had fielded only hundreds, not thousands.
- In August 2025, Secretary Hegseth consolidated Replicator 2 resources into the Joint Interagency Task Force 401 for counter-small-UAS.
- **Source:** Congressional Research Service (IF12611); Defense One. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Revealed preference point for the chapter:** The $13.4 billion AI/autonomy line item, the $200M in commercial AI contracts, and the organizational restructuring (CDAO creation, Replicator, JIATF-401) demonstrate that the US defense establishment treats AI not as a niche capability but as a foundation of future warfighting.

### 2.2 China / PLA: Military-Civil Fusion for AI

**Overall Defense Spending:**
- China's official 2025 defense budget: ~$230 billion (announced figure). Real military expenditure, accounting for off-budget spending, estimated at $330-450 billion.
- **Source:** Heritage Foundation analysis; ORF (Observer Research Foundation) 2025 report. **[Tier 2]**

**AI-Specific Military Programs:**
- The State Council has pledged greater state investment in quantum computing, brain-computer interfaces, and AI as key aspects of PLA modernization.
- In 2024, Beijing invested in AI for: unmanned systems; intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR); decision-making assistance; cyber operations; and "information campaigns."
- China's commercial AI sector has narrowed the performance gap with US models, particularly in LLMs and reasoning models (per 2024 Pentagon report on Chinese military developments).
- **Source:** DoD Annual Report to Congress on Military and Security Developments Involving the PRC (December 2025); Georgetown CSET. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Military-Civil Fusion (MCF):**
- Analysis of PLA procurement contracts reveals that the majority of suppliers for AI-related military capabilities are now civilian companies and universities, not traditional state-owned defense enterprises.
- Chinese universities have established hundreds of MCF platforms and national defense laboratories specifically supporting PLA requirements in deep learning, machine vision, and intelligent robotics.
- **Source:** Georgetown CSET, "Pulling Back the Curtain on China's Military-Civil Fusion"; Georgetown CSET, "China's Military AI Wish List." **[Tier 1/2]**

**Analytical note:** Precise Chinese military AI spending figures are unavailable. The MCF strategy means AI military investment is systematically blurred with civilian R&D spending. This opacity is itself strategically significant.

### 2.3 Other Nations: UK, Israel

**United Kingdom:**
- Strategic Defence Review 2025 positions AI as a "core enabler" of operational effectiveness across UK Defence.
- New Defence Innovation Fund: initial budget of GBP 400 million for 2025, covering drone technology, autonomous systems, AI-enabled capabilities.
- MoD committed to spending at least 10% of its equipment procurement budget on novel technologies including AI and autonomous systems.
- Target: a digital "targeting web" by 2027 (MVP by 2026), leveraging AI for real-time data analysis and decision-making.
- Funding for drones and autonomous systems to be doubled under the SDR.
- Additional GBP 2.2 billion pledged for defence spending in 2025-26; overall target of 2.5% GDP on defence by April 2027.
- **Source:** UK Strategic Defence Review 2025; GOV.UK Defence Artificial Intelligence Strategy; Burges Salmon analysis. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Israel:**
- January 2025: Ministry of Defense created a new research unit within Mafat (Directorate of Defense Research and Development) focused on AI and autonomy.
- Mafat invested ~$40 million in contracts with drone companies, including acquisition of an interceptor drone.
- IDF five-year plan explicitly calls for far more robots and autonomous systems alongside air, sea, and land forces.
- Israel has been the most operationally aggressive user of military AI, with AI-assisted targeting systems (such as "Lavender" and "The Gospel") reportedly used in Gaza operations.
- A dedicated department for AI and Autonomy coordinates efforts, with some fully autonomous systems expected in the coming years.
- **Source:** Defense News; Jerusalem Post; Carnegie Endowment. **[Tier 2/3]**

**Revealed preference summary for the chapter:** Every major military power -- and many mid-tier ones -- is investing heavily in military AI. The US, China, UK, and Israel all treat AI as foundational to future military capability, not as an optional enhancement. This is the strongest form of evidence about how seriously states take AI: they are betting their security on it.

## Section 3: AGI Timelines

### 3.1 Grace et al. Survey (2024)

- Largest survey of AI researchers to date: 2,778 respondents who published in top AI venues (NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, AAAI, IJCAI, JMLR). Survey conducted fall 2023.
- Median estimate for when unaided machines could outperform humans at every possible task: 10% chance by 2027; 50% chance by 2047.
- This 50% estimate (2047) is 13 years earlier than the same question asked in a 2022 survey -- a dramatic acceleration in expectations.
- Near-term milestones: 50%+ chance by 2028 of AI autonomously constructing a payment processing site from scratch, creating an indistinguishable song, and downloading and fine-tuning a large language model.
- **Source:** Grace et al., "Thousands of AI Authors on the Future of AI," arXiv:2401.02843 (January 2024). **[Tier 1]**

### 3.2 Metaculus Community Forecasts

- As of February 2026, Metaculus forecasters: 25% chance of AGI by 2029, 50% chance by 2033.
- Median forecast for first general AI announcement: February 21, 2028 (range: August 2026 to October 2031).
- Broader definition (including robotics): median ~mid-2030.
- Dramatic timeline compression: over four years, the mean Metaculus estimate for AGI development dropped from 50 years to 5 years.
- **Source:** Metaculus question #5121 and related questions; 80,000 Hours analysis. **[Tier 2]**

### 3.3 80,000 Hours Expert Review (March 2025)

- Comprehensive review of five types of expert forecasters finds every group has shortened their AGI timelines in recent years.
- AI company leaders predict AGI in 2-5 years (shortest estimates, but also most knowledgeable about frontier capabilities).
- Key finding: "AGI before 2030 seems within the range of expert opinion, even if many disagree."
- Caveat: "None of the forecasts seem especially reliable, so they neither rule in nor rule out AGI arriving soon."
- **Source:** 80,000 Hours, "Shrinking AGI timelines: a review of expert forecasts" (March 2025). **[Tier 2]**

### 3.4 MacAskill & Moorhouse Intelligence Explosion Framework (2025)

- William MacAskill and Fin Moorhouse argue that once AI can meaningfully substitute for human researchers, we should expect "a century's worth of progress in a decade" -- even without recursive self-improvement, even if compute scaling stops.
- The mechanism: AI that accelerates research creates a feedback loop where each improvement enables faster subsequent improvement.
- MacAskill states he "takes seriously the idea of human-level artificial intelligence within the next three to five years, and takes even more seriously the idea of an intelligence explosion."
- They identify grand challenges arising from an intelligence explosion: new WMDs, AI-enabled autocracies, races for offworld resources, and digital beings worthy of moral consideration -- alongside opportunities to dramatically improve quality of life.
- Critical argument: AGI preparedness is not just about alignment. We must prepare for the full range of developments an intelligence explosion would bring.
- **Source:** MacAskill & Moorhouse, "Preparing for the Intelligence Explosion," Forethought Foundation (March 2025); arXiv:2506.14863. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Framing for the chapter:** Whether AGI arrives in 5 years or 25, the convergence of expert opinion toward shorter timelines means the substrate thesis is conservative -- it describes what is already happening (AI as economic and military foundation), not what might happen if AGI arrives. But the plausibility of near-term AGI raises the stakes enormously.

## Section 4: Striking Physical Facts

### 4.1 ASML's EUV Lithography Machine

- The EUV (Extreme Ultraviolet) lithography machine weighs ~150,000 kg (150 metric tons; the newer High-NA EUV tool weighs 165 tons).
- Contains over 100,000 individual components and 2 kilometers of cable.
- Requires 40 freight containers, 20 trucks, and three Boeing 747s to ship.
- The optical core alone (produced by Zeiss) consists of more than 40,000 parts and weighs ~12 tons.
- Price: over $370 million per unit for the latest High-NA models (~$150-200M for standard EUV).
- Only one company on Earth (ASML, Netherlands) can produce these machines.
- **Source:** ASML product documentation; CNBC; Zeiss press releases; Quartr research. **[Tier 2/3]**

**Why this matters for the chapter:** The ASML machine is a physical monument to the complexity and interdependence of the AI supply chain. No single nation can produce it. It is perhaps the most complex machine ever built by humans -- and it exists to make the chips that make AI possible.

### 4.2 The 70-Border Chip

- According to Accenture, the various inputs to a typical integrated circuit chip cross more than 70 international borders before a final product is delivered to consumers.
- The manufacture of a modern IC involves more than 1,000 separate processing steps in a dozen or more countries.
- The production process takes 4-6 months and involves more than 500 discrete stages.
- The supply chain is "transnational and tightly coupled" -- small disruptions reverberate widely.
- **Source:** Accenture analysis; CSIS, "Mapping the Semiconductor Supply Chain"; Harvard International Review; SEMI/DHL resilience study. **[Tier 2]**

### 4.3 Industry Investment Scale

**Semiconductor industry:**
- Global semiconductor equipment sales: $133 billion in 2025 (13.7% YoY growth), projected $145 billion in 2026.
- Wafer fab equipment: $115.7 billion in 2025, projected to reach $135.2 billion by 2027.
- Global semiconductor sales projected to surpass $1 trillion for the first time in 2026.
- **Source:** SEMI equipment forecast; WSTS; Deloitte 2026 Global Semiconductor Industry Outlook; SIA 2025 State of the Industry Report. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Total AI ecosystem spending:**
- Gartner: $1.5 trillion in worldwide AI spending in 2025.
- Hyperscaler capex alone: ~$443 billion in 2025, ~$602 billion projected for 2026.
- AI venture capital: $211 billion in 2025.
- **Source:** See Sections 1.3 above. **[Tier 2]**

**Physical metaphor for the chapter:** The AI substrate is not metaphorical. It is built from silicon refined to 99.9999999% purity, etched with light at wavelengths shorter than anything found in nature, in machines that weigh as much as a blue whale, following a supply chain that touches every inhabited continent. This is the physical reality of the substrate.

## Section 5: The Framing Gap

### 5.1 Current Discourse Landscape

The dominant frames in AI public discourse are:

**Frame 1: Existential risk / alignment.**
- Focus on whether superintelligent AI could be aligned with human values.
- Key voices: Anthropic, MIRI, Future of Life Institute, Yoshua Bengio, Geoffrey Hinton.
- The International AI Safety Report (2025) structures risks as: malicious use, malfunctions, and systemic threats.
- This frame has been "more persuasive to lawmakers and journalists than realist or skeptical views" (TechPolicy.Press).
- **Source:** International AI Safety Report 2025; 80,000 Hours AI Safety Reading List 2025; TechPolicy.Press. **[Tier 1/2]**

**Frame 2: Disruption / socioeconomic harm.**
- Focus on job displacement, inequality, bias, misinformation, deepfakes.
- Key voices: labor economists, ethicists, civil society organizations.
- **Source:** General discourse observation. **[Tier 4]**

**Frame 3: Geopolitical competition.**
- Focus on US-China AI race, export controls, semiconductor supply chains.
- Key voices: CSIS, CNAS, Georgetown CSET, Rand Corporation.
- **Source:** Think tank publications. **[Tier 2]**

### 5.2 The Missing Frame: Could AI Make the World Safer?

**What is almost entirely absent from mainstream discourse:**
- Could AI reduce the probability of great-power conflict?
- Could AI-enabled transparency and intelligence make war less likely?
- Could the economic interdependence created by AI supply chains function as a peace architecture?
- Could AI-enhanced deterrence stabilize rather than destabilize?

**Evidence of the gap:**
- A Cambridge University Data & Policy paper (2024) notes that "peace remains a missing piece in conversations about AI" and represents "a blind spot that grows more urgent in the context of rising conflict and violence."
- The UN Security Council's open debates on AI and international peace have focused almost entirely on risks and regulation, not on how AI might reduce conflict probability.
- SIPRI's 2025 Yearbook chapter on AI and international peace frames the question primarily through the lens of "how AI could create or exacerbate threats."
- Harvard Belfer Center has begun exploring "AI and the Future of Conflict Resolution" but this remains a marginal research thread.
- **Source:** Cambridge Data & Policy journal; UN Security Council proceedings; SIPRI Yearbook 2025; Belfer Center. **[Tier 1/2]**

**MacAskill & Moorhouse also identify this gap implicitly:** Their framework identifies risks from an intelligence explosion but also notes "opportunities to dramatically improve quality of life and collective decision-making." However, even they do not systematically explore whether AI could reduce great-power conflict risk.

### 5.3 Positioning the Essay Series

This essay series fills a specific intellectual gap:

1. It accepts the substrate thesis (AI as foundation of economic and military power) -- this is Chapter 1's job.
2. It asks the question almost no one is asking: given that AI is becoming the substrate of civilization, could this substrate architecture *reduce* the probability of catastrophic conflict?
3. It draws on international relations theory (deterrence, economic interdependence, transparency) rather than purely technical AI safety frameworks.
4. It is neither utopian nor dystopian -- it takes seriously both the risks and the structural possibilities.

The framing gap is the essay series' intellectual opening.

## Suggested Chapter Structure

1. **Opening image:** The ASML EUV machine -- 150 tons, 100,000 components, ships in 40 containers, costs $370M, made by one company in the Netherlands, and it exists to etch patterns smaller than a virus onto silicon. This is what the AI substrate looks like physically.

2. **The economic substrate:** Convergent projections ($7T--$15.7T--$22.9T). The honesty gap (current impact near zero, but trajectory is the signal, not the snapshot). Corporate adoption rates. The $600B hyperscaler capex surge.

3. **The military substrate:** Pentagon's $13.4B AI/autonomy line. China's MCF strategy. UK, Israel. Revealed preference: every major power is betting its security on AI.

4. **The timeline question:** Grace et al., Metaculus, 80,000 Hours review. MacAskill's "century in a decade." The convergence toward shorter timelines makes the substrate thesis conservative -- it describes what is already happening, not what might happen.

5. **The 70-border chip:** Physical interdependence. The supply chain as architecture. No nation can build AI alone. (This plants a seed for later chapters on interdependence and peace.)

6. **The framing gap:** Current discourse is about risk and disruption. Almost no one asks: could this substrate make the world safer? That is the question this series will answer.
