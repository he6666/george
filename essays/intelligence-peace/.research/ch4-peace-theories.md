## Section 1: The Liberal/Commercial Peace Tradition

### 1.1 Russett & Oneal — "Triangulating Peace" (2001)

**Citation:** Bruce Russett and John R. Oneal, *Triangulating Peace: Democracy, Interdependence, and International Organizations* (New York: W.W. Norton, 2001). **[Tier 1]**

**Core argument:** Building on Immanuel Kant's perpetual peace framework, Russett and Oneal identify three pillars — or legs of a "Kantian triangle" — that jointly reduce the probability of militarized interstate disputes (MIDs):

1. **Democracy** — Democratic dyads virtually never fight each other; democracies are also more peaceful in general than authoritarian states.
2. **Trade interdependence** — Higher bilateral trade flows increase the opportunity cost of conflict, reducing the probability of MIDs.
3. **International organizations** — Joint membership in intergovernmental organizations (IGOs) provides forums for dispute resolution and creates reputational costs for aggression.

**Key methodological contribution:** The study provided the most comprehensive large-N statistical analysis to that date, covering dyads from 1886 to 2001 [VERIFY exact end-year]. Each Kantian variable independently and significantly reduces conflict probability, and their effects are reinforcing ("virtuous cycle"). The three variables have both *dyadic* effects (the specific pair is more peaceful) and *systemic* effects (higher global levels of democracy, trade, and IGO membership reduce conflict throughout the system). **[Tier 1]**

**Relevance to IPT:** Russett and Oneal establish the empirical baseline that economic interdependence contributes to peace. IPT shares this foundational insight but argues that IPT's structural features produce a qualitatively stronger peace mechanism than generic trade flows (see Section 2 below).

---

### 1.2 Gartzke — "The Capitalist Peace" (2007)

**Citation:** Erik Gartzke, "The Capitalist Peace," *American Journal of Political Science* 51, no. 1 (January 2007): 166-191. **[Tier 1]**

Note: The publication venue is AJPS, not the *Journal of Peace Research* as sometimes misattributed.

**Core argument:** Gartzke contends that "capitalism, and not democracy, leads to peace." Three liberal economic processes drive the pacifying effect commonly attributed to democratic institutions:

1. **Economic development** — Wealthy, developed states have more to lose from conflict.
2. **Free markets and capital openness** — Financial integration creates cross-border stakes that raise the cost of war.
3. **Similar interstate interests** — Capitalist economies develop convergent policy preferences, reducing the issues over which states would fight.

**Key finding:** When measures of economic freedom and capital openness are included in standard democratic-peace regressions, the statistical significance of regime type diminishes substantially or disappears. The "democratic peace" may be largely a byproduct of the "capitalist peace." **[Tier 1]**

**Relevance to IPT:** Gartzke's argument strengthens the case that structural economic features — not just regime type — drive peace. IPT extends this logic: it is not capitalism in general but a *specific industrial structure* (concentrated, irreplaceable chokepoints in the AI supply chain) that creates the strongest peace-inducing constraints.

### 1.3 Barbieri — The Skeptical View

**Citation:** Katherine Barbieri, "Economic Interdependence: A Path to Peace or a Source of Interstate Conflict?" *Journal of Peace Research* 33, no. 1 (1996): 29-49. **[Tier 1]**

**Citation:** Katherine Barbieri, *The Liberal Illusion: Does Trade Promote Peace?* (Ann Arbor: University of Michigan Press, 2002). **[Tier 1]**

**Core argument:** Barbieri finds that the relationship between trade interdependence and conflict is *curvilinear*:

- Low to moderate interdependence reduces the likelihood of MIDs.
- *Extensive* economic linkages — whether symmetrical or asymmetrical — *increase* the probability of militarized disputes.
- Asymmetric dependence is particularly dangerous because it creates vulnerability and coercive leverage.

**Additional findings (2002 book):** Trade between adversaries often *continues during wartime*, contrary to liberal predictions. Using an interrupted time-series model, Barbieri finds that war does not have a significant impact on trading relationships in most cases. However, when states are both GATT/WTO members and benefit from increased trade, they are less likely to engage in MIDs — suggesting institutional context moderates the trade-peace relationship. **[Tier 1]**

**Relevance to IPT:** Barbieri's skepticism is important for IPT to address. Her finding that *extreme* interdependence can be destabilizing — particularly when asymmetric — might seem to challenge IPT. However, IPT can respond that Barbieri's cases involve *fungible* goods where the dependent party has exit options (finding alternative suppliers over time). In the AI supply chain, there is no exit option from chokepoint dependencies (see Section 2.4). Additionally, Barbieri's finding about asymmetric dependence being conflictual applies to *bilateral* asymmetry; IPT involves *multilateral* dependence on shared nodes, which changes the strategic calculus entirely.

### 1.4 Track Record: Statistical Support and Notable Failures

**Statistical consensus (as of the most recent literature):**

**Citation:** Havard Hegre, John R. Oneal, and Bruce Russett, "Trade Does Promote Peace: New Simultaneous Estimates of the Reciprocal Effects of Trade and Conflict," *Journal of Peace Research* 47, no. 6 (2010): 763-774. **[Tier 1]**

Hegre, Oneal, and Russett address the endogeneity critique (conflict reduces trade, so the peace-trade correlation may be spurious). Using simultaneous equation models, they confirm that trade *does* promote peace even after accounting for reverse causality: "trade encourages peace or reduces conflicts between country pairs, given that the costs associated with conflicts are substantial." **[Tier 1]**

**2025 systematic review:**

**Citation:** Romaeus Hover, Gertrud Buchenrieder, and Joel Sokolsky, "Economic Interdependence and the Likelihood of War — A Systematic Literature Review," *Cogent Social Sciences* 11, no. 1 (2025). Published online April 21, 2025. DOI: 10.1080/23311886.2025.2488114. **[Tier 1]**

This review examines studies from 1977 to 2022 and finds that the liberal school (economic interdependence reduces war likelihood due to high opportunity costs from disrupted trade) and the realist school (war has minimal impact on trade) produce conflicting results in the literature. The key reconciliation is the role of *moderator variables*: the effect of trade on conflict depends on the *type* of trade, the *structure* of dependence, institutional context, and regime type. The review highlights that the neglected effect of moderator variables has been responsible for much of the contradictory findings across decades of research. **[Tier 1]**

**Key finding for IPT:** The literature shows that it is not trade *volume* alone that creates peace, but *structural features* of trade relationships. This is precisely the distinction IPT makes.

### 1.5 What Types of Trade Create Peace? Structural Features vs. Volume

**Citation:** Erik Gartzke and Oliver Westerwinter, "The Complex Structure of Commercial Peace: Contrasting Trade Interdependence, Asymmetry, and Multipolarity," *Journal of Peace Research* 53, no. 3 (May 2016): 325-343. **[Tier 1]**

**Key findings on structural features:**

Three factors account for the heterogeneous effects of trade on conflict:

1. **Interdependence** — Bilateral trade dependence significantly decreases conflict probability. Interdependence can act as a *substitute for* or a *deterrent to* militarized violence. In the substitution role, interdependence actually *increases* non-militarized conflict while discouraging militarized disputes.
2. **Asymmetry** — Asymmetric trading ties *diminish* the conflict-inhibiting effect of trade, because "dependency cannot simultaneously be used to coerce and to inform." Asymmetric ties create more gains for the less dependent country, producing a more conflictive relationship.
3. **Multipolarity** — The number of alternative trading partners (outside options) moderates the peace effect. When states have many alternative partners, the exit cost from any one relationship is lower, reducing the constraining effect.

**Additional evidence on bilateral vs. global openness:**

Bilateral trade interdependence promotes peace more significantly for *contiguous* countries, whereas *global* trade openness contributes more to peace between distant countries. Both mechanisms operate, but through different channels. **[Tier 2 — synthesized from multiple sources]**

**Mechanisms identified in the literature:**

1. *Opportunity costs* — Disruption of trade flows imposes direct economic harm.
2. *Business elite lobbying* — Business elites who benefit from trade lobby against military escalation with important trading partners.
3. *Global market reputation* — An open trading system creates reputational costs: other trading partners prefer to do business with "peaceful" players. **[Tier 2]**

---

### 1.6 Exit Options and the Trade-Peace Relationship

**Key concept (from Baldwin, synthesized through subsequent literature):** David Baldwin conceptualized economic interdependence in terms of *exit costs* — the opportunity costs incurred from breaking existing economic ties. Higher exit costs mean greater constraining power of interdependence. **[Tier 2]**

**From Gartzke and Westerwinter (2016):** The multipolarity dimension captures the availability of alternative trading partners. When outside options exist, the exit cost of disrupting any one relationship is lower, weakening the peace effect. **[Tier 1]**

**From bargaining theory:** Awareness of high opportunity costs can be *war-making*, not just peace-making. If country A knows that country B faces enormous costs from trade disruption, A may increase its demands, making conflict more likely ("issue indivisibility under asymmetric exit costs"). **[Tier 2 — synthesized from bargaining literature]**

**Relevance to IPT:** This is a critical distinction. In most trade relationships, exit options exist: a country can find another oil supplier, another wheat exporter, another manufacturing hub — even if switching is costly. In the AI supply chain, *there are no exit options* for key chokepoints. There is one EUV lithography manufacturer (ASML, Netherlands), a handful of advanced foundries (TSMC dominates), and concentrated supplies of critical inputs. The impossibility of exit transforms the strategic calculus from "how costly would switching be?" to "there is no switch available." This makes the IPT mechanism qualitatively different from the generic trade-peace mechanism.

## Section 2: How IPT Relates to — and Differs from — the Commercial Peace

### 2.1 Shared Foundation

IPT shares with the commercial peace tradition the core insight that economic integration constrains conflict by making war self-destructive. The theoretical ancestry runs from Kant through Angell, Russett and Oneal, and Gartzke. IPT does not reject this tradition; it builds on it.

### 2.2 IPT's Four Structural Distinctions from Generic Trade-Peace

**Distinction (a): Existential vs. Substitutable Dependency**

In the standard commercial peace, trade goods are typically *substitutable*. Oil can be sourced from multiple producers; manufactured goods can be reshored or rerouted. The costs of substitution may be large, but the option exists. In the AI supply chain, the dependency is *existential*: advanced semiconductors are the foundation of military capability, economic competitiveness, and increasingly of national governance. And the chokepoint nodes that produce them — EUV lithography (ASML), advanced foundries (TSMC), high-bandwidth memory (SK Hynix/Samsung), and AI chip design (Nvidia) — cannot be replicated on any relevant time horizon.

**Supporting evidence:** ASML holds a complete monopoly on EUV lithography systems. No other company in the world can produce comparable machines. Every major chipmaker — TSMC, Samsung, Intel — depends on ASML's machines. Replicating ASML's technology would require not just one company but an entire ecosystem of hundreds of specialized suppliers, many of which are themselves sole-source. **[Tier 2 — widely reported, cross-verified across multiple sources]**

**Distinction (b): Chokepoints vs. Diffuse Trade Flows**

Standard trade flows are typically diffuse: many producers, many consumers, many routes. The AI supply chain is defined by *chokepoints* — nodes where a single entity or a small number of entities control an irreplaceable step. This is the topology that Farrell and Newman (2019) identify as enabling "weaponized interdependence," but IPT inverts the valence: the same chokepoint structure that enables coercion also *protects* the chokepoint holder from attack.

**Citation:** Henry Farrell and Abraham L. Newman, "Weaponized Interdependence: How Global Economic Networks Shape State Coercion," *International Security* 44, no. 1 (2019): 42-79. **[Tier 1]**

Farrell and Newman identify two mechanisms: the *panopticon effect* (network centrality enables surveillance) and the *chokepoint effect* (centrality enables exclusion). Their framework is about how powerful states *use* chokepoints offensively. IPT adds a defensive dimension: chokepoint nodes are protected because disrupting them would harm all dependent states simultaneously.

**Distinction (c): Multilateral vs. Bilateral Harm**

In the standard trade-peace framework, the harm from conflict is primarily *bilateral*: states A and B lose their mutual trade gains. Third parties may be affected, but the primary constraint operates within the dyad. In the AI supply chain, attacking any chokepoint node harms *every dependent nation simultaneously*. An attack on ASML does not merely harm the Netherlands and the attacker — it harms Taiwan (TSMC loses its lithography supplier), South Korea (Samsung loses its supplier), the United States (every major tech company loses access to advanced chips), Japan, and dozens of other nations. This creates a *collective security* incentive: every dependent nation individually has a reason to defend the chokepoint node, regardless of alliance obligations.

**Distinction (d): No Exit Option**

As established in Section 1.6, the critical moderating variable in the trade-peace literature is the availability of *exit options*. When exit options exist, the constraining power of interdependence is weakened. In the AI supply chain, exit options do not exist on any policy-relevant time horizon. Building a new EUV manufacturer from scratch would take, at minimum, a decade and tens of billions of dollars — and no one has succeeded in doing so. This means the standard realist escape route from commercial peace (find alternative suppliers, build domestic capacity, accept short-term costs for long-term autonomy) is blocked.

## Section 3: Nuclear Deterrence Comparison

### 3.1 Nuclear Deterrence — Track Record Since 1945

**Citation:** John Lewis Gaddis, *The Long Peace: Inquiries into the History of the Cold War* (New York: Oxford University Press, 1987). **[Tier 1]**

**Citation:** Kenneth N. Waltz, "Nuclear Myths and Political Realities," *American Political Science Review* 84, no. 3 (1990): 731-745. **[Tier 1]** [VERIFY exact citation details]

**Citation:** Thomas C. Schelling, *Arms and Influence* (New Haven: Yale University Press, 1966). **[Tier 1]**

Gaddis coined the term "the Long Peace" to describe the absence of direct great-power war since 1945. The role of nuclear weapons in this peace is debated:

- **Waltz (neorealist):** Nuclear weapons created stability by making the costs of great-power war unacceptably high. Bipolarity and nuclear deterrence together explain the Long Peace. Stability depends on the amount of damage each side can inflict — a condition met when both possess secure second-strike capabilities. **[Tier 1]**
- **Gaddis:** The Long Peace resulted from multiple factors, not nuclear weapons alone. Gaddis attributes it partly to the absence of political reasons for starting a great-power war, not exclusively to deterrence. **[Tier 1]**
- **Schelling (1966):** Nuclear weapons divorced military power from coercive power. Before 1945, coercion rested on the credible threat of military victory. Nuclear weapons allowed an opponent to inflict unbearably high costs *even in the process of losing a war*. Military strategy was no longer "the science of military victory" but "the art of coercion, intimidation, and deterrence." **[Tier 1]**

**Core mechanism of nuclear deterrence (MAD):** Both sides maintain secure second-strike capabilities — nuclear forces that can survive a first strike and retaliate with devastating effect. The nuclear triad (land-based ICBMs, submarine-launched ballistic missiles, strategic bombers) ensures that no first strike can eliminate retaliatory capacity. This mutual vulnerability creates stability: if both sides know they can destroy each other, neither risks starting a nuclear war. **[Tier 1 — standard doctrine, widely verified]**

**Known weaknesses:**

1. *Credibility problem:* Following through on a nuclear threat means self-destruction. Rational adversaries may doubt that retaliation will actually occur, especially for *extended deterrence* (defending allies). Would the U.S. risk nuclear war to defend Estonia? **[Tier 1]**
2. *Requires rational actors:* Deterrence assumes leaders make cost-benefit calculations. It does not account for miscalculation, accidental launch, rogue actors, or leaders operating in information echo chambers. **[Tier 2]**
3. *Does not prevent conventional war:* Nuclear deterrence did not prevent Korea, Vietnam, Afghanistan, or numerous proxy wars. It constrains only the most extreme escalation. **[Tier 1]**
4. *Proliferation risk:* The more states that acquire nuclear weapons, the higher the probability of eventual use through accident or miscalculation. **[Tier 2]**

---

### 3.2 How IPT Is Similar to Nuclear Deterrence

Both IPT and nuclear deterrence rest on the same foundational logic: the costs of aggression are so catastrophic that rational actors are deterred from attacking. In both cases, the "weapon" is not military capability per se but the certainty of devastating consequences.

- **Catastrophic cost structure:** Just as a nuclear exchange would destroy the attacker's own society, attacking an AI supply chain chokepoint would destroy the attacker's own access to advanced computation — the foundation of modern military capability, economic competitiveness, and increasingly of governance.
- **Deterrence through self-harm:** In both cases, the aggressor's action produces consequences so severe that the attack is self-defeating.

---

### 3.3 How IPT Differs from Nuclear Deterrence

**Difference (a): Structural vs. Volitional**

Nuclear deterrence requires a *decision to retaliate*. A leader must choose to launch a second strike. This is the source of the credibility problem: adversaries can doubt that retaliation will occur. In IPT, the "retaliation" is *automatic and structural*. If ASML's Veldhoven facility is destroyed, there is no decision required for the consequences to materialize — advanced chip production simply stops globally. The destruction is embedded in the structure of the supply chain, not in a leader's willingness to press a button.

**Difference (b): Multilateral vs. Bilateral**

Nuclear deterrence is fundamentally *bilateral*: the U.S. deters Russia, Russia deters the U.S. Extended deterrence to allies is derivative and suffers from the credibility gap. IPT's deterrence is inherently *multilateral*: an attack on any chokepoint node simultaneously threatens every dependent nation. There is no need for alliance commitments or credibility signals — every affected state has an independent, self-interested reason to prevent the attack.

**Difference (c): Protects Small States by Structural Position**

Nuclear deterrence protects states roughly in proportion to their military power or alliance relationships with nuclear powers. A small state without nuclear weapons or a nuclear patron is unprotected. Under IPT, the Netherlands — a country of 17 million people with no nuclear weapons — matters more to global security than most large nations because it is home to ASML. Its protection derives not from military capability or alliance obligations but from its *structural position* in the supply chain. Taiwan (23 million people) is similarly protected by hosting TSMC. This is a fundamentally different logic of protection.

**Difference (d): No Arsenal Needed**

Nuclear deterrence requires enormous investment in weapons systems, delivery vehicles, command-and-control infrastructure, and continuous modernization. IPT requires no arsenal. The "deterrent" is a civilian industrial asset that was built for commercial purposes. The protective effect is an emergent property of the supply chain structure, not a deliberately constructed weapons system.

## Section 4: WWI — The Perennial Counterexample

### 4.1 The Standard Objection

The outbreak of World War I in 1914, amid historically high levels of economic integration, is the most frequently cited counterexample to the commercial peace thesis. If trade prevents war, why did the most economically integrated powers of 1914 go to war?

### 4.2 Gartzke & Lupu — The War Began in the Weakly Integrated Balkans

**Citation:** Erik Gartzke and Yonatan Lupu, "Trading on Preconceptions: Why World War I Was Not a Failure of Economic Interdependence," *International Security* 36, no. 4 (Spring 2012): 115-150. **[Tier 1]**

**Core argument:** The conventional view that WWI demonstrates the failure of economic interdependence "relies on methodologically flawed inference and an incomplete accounting of the antecedents to the war."

**Key findings:**

1. **The war began in the weakly integrated Balkans.** Serbia and Austria-Hungary were not highly integrated trading partners. Serbia had been actively reducing its economic dependence on Austria-Hungary, increasing French imports instead. The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand and the subsequent Austro-Serbian crisis occurred in the *least* economically integrated part of Europe.

2. **Crises among highly integrated Western powers were resolved peacefully.** The Fashoda crisis (1898, Britain-France), the Moroccan crises (1905, 1911), and the Agadir crisis (1911) — all involving highly integrated Western powers — were resolved without war. Economic integration *did* constrain these dyads.

3. **Alliance entanglement dragged integrated states into a conflict that originated in the periphery.** The alliance system — not trade integration — was the transmission mechanism that spread a Balkan conflict into a world war. Russia's commitment to Serbia, Germany's "blank check" to Austria-Hungary, France's alliance with Russia, and Britain's entente obligations created a chain of escalation that overrode the constraining effects of trade.

4. **Repeated Balkan conflicts had been localized before 1914.** The Balkan Wars of 1912-1913 flared up but were contained. The difference in 1914 was not that economic integration failed but that the alliance system entangled integrated powers in a peripheral conflict.

**Relevance to IPT:** Gartzke and Lupu's reinterpretation is directly useful. IPT can argue that WWI actually *supports* the commercial peace thesis when properly analyzed — trade integration did constrain the integrated core; the failure was in alliance entanglement with the non-integrated periphery. IPT is even more robust against this objection because the AI supply chain's integration is not peripheral — it is central to every major power's core strategic interests.

---

### 4.3 Norman Angell — "The Great Illusion" (1910)

**Citation:** Norman Angell, *The Great Illusion* (London: William Heinemann, 1910; first edition as *Europe's Optical Illusion*, 1909). **[Tier 1]**

**What Angell actually argued:** Angell is routinely mischaracterized as having predicted that war was impossible because of economic integration. In fact, Angell argued that war was *futile* — "useless, even when completely victorious, as a means of securing those moral or material ends which represent the needs of modern civilized peoples." He never said war was impossible, only that it was economically irrational. His "Great Illusion" was the *illusion that nations gained by armed confrontation*: the belief that conquest pays.

**Angell's key insight:** Wealth in the modern world "is founded upon credit and commercial contract," and these are "the outgrowth of economic interdependence due to the increasing division of labour and greatly developed communication." Conquest cannot transfer this kind of wealth because it depends on institutional trust and voluntary exchange. **[Tier 1]**

**Relevance to IPT:** Angell was *right* that war was futile among economically integrated states — he merely could not prevent it. IPT argues something stronger: not merely that attacking an AI supply chain chokepoint is *futile* (producing no gains), but that it is *automatically self-destructive* (producing catastrophic losses for the attacker itself). Angell showed that conquest does not pay; IPT shows that disruption actively harms the disruptor. This is a qualitative escalation from "war is irrational" to "war is structurally suicidal."

---

### 4.4 Pre-WWI Trade and the Decision for War — Empirical Literature

The broader empirical literature confirms that pre-WWI trade levels, while high by historical standards, were not uniformly high across all belligerent dyads:

- Anglo-German trade was substantial, and the Anglo-German rivalry was characterized by repeated crisis management and avoidance of direct war.
- Austro-Serbian trade was comparatively modest, and it was in this low-integration dyad that the casus belli originated.
- The decision for war in 1914 was driven by alliance commitments, military timetables (the Schlieffen Plan's inflexibility), and fears of relative decline — not by a failure of trade to restrain. **[Tier 2 — synthesized from Gartzke & Lupu and broader WWI historiography]**

## Section 5: Russia-Ukraine — The Recent Counterexample

### 5.1 Copeland's Trade Expectations Theory Applied

**Citation:** Dale C. Copeland, *Economic Interdependence and War* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2015). Winner of the 2017 ISA Annual Best Book Award. **[Tier 1]**

**Core theory:** It is not *current* trade levels that determine whether interdependence promotes peace, but *expectations about future trade*. When leaders expect the future trade environment to remain favorable, they have incentives to maintain peace. When expectations turn *negative* — when leaders anticipate being cut off from vital trade flows — they are more likely to initiate conflict as the "lesser of two evils."

**Application to Russia-Ukraine:** Putin's decision to invade in February 2022 aligns with Copeland's framework:

- **Negative expectations about future energy leverage:** Ukraine's moves in late 2021 to deepen political and economic ties with the West (and the EU's long-term decarbonization agenda) threatened Russia's future energy leverage. Putin anticipated that Russia's primary source of geopolitical influence — energy dependency — would decline over time. **[Tier 2]**
- **"Now or never" logic:** If Russia's leverage was depreciating, the expected future trade environment was negative. This created incentives to act while leverage remained. **[Tier 2]**
- **Nord Stream as coercive tool:** After completion of Nord Stream 1 (2011) and progress on Nord Stream 2, Russia had already weaponized energy interdependence — blocking all trade with Ukraine in August 2013 as a warning against Kyiv's planned EU association agreement. **[Tier 2]**

**Citation for Russia-Ukraine analysis:** Dylan Blyth, "Russia and the Ukraine Crisis: An Analysis of Trade Expectations Theory," Swansea University (2022), deposited at Cronfa repository. **[Tier 2]**

**Citation:** Sarang Shidore and Aditya Bhan, "Economic Interdependence and Realistic Interests: A Review of the Russia-Ukraine Conflict," *Strategic Analysis* 47, no. 3 (2023). **[Tier 1]**

---

### 5.2 Five Structural Reasons Russia-Ukraine Is Not Analogous to AI Supply Chains

**Reason (a): Energy is fungible; semiconductors are not.**

Russia's primary export to Europe was natural gas and oil — commodities that, while difficult to replace in the short term, are *substitutable* over time. Europe has in fact substantially replaced Russian gas with Norwegian, Qatari, and American LNG since 2022. Russia has redirected oil exports to India and China, albeit at discounted prices. This substitutability is precisely what the exit-options literature predicts: when alternatives exist, the constraining effect of interdependence is weaker. Advanced semiconductors are *not* fungible. There is no alternative EUV lithography supplier. There is no alternative to TSMC for cutting-edge logic fabrication at scale. The exit option does not exist.

**Reason (b): Russia was a supplier, not a consumer — IPT's restraint mechanism works differently on consumers.**

Russia occupied the *supplier* side of the energy relationship. Suppliers can choose to withhold exports as a coercive tool (and Russia did exactly this). The risk calculus for a supplier-aggressor is different: Russia calculated that it could survive losing European customers by finding new ones (China, India). IPT's restraint mechanism operates primarily on *consumers* (and on third parties dependent on the same supply chain). A consumer that attacks a chokepoint supplier destroys its own access. Russia's position as an energy *supplier* with alternative customers is structurally different from a nation dependent on a monopoly *supplier* with no alternative source.

**Reason (c): Bilateral dependency vs. multilateral dependency.**

Russia-Europe energy interdependence was fundamentally *bilateral* (or at most, a small number of dyads). Russia's leverage over Germany was different from its leverage over Poland or its leverage over Turkey. Each dyad could be disaggregated. In the AI supply chain, dependency is *multilateral*: every advanced economy depends on the *same* chokepoints simultaneously. An attack on ASML does not harm one country — it harms everyone. This creates collective defense incentives that the Russia-Europe energy relationship did not generate.

**Reason (d): Fait accompli strategy — grab territory before costs materialize.**

Putin's strategy was designed as a *fait accompli*: seize Kyiv in 72 hours, install a puppet government, and present the world with an irreversible outcome before sanctions and military aid could materialize. This strategy relies on *speed* to outrun the economic costs of aggression. The fait accompli model is not applicable to AI supply chain chokepoints because the consequences of disruption are *immediate and automatic*. There is no "grabbing" ASML's technology quickly — destroying the facility destroys the capability; seizing it without its workforce and supply ecosystem renders it useless. The costs cannot be outrun.

**Citation on fait accompli and miscalculation:** Multiple sources confirm that Russia expected a quick victory. "Russian military planners overestimated their own military capabilities, assuming they could rapidly seize the capital with minimal resistance." The pattern of prior quick victories — Georgia (2008), Crimea (2014), Syria — created overconfidence. **[Tier 2 — synthesized from CSIS, LSE, Georgetown analyses]**

**Reason (e): Authoritarian decision-making — echo chamber, no institutional checks.**

Putin's decision to invade was made within an authoritarian echo chamber. Intelligence agencies told Putin what he wanted to hear. There were no institutional checks — no parliamentary debate, no free press scrutiny, no independent military assessment process. The decision reflected one leader's perception of a closing window of opportunity, filtered through sycophantic advisers.

**Citation:** "Inside Vladimir Putin's Hall of Mirrors: How the Kremlin's Miscalculation of Western Resolve Emboldened Russia's Invasion of Ukraine," *Nationalities Papers* (Cambridge University Press, 2023). **[Tier 1]**

This is relevant but does not *distinguish* IPT from other peace theories — authoritarian miscalculation is a risk for all deterrence frameworks, including nuclear deterrence. IPT's response is that even under authoritarian decision-making, the *automatic* nature of supply chain disruption (Distinction 3a above) reduces the space for miscalculation: there is no need to calculate whether the adversary will retaliate, because the disruption is self-executing.

### 5.3 Best Academic Analyses of Why Interdependence "Failed" in Russia-Ukraine

1. **Copeland framework (trade expectations):** Interdependence did not "fail" — it was *anticipated to decline*, which is precisely when Copeland's theory predicts conflict. The key variable was not current trade levels but expectations about *future* trade. **[Tier 1]**

2. **Farrell and Newman (weaponized interdependence):** Russia had already *weaponized* energy interdependence as a coercive tool, demonstrating that interdependence does not automatically promote peace — it can also be a weapon. The question is whether the *structure* of interdependence makes weaponization or disruption self-destructive. In the energy case, it did not (Russia found alternative customers). In the AI supply chain case, IPT argues it would. **[Tier 1]**

3. **Asymmetric vulnerability analysis:** Germany's dependence on Russian gas was *asymmetric* — Germany needed Russian gas more than Russia needed German payments (at least in the short term). This asymmetry gave Russia coercive leverage. In the AI supply chain, the asymmetry runs in the opposite direction for potential aggressors: major powers *need* the chokepoint suppliers more than the suppliers need any single customer, and there are no alternative suppliers. **[Tier 2]**

4. **War in Ukraine as a test of the "weaponized interdependence" thesis:**

**Citation:** "The War in Ukraine: The Deterrent Effect of Weaponized Interdependence," *Nationalities Papers* (Cambridge University Press, 2024). **[Tier 1]**

This analysis argues that the deterrent effect of economic interdependence "is only effective for a short bilateral conflict because the third party will eventually activate counterforces, reducing economic dependency and increasing the likelihood to intervene on behalf of the target." This temporal dimension — interdependence deters short conflicts but not sustained ones — is relevant to IPT: because AI supply chain disruption is *immediate and permanent* (rebuilding takes a decade, not months), the temporal limitation does not apply.

| Feature | Commercial Peace | Nuclear Deterrence | IPT |
|---|---|---|---|
| Core mechanism | Opportunity costs of lost trade | Catastrophic costs of retaliation | Catastrophic costs of supply chain disruption |
| Type of cost | Economic | Existential (physical destruction) | Existential (loss of AI capability = military/economic collapse) |
| Requires a decision to retaliate? | N/A (costs are automatic) | Yes (credibility problem) | No (disruption is automatic) |
| Bilateral or multilateral? | Primarily bilateral | Primarily bilateral | Inherently multilateral |
| Protects small states? | Only if they are significant trade partners | Only if allied to nuclear power | Yes, by structural position (Netherlands, Taiwan) |
| Exit options available? | Usually yes (substitutable goods) | N/A | No (irreplaceable chokepoints) |
| Arsenal required? | No | Yes (enormous) | No |

### 6.2 What IPT Adds

IPT's novel contribution is the combination of four features that no prior peace theory possesses simultaneously:

1. **Automatic costs** (shared with commercial peace, distinct from nuclear deterrence)
2. **Existential stakes** (shared with nuclear deterrence, distinct from commercial peace)
3. **Multilateral scope** (distinct from both)
4. **No exit option** (distinct from both)

Any one of these features alone would not be sufficient. It is their *conjunction* that makes IPT a distinct and potentially more robust peace mechanism than either the commercial peace or nuclear deterrence alone.
