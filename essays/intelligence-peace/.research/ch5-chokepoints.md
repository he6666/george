## SECTION 1: FARRELL & NEWMAN'S FRAMEWORK — DETAILED TREATMENT

### 1A. The Foundational Paper (2019)

**Citation:** Henry Farrell and Abraham L. Newman, "Weaponized Interdependence: How Global Economic Networks Shape State Coercion," *International Security* 44, no. 1 (Summer 2019): 42-79.
- **Tier:** 1
- **URL:** https://direct.mit.edu/isec/article/44/1/42/12237/Weaponized-Interdependence-How-Global-Economic

**Core Framework:**

Farrell and Newman challenge the liberal IR claim that globalization produces fragmented, decentralized power. Drawing on network science, they argue that many global economic networks exhibit "scale-free" properties — they grow asymmetrically such that certain nodes become far more connected than others. This hub-and-spoke topology concentrates power in states with jurisdiction over central nodes.

**Two Mechanisms of Weaponized Interdependence:**

1. **The Panopticon Effect:** States controlling network hubs can extract strategically valuable information by monitoring flows passing through their jurisdiction. The US Treasury's post-9/11 access to SWIFT data gave Washington real-time visibility into global financial transactions. The NSA's upstream collection through internet backbone chokepoints (revealed by Snowden, formalized under FISA Section 702) provided surveillance over global communications.

2. **The Chokepoint Effect:** States can deny adversaries access to network flows by leveraging control over central nodes. The US-led exclusion of Iranian banks from SWIFT in 2012 severed Iran's access to the global financial system. Oil exports declined from 2.5 million bpd to approximately 1 million bpd between 2011 and 2014 — described as "the financial market equivalent of crossing the nuclear threshold."

**Two Conditions for Weaponization:**
- (a) **Effective jurisdiction** over the central economic node (physical location or legal incorporation within state territory)
- (b) **Appropriate domestic institutions and norms** allowing the state to compel the hub entity to cooperate with intelligence or coercive operations

**Key Insight for IPT:** Globalization does not diffuse power; it concentrates it in new ways. This is the theoretical scaffolding for why AI supply chain chokepoints exist and persist. But Farrell & Newman analyze this power exclusively as an *offensive* instrument. The IPT must explain why the same structural feature can produce *defensive* outcomes.

### 1B. Underground Empire (2023)

**Citation:** Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman, *Underground Empire: How America Weaponized the World Economy* (New York: Henry Holt/Macmillan, 2023).
- **Tier:** 2
- **URL:** https://us.macmillan.com/books/9781250840554/undergroundempire/

**Empirical Cases Directly Relevant to Chapter 5:**

1. **SWIFT and Iran:** After 9/11, the Treasury Department discovered it could use SWIFT — a Belgian-based financial messaging system processing the vast majority of international transactions — as both panopticon and chokepoint. First for counter-terrorism surveillance, then as a sanctions weapon against Iran's nuclear program. The book documents how SWIFT, initially resistant to government access, acquiesced under post-9/11 pressure, creating the playbook subsequently applied to North Korea, Sudan, Belarus, Myanmar, and Russia.

2. **Semiconductor Export Controls:** The US lost its manufacturing edge to TSMC and Samsung but retained dominance in design and manufacturing tools — particularly EDA (electronic design automation) software. Synopsys, Cadence Design Systems, and Mentor Graphics (now Siemens EDA) control approximately 85% of the global EDA market. The US forced TSMC to deny advanced chips to Huawei, demonstrating cross-network weaponization: US centrality in the design network enabled chokepoint power in the manufacturing network.

3. **EDA Tools as Chokepoint:** When Huawei was placed on the Entity List in May 2019, the immediate implications were primarily software-related. EDA tools and fabrication capital equipment represented "areas in which China has very weak capabilities." The Foreign-Produced Direct Product Rule (May 2020) extended this chokepoint extraterritorially, restricting any chip made using US-origin technology from reaching Huawei.

**Critical Quote:** "Power without discipline is dangerous, and the United States has slipped into a pattern of economic coercion as a first resort, often without thinking through the long-term consequences."

**The "Accidental Empire" Narrative:** The infrastructure of globalization — SWIFT, dollar clearing, internet backbone, semiconductor design tools — was built by market actors pursuing efficiency and profit, not by states designing instruments of coercion. The chokepoints emerged from network effects and path dependence. This is important for the IPT: it means the AI supply chain's hub-and-spoke structure is a durable market feature, not something states can easily engineer away.

### 1C. "The Weaponized World Economy" (2025)

**Citation:** Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman, "The Weaponized World Economy: Surviving the New Age of Economic Coercion," *Foreign Affairs* 104, no. 5 (September/October 2025).
- **Tier:** 2
- **URL:** https://www.foreignaffairs.com/united-states/weaponized-world-economy-farrell-newman

**Key Evolution from the 2019 Paper:**

The central claim: "the age of weaponized interdependence [has arrived], in which the United States is discovering what it is like to have others do unto it as it has eagerly done unto others." After two decades of unilateral American weaponization of chokepoints, other powers — principally China — have developed counter-chokepoints of their own.

**Counter-Chokepoints:** China's rare-earth mineral dominance (controlling over 90% of processing) now functions as a reciprocal chokepoint. The Trump administration made concessions on semiconductor export controls in exchange for China easing rare-earth restrictions that were "crippling the United States' auto industry."

**The Arms Control Analogy:** Farrell & Newman's most important policy recommendation: "As during the nuclear era, the United States needs to turn away from unilateralism, toward detente and arms control, and, perhaps in the very long term, toward rebuilding an interdependent global economy on more robust foundations." This directly supports the IPT's policy direction — but Farrell & Newman frame chokepoints as inherently dangerous weapons requiring arms control, whereas the IPT argues chokepoints can be restructured as a defensive peace architecture.

### 1D. "The Enshittification of American Power" (2025)

**Citation:** Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman, "The Enshittification of American Power," *WIRED*, July 2025.
- **Tier:** 3 (magazine feature by leading scholars)
- **URL:** https://henryfarrell.net/the-enshittification-of-american-power/

**Core Argument:** Applies Cory Doctorow's 2022 "enshittification" concept — the cycle where platform owners first attract users with generous terms, then squeeze them once locked in by network effects and switching costs — to American hegemony itself.

**Key Claims:**
- Military hardware (F-35), the US dollar, and satellite constellations (Starlink) should be understood as *platforms*. When allies buy F-35s, they get not just a plane but the associated communications, parts supply, and technological support ecosystem. They become locked in.
- "Since Trump retook office in January [2025], rapid enshittification has become the organizing principle of US statecraft." The administration understands that controlling the infrastructure layer of global finance, technology, and security gives it "vast machineries of coercion."
- Examples of hegemonic enshittification: using control of global finance to sanction the International Criminal Court; threatening to deny Starlink access to Ukraine to force mineral concessions.
- Consequence: allies are actively discussing breaking away from US dependency. German politician Friedrich Merz declared independence from the US a priority; Macron emphasized need for independence; Canada's prime minister stated the old relationship with the US is "over."

**Relevance to Chapter 5:** This is the strongest empirical evidence for the Copeland dynamic applied to chokepoint power. When the hegemon abuses its platform position, it generates exactly the "negative expectations about future access" that Copeland identifies as war triggers. The enshittification argument provides the mechanism by which offensive chokepoint use becomes self-undermining — and thus why the IPT's distinction between offensive and defensive chokepoint architectures is not merely academic but urgent.

## SECTION 2: THE CRITICAL DISTINCTION — SELECTIVE DENIAL VS. TOTAL DESTRUCTION

This is the chapter's key theoretical contribution. It has no single existing source because the distinction itself is original to the IPT. What follows is the conceptual architecture assembled from multiple literatures.

### 2A. Farrell & Newman's Mode: Selective Denial

Every Farrell & Newman case study involves **selective denial** — cutting a specific actor off from the network while the network continues to function for everyone else:

| Case | Target | Network Continues For |
|------|--------|-----------------------|
| Iran/SWIFT (2012) | Iranian banks | All other global banks |
| Huawei Entity List (2019-20) | Huawei + affiliates | All other chip designers |
| Russia/SWIFT (2022) | Selected Russian banks | Global financial system |
| Semiconductor export controls | Chinese AI chip buyers | US allies, domestic firms |

**Structural logic of selective denial:** The network's value *increases* for the wielder because the target is excluded while the wielder and its allies retain full access. This is an **offensive weapon** — it works precisely because the network survives intact.

### 2B. The IPT's Mode: Total Destruction Deterrence

The IPT's deterrence logic depends on a categorically different scenario: **invasion of a chokepoint nation destroys the chokepoint itself**, denying the capability to *everyone*, including the attacker.

- If China invades Taiwan, TSMC fabs are destroyed (through combat, scorched-earth policy, or deliberate sabotage). The attacker does not gain the fabs — it destroys them.
- This is not selective denial (I cut you off but keep the network for myself). This is **mutual denial** (the capability ceases to exist for all parties).
- The closest analogy is nuclear deterrence via mutually assured destruction (MAD), not economic sanctions.

### 2C. Closest Literature to This Distinction

**Thomas J. Christensen, "Mutually Assured Disruption: Globalization, Security, and the Dangers of Decoupling," *World Politics* 75th Anniversary Special Issue (January 2024).**
- **Tier:** 1
- **URL:** https://muse.jhu.edu/pub/1/article/918726/pdf
- **Argument:** Transnational production in the Asia-Pacific has been a force for peace. Decoupling would not only harm the global economy but increase the likelihood of military conflict. Government interventions to build supply chain resilience "should be limited in scope so as to avoid fundamental damage to the complex economic interdependence that has fostered growth and helped to deter war."
- **Relevance:** Christensen's concept of "mutually assured disruption" is the economic analogue of MAD — deterrence through entanglement rather than punishment. But unlike nuclear MAD, disruption is reversible, repeatable, and ratchetable. The IPT can build on this: TSMC destruction falls *between* Christensen's "mutually assured disruption" (too mild — implies reversibility) and nuclear MAD (too extreme — implies civilizational destruction). It is a distinct category: **catastrophic but non-existential mutual harm** that destroys an irreplaceable capability for all parties.

**Deterrence Theory: Denial vs. Punishment**
- **Tier:** 1 (classical IR theory)
- The deterrence literature distinguishes between **deterrence by punishment** (threatening retaliation to change cost-benefit calculations) and **deterrence by denial** (making the objective physically impossible to achieve). See Glenn Snyder, *Deterrence and Defense* (1961); and the contemporary revival in Alex S. Wilner and Andreas Wenger, *Deterrence by Denial: Theory and Practice* (Cambria Press).
- The IPT's deterrence logic is a hybrid: it is deterrence by denial in the sense that invasion cannot achieve the objective (seizing functional fabs), but it also functions as deterrence by punishment in the sense that invasion destroys a capability the attacker itself depends on.
- **No existing source makes this hybrid argument in the semiconductor/AI context.** [VERIFY — search for any 2025-2026 publications making this specific point]

### 2D. Summary Table: Two Modes of Chokepoint Power

| Dimension | Selective Denial (Farrell & Newman) | Total Destruction (IPT) |
|-----------|-------------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Who is denied** | Specific target | Everyone, including attacker |
| **Network status** | Continues operating | Destroyed |
| **Wielder benefits** | Yes (retains access) | No (loses access too) |
| **Strategic logic** | Offensive coercion | Defensive deterrence |
| **Analogy** | Trade embargo | MAD (mutual assured destruction) |
| **Generates** | Instability (Copeland dynamic) | Restraint (collective security) |
| **Example** | Iran/SWIFT, Huawei controls | Taiwan invasion scenario |

### 3B. The Copeland Dynamic: Negative Expectations Generate Countermeasures

**Citation:** Dale C. Copeland, *Economic Interdependence and War* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2015).
- **Tier:** 1 (ISA Best Book Award 2017)
- **URL:** https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691161594/economic-interdependence-and-war

**Also:** Dale C. Copeland, "Economic Interdependence and War: A Theory of Trade Expectations," *International Security* 20, no. 4 (Spring 1996): 5-41.
- **Tier:** 1
- **URL:** https://muse.jhu.edu/article/447422/summary

**And:** Dale C. Copeland, "When Trade Leads to War: China, Russia, and the Limits of Interdependence," *Foreign Affairs*, June 2023.
- **Tier:** 2
- **URL:** https://www.foreignaffairs.com/china/when-trade-leads-war-china-russia

**Core Mechanism:** When leaders have **positive expectations** of future trade, they want to remain at peace to secure economic benefits. When expectations turn **negative** — leaders fear being cut off from vital resources, markets, or supply chains — the anticipated economic decline makes war appear as "the rational lesser of two evils."

**Key Passage (Copeland 1996):** "But if state A is dependent and has negative expectations for future trade with B, then the expected value of trade will be very low or negative. If the expected value for trade is lower than the expected value for invasion, war becomes the rational choice, and this is so even when the expected value of invasion is itself negative: war becomes the lesser of two evils."

**Application to Chapter 5:** US semiconductor export controls are generating exactly the Copeland dynamic. China perceives the controls as signals of permanent exclusion from the advanced chip ecosystem. This flips expectations negative, creating incentives for:
- Massive state investment in self-sufficiency (Big Fund I-III, totaling >$100 billion across three phases)
- Aggressive technology transfer and espionage
- Potential preventive military action against Taiwan before the window of dependency closes

**Copeland in Foreign Affairs (2023):** Applied his theory directly to U.S.-China relations, arguing that "optimistic economic expectations should outweigh pessimistic ones for years to come" but warning that technology decoupling could shift the calculus. Semiconductor export controls perceived as permanent exclusion are precisely the trigger he identifies.

### 3C. The Decoupling Dilemma: Sanctions Erode the Interdependence They Exploit

**Citation:** Dongan Tan, "The Decoupling Dilemma: How US Sanctions Erode Global Economic Governance," *International Organization* 79, Supplement Issue (December 2025): S134-S147.
- **Tier:** 1
- **URL:** https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/international-organization/article/decoupling-dilemma-how-us-sanctions-erode-global-economic-governance/8FA0868798CE6033CBAF995F0E763084

**Core Argument:** The United States' expansive use of financial sanctions has paradoxically accelerated the erosion of the liberal economic order. As sanctions proliferate, targets move from short-term evasion to building alternative infrastructures:
- China's RMB settlement system (CIPS) as alternative to SWIFT
- BRICS financial mechanisms
- Central bank digital currencies (CBDCs)
- Barter-based trade arrangements

**Key Finding:** Each new wave of sanctions intensifies incentives for targets to build alternative nodes and interconnections, producing "governance decoupling" that erodes US leverage and dollar centrality — even as the dollar remains dominant in the short term.

**Relevance to Chapter 5:** This is the self-undermining logic of offensive chokepoint use. Sanctions catalyze the very parallel economic ecosystems that fragment the financial architecture the sanctions depend on. Applied to semiconductors: export controls drive China's semiconductor self-sufficiency investments, which — if successful over 10-15 years — would erode the chokepoint power the controls exploit. The weapon destroys itself through use.

### 3D. Beaumier & Cartwright: Cross-Network Weaponization

**Citation:** Guillaume Beaumier and Madison Cartwright, "Cross-Network Weaponization in the Semiconductor Supply Chain," *International Studies Quarterly* 68, no. 1 (March 2024): sqae003.
- **Tier:** 1
- **URL:** https://academic.oup.com/isq/article/68/1/sqae003/7578750

**Core Contribution:** Extends Farrell & Newman by mapping the semiconductor supply chain as **four interrelated networks**: (1) design, (2) raw materials, (3) manufacturing equipment, and (4) assembled chips. A state's position across *multiple* networks determines its coercive capacity.

**Key Finding:** The United States' centrality in the design network (EDA tools, chip architecture) enables it to weaponize chokepoints in the trade network of assembled chips — even though the US has limited manufacturing capacity. This "cross-network" weaponization multiplies chokepoint power beyond what any single network position would predict.

**Relevance to Chapter 5:** This framework shows that chokepoint power is even more complex and multi-layered than Farrell & Newman's original formulation suggests. For the IPT, this cuts both ways: (a) it means the AI supply chain has multiple, overlapping chokepoints, making it harder for any single state to exit the interdependence web; but (b) it also means offensive weaponization has more dimensions than the IPT's simple "total destruction" scenario captures.

A Chinese blockade or invasion would halt production, causing trillions in economic losses worldwide.

### 4C. Critiques of the Silicon Shield

- **Citation:** "U.S.-Taiwan Relations in a New Era: Responding to a More Assertive China," Council on Foreign Relations Task Force Report, 2024.

### 4D. Horster: Silicon Shield or Silicon Trap?

**Citation:** Tim Horster, "Silicon Shield or Silicon Trap? Taiwan's Semiconductor Dominance and the Strategic Calculus of Chinese Military Aggression," SSRN Working Paper, May 2025.
- **Tier:** 5 (pre-print, not peer-reviewed)
- **URL:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5245142
- **Also available at:** https://www.researchgate.net/publication/391522122

**Core Argument:** Examines the dual narratives — the "Silicon Shield" (Taiwan's chip supremacy deters Chinese aggression) and the "Silicon Trap" (it may instead invite it). The paper argues that while interdependence has thus far preserved peace, three shifting conditions may transform semiconductor dominance from deterrent to catalyst:

1. **US diversification efforts** (TSMC Arizona, CHIPS Act) erode Taiwan's monopoly position, reducing the costs China would pay for attacking
2. **Chinese perceptions of a closing window** — between approximately 2027 and 2032, a dangerous gap exists where China may calculate that (a) the West's threat to destroy TSMC lacks credibility, and (b) China's own self-sufficiency remains insufficient to absorb the loss
3. **Accelerating Chinese investment** in domestic semiconductor capacity (Big Fund III, SMIC push)

**The "Closing Window" Concept:** China's best domestic foundry (SMIC) mass-produces chips around 14nm, with only small experimental batches at ~7nm — four to five years behind TSMC's cutting edge (3-5nm as of 2024-25). But this gap could narrow, creating a period of maximum danger when China's self-sufficiency is rising but not yet sufficient, and Taiwan's monopoly is eroding but not yet broken.

**Relevance to Chapter 5:** Horster's analysis directly supports the IPT's concern that the silicon shield is *politically incomplete* — it provides economic deterrence but not the institutional architecture of collective security. The "closing window" dynamic is a Copeland scenario: negative expectations about future access (driven by US diversification) combined with rising but still inadequate self-sufficiency create incentives for preventive action.

**The Escape Paradox (Chapter 5's Argument):**
Both superpowers are spending hundreds of billions to escape the mutual dependency that constrains them. But escape is extraordinarily slow:
- Leading-edge fabs take 3-5+ years to build in the US (vs. ~2 years in Taiwan)
- Intel Ohio first fab delayed until 2030
- SMIC remains 4-5 years behind TSMC
- ASML's EUV monopoly is decades in the making and has no alternative supplier
- Carl Zeiss SMT's mirror optics took 15 years to develop — sole supplier

The escape attempts *confirm the logic*: if the interdependence were not genuinely constraining, neither side would be spending this much to escape it. But the glacial pace of escape means the interdependence will persist for at least 5-10 more years, during which the policy choice between offensive weaponization and defensive architecture remains decisive.

## SECTION 5: THEORETICAL FRAMING — THE CHAPTER'S CORE CLAIM

### 5A. The Central Argument

Chokepoint power is real — Farrell & Newman have proven this beyond reasonable doubt. But **how chokepoints are used** determines whether they generate instability or peace:

| Use | Mechanism | Outcome | Example |
|-----|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Offensive weaponization** | Selective denial | Instability (Copeland dynamic) | Iran/SWIFT, Huawei controls |
| **Defensive architecture** | Total-destruction deterrence | Restraint (collective security) | Taiwan/TSMC (embryonic) |

**The policy choice between these two uses is the central question of AI geopolitics.**

Current US policy treats chokepoints as offensive weapons. This generates:
1. Negative expectations about future access (Copeland) → incentive for preventive countermeasures
2. Governance decoupling (Tan 2025) → erosion of the interdependence the sanctions exploit
3. Counter-chokepoint development (China rare earths) → reciprocal weaponization spiral
4. Enshittification of American hegemony (Farrell 2025) → allied distrust and diversification

The alternative — restructuring AI supply chain interdependence as a *defensive peace architecture* — would generate:
1. Positive expectations about future access → incentive for continued cooperation
2. Collective interest in protecting chokepoint nations → collective security
3. Mutual vulnerability to total destruction → restraint (analogous to nuclear deterrence but non-catastrophic)
4. Institutional framework for managing access disputes → stability

### 5B. Key Theoretical Sources for Framing

**Dale Copeland — Trade Expectations Theory:**
- When offensive chokepoint use generates negative expectations about future access, it creates the very war incentives that interdependence is supposed to prevent
- The IPT must ensure *positive* expectations are credible — which requires institutional commitment mechanisms, not unilateral guarantees

**Thomas Christensen — Mutually Assured Disruption:**
- *World Politics* 75th Anniversary Issue (January 2024)
- Tier 1
- URL: https://muse.jhu.edu/pub/1/article/918726/pdf
- Economic interdependence in the Asia-Pacific has been a force for peace. Decoupling would increase conflict risk. But government interventions for resilience should be "limited in scope."
- Provides the bridging concept between nuclear MAD and economic deterrence that the IPT needs.

**Farrell & Newman — Arms Control Analogy:**
- Their call for economic "arms control" is the nearest existing proposal to the IPT's defensive architecture. But the IPT goes further: not just restraining the use of economic weapons, but restructuring the supply chain so that the *defensive* properties of chokepoints are maximized and the *offensive* properties are constrained.

**Dongan Tan — The Decoupling Dilemma (2025):**
- Provides the causal mechanism by which offensive chokepoint use is self-undermining: sanctions catalyze the parallel ecosystems that erode the sanctioner's leverage. The weapon consumes itself through use.

### 5C. Outstanding Questions and Gaps

1. **Credibility of total-destruction deterrence:** The Defense Priorities and CFR critiques argue that China might attack Taiwan regardless of economic costs if political stakes (independence declaration, domestic legitimacy crisis) are high enough. The IPT must address the conditions under which the "total destruction" deterrent fails. What is the threshold? Is there a credible commitment mechanism that makes destruction automatic (like a dead man's switch)?

2. **The transition problem:** Even if the IPT's defensive architecture is superior in theory, getting from the current offensive-weaponization equilibrium to a collective-security equilibrium requires coordinated policy change during a period of maximum tension. How do you restructure the system without triggering the Copeland dynamic during the transition?

3. **The escape timeline:** If both superpowers achieve enough semiconductor self-sufficiency within 10-15 years to reduce mutual dependency below the deterrence threshold, the IPT's peace mechanism has an expiration date. The chapter must address whether AI supply chain chokepoints are *permanently* irreplaceable or merely *temporarily* irreplaceable.

4. **The selectivity problem:** The IPT assumes that chokepoint destruction in war is total and indiscriminate. But what if precision strikes could capture fabs intact, or if key personnel could be extracted? The logic depends on destruction being inevitable in the event of invasion — this assumption needs interrogation.

5. **Missing literature:** No existing source makes the explicit distinction between selective denial and total destruction as two modes of chokepoint power. The chapter is making an original theoretical contribution and should signal this clearly rather than attributing it to the existing literature.
