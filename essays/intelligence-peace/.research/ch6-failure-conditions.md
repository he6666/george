## 1. FAILURE CONDITION: AI NOT YET EXISTENTIAL (Condition 1 Weakens)

### 1.1 The Measured GDP Gap

The single most uncomfortable fact for IPT Condition 1: AI's measured economic contribution remains negligible relative to projections.

**Current reality (as of early 2026):**

- Goldman Sachs chief economist Jan Hatzius: AI's impact on the US economy was "basically zero" in 2025. "There's been a lot of misreporting of the impact that AI investment had on GDP growth." [TIER 2]
  - Source: [Tom's Hardware](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/artificial-intelligence/ai-boosted-us-economy-by-basically-zero-in-2025-says-goldman-sachs-chief-economist-we-think-theres-been-a-lot-of-misreporting-of-the-impact-that-ai-investment-had-on-gdp-growth)

- Goldman Sachs (March 2026): "No meaningful relationship between productivity and AI adoption at the economy-wide level." Only 10% of S&P 500 management teams quantified AI's impact. Median reported gain where quantified: ~30%, but confined to narrow task-specific use cases. [TIER 2]
  - Source: [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/03/03/goldman-earnings-ai-anxiety-no-meaningful-impact-productivity-economy-30-percent-in-2-areas/)

- St. Louis Fed (January 2026): AI-related investment categories contributed 40-50 basis points to real GDP growth in Q1-Q3 2025 when adjusted for imports (roughly 20-25% of total GDP growth). This exceeds dot-com-era IT investment contributions. However, this is *investment-driven* growth, not productivity-driven growth. [TIER 1]
  - Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://www.stlouisfed.org/on-the-economy/2026/jan/tracking-ai-contribution-gdp-growth)

- Goldman Sachs projects AI spending will contribute roughly 1.5 percentage points to measured capex growth in 2026, but net GDP impact of only 0.1-0.2 percentage points due to heavy reliance on imported capital goods. [TIER 2]
  - Source: [Fortune](https://fortune.com/2026/03/03/goldman-earnings-ai-anxiety-no-meaningful-impact-productivity-economy-30-percent-in-2-areas/)

### 1.2 Trajectory Evidence (The Bull Case)

The gap between promise and measured impact does not necessarily invalidate the trajectory. Key indicators:

**Investment momentum:**
- Big Tech AI capex in 2025: ~$405 billion (up 62% YoY). Amazon $100B, Microsoft $80B, Google $75B, Meta $65B. [TIER 2]

**Adoption acceleration:**
- 71% of firms use generative AI in at least one function (up from 55% in 2024). [TIER 2]

  - Sources: [McKinsey State of AI](https://www.punku.ai/blog/state-of-ai-2024-enterprise-adoption); [Anthropic Economic Index](https://www.anthropic.com/research/anthropic-economic-index-september-2025-report)

### 1.3 Analytical Assessment for Chapter 6

**The honest framing:** As of early 2026, AI is economically significant as an *investment category* but not yet as a *productivity driver* at macroeconomic scale. Condition 1 of the IPT is not yet fully met. The thesis therefore currently operates in what might be called "anticipatory mode" -- the deterrent power rests on trajectory and expectation, not yet on measured impact.

**The hedge:** Even narrow AI creates *substrate dependency*. The semiconductor supply chain is already existentially important because modern economies cannot function without advanced chips -- for military systems, communications, financial infrastructure, energy grids. AI accelerates this dependency dramatically but is not strictly required for the supply chain to have strategic weight. The question is one of degree: without AGI-level impact, the costs of disruption are severe but perhaps not catastrophic enough to deter a sufficiently motivated actor.

## 2. FAILURE CONDITION: AUTARKY ACHIEVED (Condition 2 Collapses)

### 2.1 China's Self-Sufficiency Drive: Current State

**Big Fund III:**
- Established May 24, 2024. Registered capital: 344 billion yuan ($47.5 billion) -- the largest tranche since the initiative began in 2014. Roughly on par with the US CHIPS Act ($53 billion). 19 equity investors led by China's Ministry of Finance (17% stake). Operations began December 31, 2024. [TIER 1]
  - Source: [South China Morning Post](https://www.scmp.com/tech/tech-war/article/3264612/tech-war-chinas-big-fund-iii-brings-us475-billion-fresh-outlay-nations-semiconductor-supply-chain)

**SMIC Process Technology:**
- 7nm (N+2): Achieved 2023. Yields improved to 60-70% (up from <40% at initial launch in H2 2023). [TIER 2]
- 5nm (N+3): Development reportedly completed by end of 2025. Uses only DUV equipment (no EUV). Conflicting yield reports: one estimate at ~33% (one-third of TSMC's yield); another tipster claims 60-70%. Production cost estimated at 50% higher than TSMC's EUV-based 5nm. DUV requires 34 patterning steps vs. 9 for EUV. [TIER 3 -- yield figures conflict]
  - Sources: [TrendForce](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2025/03/28/news-smic-reported-to-complete-5nm-chips-by-2025-but-costs-may-be-50-higher-than-tsmcs/); [WCCFTech](https://wccftech.com/smic-5nm-yields-between-60-and-70-percent-claims-tipster/)

**Equipment Self-Sufficiency:**
- Rose to ~35% by end of 2025, up from 25% in 2024 (and ~13.6% baseline as of 2024 by some measures). Exceeded Beijing's target of 30%. [TIER 2]
- Etching and thin-film deposition: local adoption surpassed 40% (Naura Technology, AMEC). [TIER 2]
- Remaining weaknesses: metrology, coating/developing, lithography, and ion implantation equipment. [TIER 2]
  - Sources: [South China Morning Post](https://www.scmp.com/tech/big-tech/article/3339366/great-chip-leap-chinas-semiconductor-equipment-self-reliance-surges-past-targets); [TrendForce](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2025/02/14/news-chinas-semiconductor-equipment-industry-booming-self-sufficiency-to-reach-50-by-2025/)

**Domestic EUV Development:**
- A Chinese consortium (involving Huawei and Shenzhen-based SiCarrier, with 3,000+ researchers) assembled a prototype EUV machine in early 2025 using components partly sourced from older ASML systems and Laser-Induced Discharge Plasma (LDP) technology. [TIER 2]
- The prototype can generate EUV light but has not produced working chips. [TIER 2]
- Government target: functional chips by 2028. Industry consensus: 2030 is more realistic. [TIER 2]
- SiCarrier raised $2.8 billion in funding. [TIER 3]
- ASML CEO Christophe Fouquet: China would need "many, many years" to develop such technology. [TIER 2]
  - Sources: [TrendForce](https://www.trendforce.com/news/2025/12/18/news-china-reportedly-builds-euv-prototype-using-older-asml-components-eyes-2028-chipmaking/); [Asia Times](https://asiatimes.com/2025/12/made-in-china-euv-machine-targets-ai-chip-output-by-2028/)

- CSIS assessment ("Breakthroughs or Boasts?"): Chinese announcements "project ambition but do not fundamentally alter the strategic reality." State-directed capital produces "fragmentation, duplication, and a 'rotten tail' problem." [TIER 1]
  - Source: [CSIS Strategic Technologies Blog](https://www.csis.org/blogs/strategic-technologies-blog/breakthroughs-or-boasts-assessing-recent-chinese-lithography)

### 2.2 Honest Assessment: Distance from True Leading-Edge Autarky

China's progress is real but must be evaluated against the correct benchmark -- not whether China can make *some* advanced chips, but whether it can achieve *self-sustaining, competitive-yield, leading-edge production at scale without any foreign inputs*.

**Key gaps:**
1. **Yield penalty:** Even if SMIC achieves 5nm, at 33-70% yields vs. TSMC's >90%, the cost-per-working-chip renders large-scale AI training economically prohibitive relative to competitors.
2. **EUV gap:** Prototype to production took ASML 18 years (2001-2019). China wants 3-5 years. Even with state mobilization, the physics and engineering cannot be easily compressed. [TIER 2]
3. **Materials and subsystems:** Photoresist localization at 20% (2024), targeting 50% by 2027-2030. EUV-grade mirrors produced only by Carl Zeiss SMT -- no known Chinese equivalent. [TIER 2]
4. **Equipment breadth:** 35% overall self-sufficiency means 65% still depends on foreign equipment. Critical categories (metrology, lithography) remain below 20%. [TIER 2]

**Assessment:** China is at least 5-10 years from anything approaching leading-edge autarky, and even that timeline assumes no further tightening of export controls and continued successful execution. True parity in EUV lithography -- matching ASML's reliability, yield, and throughput -- is likely 10-15 years away at best. Full ecosystem autarky (materials, chemicals, metrology, design tools) may never be fully achieved.

### 2.3 Why Autarky Is the Enemy of Peace

This is the critical policy implication for IPT: *autarky should be actively prevented, not passively observed*. If China achieves true self-sufficiency in leading-edge semiconductors, the interdependence that restrains conflict evaporates. But prevention must be calibrated -- see Section 5 (the Copeland Problem) on how excessive restriction can be counterproductive.

## 3. FAILURE CONDITION: REPLICABILITY (Chokepoints Are Not Durable)

If destroyed chokepoints can be quickly rebuilt, the cost of disruption drops and IPT's deterrent weakens.

### 3.1 Fab Reconstruction Timelines

- **New fab construction:** 3-4 years and >$10 billion under normal conditions. In Taiwan: ~19 months. In the US: ~38 months. Full volume production: add 1.5-2 years beyond construction. [TIER 2]
  - Source: [Intel/SEMI estimates](https://download.intel.com/newsroom/2022/manufacturing/fab-final-static.pdf); [UltraFacility](https://www.ultrafacilityportal.io/insights/semiconductor-in-numbers:-global-fab-construction-timelines,-from-breakthroughs-to-breakdowns)
- **From-scratch in a post-conflict scenario:** Estimates range 5-7 years minimum with full international cooperation, longer without existing supply chain access. [VERIFY -- composite estimate]
- **Real-world evidence:** Micron's $100B New York megafab -- first production initially scheduled for 2028, now expected 2030, with full buildout extending through 2041. [TIER 2]
  - Source: [Construction Today](https://construction-today.com/news/micron-delays-construction-timeline-for-100b-new-york-chip-megafab/)

### 3.2 EUV Replication Timeline

- ASML spent 30+ years and over euro 6 billion (industry-wide: $14-21 billion) developing EUV. [TIER 2]
- Timeline from prototype to production: 18 years (2001-2019). [TIER 1]
- ASML holds 16,000+ patents. The real moat: millions of lines of proprietary code and decades of calibration data. [TIER 2]
- EUV-grade mirrors: single supplier (Carl Zeiss SMT), product of decades of tightly integrated co-development with ASML. [TIER 2]
  - Sources: [Strange VC Review](https://thereview.strangevc.com/p/asmls-30-year-monopoly-the-moonshot); [Michigan Journal of Economics](https://sites.lsa.umich.edu/mje/2023/04/05/asml-the-little-known-source-of-the-worlds-technological-progress/)

### 3.3 TSMC's Process Knowledge: The Irreplicability Argument

- TSMC's Arizona fab evidence: identical equipment and processes, but finished wafers cost 30-50% more than in Taiwan. Yields slow to increase. TSMC temporarily relocated hundreds of experienced workers from Taiwan -- conceding that the missing ingredient was tacit knowledge, not machines. [TIER 2]
  - Source: [Medium/Marc Bara](https://medium.com/@marc.bara.iniesta/the-advanced-semiconductor-supply-chain-why-money-is-not-enough-e39325015d01)
- Tacit knowledge: "intuitive, unarticulated, and based on personal experience, tradition, and practice." Institutional knowledge in yield optimization and equipment troubleshooting is not documented in any manual. [TIER 2]
- Key formulation: "Money can build cleanrooms, but it cannot create experienced semiconductor engineers overnight." [TIER 2]
- Samo Burja, Bismarck Analysis: ASML's capability is embedded in a "tradition of knowledge" -- a living chain of practitioners whose expertise cannot be separated from the institution. [TIER 3]
  - Source: [Bismarck Analysis](https://brief.bismarckanalysis.com/p/the-tradition-of-knowledge-behind)

### 3.4 Assessment for Chapter 6

Replicability is not a serious near-term threat to IPT. The relevant chokepoints -- EUV lithography, leading-edge process knowledge, critical materials -- exist on timescales of decades, not years. An adversary contemplating disruption must account for 5-10+ years of severely degraded capability with no guarantee of recovery. This makes the cost calculus extremely unfavorable.

## 4. FAILURE CONDITION: IRRATIONALITY

### 4.1 The Critique

**Defense Priorities (explicit Silicon Shield critique):**
- "The 'silicon shield' is a weak deterrent against a cross-strait conflict, as Beijing may accept enormous economic costs if its objective of national reunification is otherwise at risk."
- "The vast asymmetry in the balance of resolve between the United States and China" undermines semiconductor-based deterrence.
- Key argument: China cannot operate Taiwan's chip industry post-invasion (cannot substitute vital upstream US/allied inputs), making capture a "hollow prize" -- but this does not mean Beijing won't try.
  - Source: [Defense Priorities](https://www.defensepriorities.org/explainers/semiconductors-are-not-a-reason-to-defend-taiwan/) [TIER 2]

### 4.2 The Response: Structural Advantages over Nuclear Deterrence

No deterrence system claims absolute effectiveness against irrational actors. The relevant comparison is nuclear deterrence, which suffers from the same vulnerability:

- **Nuclear deterrence assumes rationality.** Chatham House (2020): "Decision-makers in a nuclear crisis may well act less predictably than the assumption they are rational suggests." Emotions "create an overriding bias against objective facts." Deterrence theory has been "criticized by numerous scholars" for assuming rational actors. [TIER 1]
  - Source: [Chatham House](https://www.chathamhouse.org/2020/04/perspectives-nuclear-deterrence-21st-century-0/human-rationality-and-nuclear-deterrence)

**IPT's structural advantage -- automaticity:**

The critical distinction: nuclear deterrence is *discretionary* (someone must choose to retaliate, creating second-strike credibility problems). Semiconductor interdependence deterrence is *automatic*:
- Physical invasion destroys fabs (vibration, contamination, power disruption render cleanrooms inoperable).
- No one needs to decide to impose costs -- they are inherent in the act of aggression.
- The defender cannot "chicken out" of retaliation because the retaliation is built into the physics.

**The bar for "irrational enough":**
- The adversary must be willing to voluntarily accept the destruction of its access to advanced semiconductors -- equivalent to voluntarily regressing to a pre-2010s technological capability for a minimum of 5-10 years (and likely longer).
- In a world where AI is existentially important (Condition 1 fully met), this is equivalent to voluntarily returning to a pre-industrial competitive position relative to rivals who retain access.
- This is a dramatically higher bar than "willing to accept some economic costs." It requires acceptance of comprehensive technological regression with no guaranteed timeline for recovery.

## 5. THE COPELAND PROBLEM: The Most Dangerous Failure Mode

### 5.1 Copeland's Theory of Trade Expectations

**Core works:**

1. Dale C. Copeland, "Economic Interdependence and War: A Theory of Trade Expectations," *International Security* 20, no. 4 (Spring 1996): 5-41. [TIER 1]
   - The foundational article. Introduces the variable of *expectations about future trade* as the critical determinant.

2. Dale C. Copeland, "Economic Interdependence and the Future of U.S.-Chinese Relations," in *International Relations Theory and Regional Transformation*, ed. T.V. Paul (Cambridge University Press, 2012). [TIER 1 -- exact year/volume VERIFY]
   - Application to US-China: Chinese leaders' optimistic expectations hinge on confidence that oil and trade flows will remain open -- "a mindset that might change in the coming decades if the United States moves toward a policy of containing and economically coercing China."
   - Source: [Columbia CIAO](https://ciaotest.cc.columbia.edu/book/ike01/ike01_10ch10.pdf)

3. Dale C. Copeland, *A World Safe for Commerce: American Foreign Policy from the Revolution to the Rise of China* (Princeton University Press, 2024). [TIER 1]
   - Full-length treatment: "When the great power has reasons to believe its ability to economically access a vital realm will decline over time, it will shift to expansionist and conflictual policies in order to forcibly rectify a deteriorating situation."
   - Source: [Princeton University Press](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691172552/a-world-safe-for-commerce)

**The mechanism:**

| Expectations | Dependency | Predicted Outcome |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Positive | High | Peace (liberal prediction holds) |
| Positive | Low | Peace (but less robust) |
| Negative | Low | Rivalry, but low war risk |
| **Negative** | **High** | **Preventive war incentive (maximum danger)** |

The dangerous quadrant: high dependency + negative expectations about future access = incentive for preventive war *before* the window closes.

### 5.2 Application to Current US-China Semiconductor Dynamics

**Current US export controls are generating exactly the Copeland dynamic:**
- US export controls (October 2022, October 2023, December 2024) progressively restrict China's access to advanced chips, EUV equipment, and semiconductor manufacturing tools.
- China's response: Big Fund III ($47.5B), crash EUV program, equipment localization push.
- The message received in Beijing: *future access to the AI substrate will be denied*.

**Horster (2025) -- "Silicon Shield or Silicon Trap?":**
- Key argument: "While interdependence has thus far preserved peace, shifting geopolitical conditions, U.S. diversification efforts, and Chinese perceptions of a *closing window* may transform this asset into a catalyst for conflict." [TIER 3]
- US diversification (TSMC Arizona, Samsung Texas, Intel Ohio) reduces US dependency on Taiwan, potentially signaling to Beijing that the US is preparing to absorb the loss of Taiwan's fabs.
- Chinese perception: if the window for accessing advanced semiconductor technology is closing, the incentive for preemptive action rises.
  - Source: [SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5245142)

**The Decoupling Dilemma -- Tan (2025):**
- Dongan Tan, "The Decoupling Dilemma: How US Sanctions Erode Global Economic Governance," *International Organization* 79, Supplement 1 (December 2025). [TIER 1]
- Core finding: sanctions incentivize targets to build "durable parallel infrastructures and alternative legal standards that outlive any individual sanctions episode." Each round of coercion "durably reshapes the global governance architecture."
- The paradox for IPT: *the very sanctions designed to maintain technological advantage erode the interdependence on which deterrence depends*.
  - Source: [Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/international-organization/article/decoupling-dilemma-how-us-sanctions-erode-global-economic-governance/8FA0868798CE6033CBAF995F0E763084)

### 5.3 The Transition Period: Maximum Danger Zone

The Copeland Problem identifies the *transition period* -- between the current state of integrated supply chains and any future steady state -- as the period of maximum danger.

**Why:**
1. China is currently highly dependent on foreign semiconductor technology (Condition 2 is met).
2. US export controls signal that future access will be restricted (negative expectations).
3. China's autarky drive has not yet succeeded (the window has not fully closed).
4. During this window, China has maximum incentive for preventive action -- it is dependent enough that losing access is catastrophic, but not yet self-sufficient enough to absorb the loss.

**The policy imperative:** Export controls must be calibrated to maintain chokepoint leverage *without* pushing expectations so negative that they trigger the Copeland mechanism. This is an extraordinarily narrow needle to thread. Too permissive: China achieves autarky (Condition 2 fails). Too restrictive: China concludes access will be permanently denied and acts preemptively.

### 5.4 Recommended Framing for Chapter 6

The Copeland Problem should be presented as the *most intellectually serious* objection to IPT and the one the author takes most seriously. It is not a reason to abandon the thesis but rather a reason to be precise about the policy regime required to sustain it:

- Interdependence must be *maintained*, not weaponized to destruction.
- Export controls should preserve chokepoints, not signal permanent exclusion.
- The goal is to keep China dependent enough that restraint is rational, while offering enough access that expectations remain at least conditionally positive.
- This is the central policy challenge of the IPT framework.

| Failure Mode | Current Status | Severity | Timeframe |

## 8. NOTES FOR DRAFTING

**Tone guidance:** This chapter is where the author earns intellectual credibility. The failure conditions should be presented with full honesty -- no hand-waving, no motivated reasoning. The strongest version of each objection should be stated before the response.

**Recommended structure:**
1. Open with the honest admission: IPT is a conditional thesis, and conditions can fail.
2. Map each failure mode with current evidence.
3. Give the Copeland Problem disproportionate weight -- it is the most urgent and the most intellectually challenging.
4. Close with the argument that identifying failure conditions is a feature, not a bug: a theory that specifies when it breaks is stronger than one that claims universality.

**Key rhetorical move:** The nuclear deterrence comparison. Every objection to IPT (irrationality, imperfect deterrence, motivated actors) applies equally or more strongly to nuclear deterrence -- and yet nuclear deterrence has broadly held for 80 years. IPT has structural advantages (automaticity, non-discretionary costs) that nuclear deterrence lacks.
