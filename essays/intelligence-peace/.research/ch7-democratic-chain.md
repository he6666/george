## I. The Democratic Peace Tradition -- Applied to Supply Chains

### A. Foundational Literature

**Michael Doyle, "Kant, Liberal Legacies, and Foreign Affairs," Philosophy & Public Affairs 12, no. 3-4 (1983).**
[TIER 1]

Doyle revived Kant's 1795 theory of perpetual peace and demonstrated empirically that "constitutionally secure liberal states have yet to engage in war with one another." He identified three Kantian conditions: (1) republican constitutions, (2) a gradually expanding "pacific union" among liberal states, and (3) cosmopolitan law governing treatment of foreigners. Doyle's operational definition of liberal states requires market economies, internal sovereignty, juridical citizen rights, and representative government. This is the modern origin point of the democratic peace research program.

- Doyle, Michael W. "Kant, Liberal Legacies, and Foreign Affairs, Part I." *Philosophy & Public Affairs* 12, no. 3 (1983): 205-235. [Part II in vol. 12, no. 4.]

**Bruce Russett, *Grasping the Democratic Peace: Principles for a Post-Cold War World* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1993).**
[TIER 1]

Russett provided the most rigorous quantitative test of the democratic peace proposition. His central finding: "In the modern international system, democracies are less likely to use lethal violence toward other democracies than toward autocratically governed states or than autocratically governed states are toward each other." Russett tested two competing causal models:

1. **Cultural/Normative Model:** Democratic culture, perceptions, and practices of compromise are externalized into foreign policy, permitting peaceful conflict resolution between democracies.
2. **Structural/Institutional Model:** Institutional constraints (elections, legislative checks, free press) slow democratic leaders' path to war, meaning democracies do not fear surprise attacks from each other.

Russett found greater empirical support for the normative model. He systematically rejected counterexamples (Boer War, Spanish-American War, U.S. covert actions against Guatemala and Chile) and discounted alternative explanations based on international institutions or geographic distance.

- Russett, Bruce. *Grasping the Democratic Peace: Principles for a Post-Cold War World.* Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1993.

### B. Robustness and Critiques

The democratic peace is widely regarded as one of the strongest empirical regularities in international relations, but serious critiques exist:

**Small-N Problem.** John Mearsheimer and other realists note that "democracies have been few in number over the past two centuries, and thus there have been few opportunities where democracies were in a position to fight one another." The statistical significance of the finding depends on how wars and democracies are coded. [TIER 1]

- Mearsheimer, John J. *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics.* New York: W.W. Norton, 2001. [VERIFY specific page]

**Coding Disputes.** Critics argue that democratic peace researchers sometimes reclassify conflicts as non-wars or political systems as non-democracies without correcting the full dataset -- a form of selection bias. [TIER 2]

**Microfoundational Weakness.** Experimental research (including studies testing U.S., Russian, and Chinese subjects) found that neither regime type nor liberal norms reliably influenced individual willingness to attack opponents, casting doubt on the micro-level causal mechanism. [TIER 2]

- Schwartz, Jonathan, and Christopher W. Blair. "The Microfoundations of Normative Democratic Peace Theory." *Journal of Global Security Studies* 5, no. 3 (2020). [VERIFY exact citation]

**Incomplete Democratization.** Mansfield and Snyder argue that states in early phases of democratic transition are actually *more* war-prone than stable autocracies, especially when institutions are too weak to channel newly mobilized publics. This complicates any simple "more democracy = more peace" equation. [TIER 1]

- Mansfield, Edward D., and Jack Snyder. *Electing to Fight: Why Emerging Democracies Go to War.* Cambridge, MA: MIT Press, 2005.

**The Capitalist Peace Alternative.** Erik Gartzke argues that "the 'democratic peace' is a mirage created by the overlap between economic and political freedom." His calculation: the correlation between economic liberty and peace is 50 times greater than that between democracy and peace. This challenges whether regime type or market openness is the true causal variable. [TIER 1]

- Gartzke, Erik. "The Capitalist Peace." *American Journal of Political Science* 51, no. 1 (2007): 166-191.

**Chapter application:** The Gartzke critique actually *strengthens* the supply-chain argument. If capitalist openness -- not democracy per se -- drives peace, then the key variable is institutional commitment to open markets and rule of law. Democracies provide these commitments more credibly than autocracies because of independent judiciaries, transparent regulation, and electoral accountability. The democratic peace and the capitalist peace converge on supply chains: deep economic cooperation requires the credible commitments that democratic institutions uniquely provide.

### C. Democratic Institutions and Innovation Ecosystems

**Acemoglu and Robinson, *Why Nations Fail* (2012).**
[TIER 1]

The core argument: "inclusive" political and economic institutions -- those that enforce property rights, create a level playing field, and encourage investment in new technologies -- are the engine of sustained growth. "Extractive" institutions concentrate resources in the hands of the few, stifling the creative destruction that innovation requires. "Sustained economic growth requires innovation, and innovation cannot be decoupled from creative destruction, which replaces the old with the new in the economic realm and also destabilizes established power relations in politics." Authoritarian regimes resist creative destruction precisely because it threatens incumbent power.

- Acemoglu, Daron, and James A. Robinson. *Why Nations Fail: The Origins of Power, Prosperity, and Poverty.* New York: Crown Business, 2012.

**CSIS, "Democracies' Advantage: Leveraging Innovation Coalitions" (2023).**
[TIER 2]

Policy analysis arguing that "the critical intellectual posture inherent to creatively disruptive innovation is incompatible with authoritarianism." The innovation coalition retains technological leadership "as long as its members maintain commitment to free inquiry and market-driven economies," which are the "crown jewel assets" of the U.S.-aligned coalition. The Cold War demonstrated that ideological conformity undermines the open inquiry that frontier innovation demands.

- CSIS Perspectives on Innovation blog. "Democracies' Advantage: Leveraging Innovation Coalitions to Meet the Autocratic Challenge." 2023. [VERIFY exact date]

**Atlantic Council, "Toward a Democratic Technology Alliance" (2021).**
[TIER 2]

Proposes that democratic institutions should strengthen innovation ecosystems "through joint research and development, increased data sharing, and forging a common approach to technology regulation." A Democratic Technology Alliance would ensure "the free world prevails in the race for advanced technologies by jointly investing in innovation, countering unfair practices, and developing rules and norms consistent with democratic values."

- Atlantic Council. "Toward a Democratic Technology Alliance: An Innovation Edge That Favors Freedom." 2021. [VERIFY exact date]

**Empirical evidence on democracy and innovation.** Cross-national studies find that democratic regimes show significantly better innovation performance than autocratic ones, with democratic openness to new ideas, political stability, and protection of intellectual freedom as key mechanisms. However, a study of 61 developing countries (2013-2020) found no statistically significant relationship, suggesting the effect may be conditional on development level. [TIER 2]

- Gao, Yongqiang, et al. "The Impacts of Democracy on Innovation: Revisited Evidence." *Technological Forecasting and Social Change* 108 (2021). [VERIFY exact citation]

## II. The Democratic Chokepoint Map

### A. Current Distribution -- All Major Chokepoints Are Democracies

The semiconductor supply chain features an extraordinary concentration of chokepoint control in democratic nations. This section maps each chokepoint role and its regime type.

**Netherlands -- ASML (Lithography Equipment)**
[TIER 1]

ASML holds 100% market share in extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography and approximately 90% of the overall lithography equipment market. No advanced chip below 7nm can be manufactured without ASML's EUV machines. ASML spent over three decades and more than EUR 6 billion in R&D to develop EUV technology -- "the longest and most expensive gamble in semiconductor history." Even with unlimited funding and access to ASML's engineers, a competitor would face "30 years of iterative improvements, dead-ends discovered and avoided, supplier relationships forged through shared problem-solving." The Netherlands is a constitutional monarchy with parliamentary democracy (Freedom House: Free).

- Miller, Chris. *Chip War: The Fight for the World's Most Critical Technology.* New York: Scribner, 2022.
- ASML corporate filings and market data, 2024-2025.

**Germany -- Zeiss SMT and Trumpf (Optics and Laser Systems)**
[TIER 1]

Zeiss SMT provides the lithography optics that are key elements of ASML's wafer scanners; 80% of all microchips worldwide are produced with Zeiss-enabled lithography optics. Trumpf manufactures "the world's most powerful pulsed industrial laser" required for EUV light generation -- there is "no economical alternative" for generating EUV light. Both companies are headquartered in Germany. Germany is a federal parliamentary republic (Freedom House: Free).

- Zeiss SMT corporate publications; Trumpf corporate disclosures.
- "ZEISS, TRUMPF, and Fraunhofer IOF Awarded German Future Prize 2020."

**Taiwan -- TSMC (Advanced Foundry Manufacturing)**
[TIER 1]

TSMC commands approximately 67-72% of global semiconductor foundry revenue (2025) and over 90% of the world's most advanced chip manufacturing (sub-5nm). Taiwan accounts for 92% of manufacturing capacity for leading-edge chips. TSMC's CoWoS advanced packaging capacity is projected to reach 65,000-75,000 wafers/month by end of 2025. Taiwan is a multi-party democracy (Freedom House: Free).

- TrendForce and Counterpoint Research, foundry market share reports, Q2-Q3 2025.
- U.S. International Trade Administration, "Taiwan -- Semiconductors Including Chip Design for AI," 2025.

**United States -- Design, EDA Tools, and Equipment**
[TIER 1]

The U.S. dominates chip design (Nvidia, AMD, Apple, Qualcomm, Broadcom) and electronic design automation (EDA) tools. Synopsys, Cadence, and Siemens (German-American) hold approximately 31%, 30%, and 13% of the EDA market respectively -- totaling ~74% under democratic-nation control. EDA tools are an "indispensable" chokepoint: no advanced chip can be designed without them, and market-entry barriers are extremely high. Applied Materials, Lam Research, and KLA Corporation provide critical semiconductor manufacturing equipment. The United States is a federal presidential republic (Freedom House: Free).

- Kleinhans, Jan-Peter. "The EDA Chokepoint Dilemma? Openness, Oligopolies, and China's Ecosystem." UC IGCC Working Paper, December 2022.
- Synopsys, Cadence, corporate filings 2024-2025.

**South Korea -- Samsung and SK Hynix (Memory, Advanced Packaging)**
[TIER 1]

SK Hynix dominates high-bandwidth memory (HBM) critical for AI training chips, with its proprietary MR-MUF packaging process giving it a decisive lead. Samsung is a major player in both memory and foundry services. Together, South Korean firms control a majority of global DRAM and NAND flash production. South Korea is a presidential republic (Freedom House: Free).

- SK Hynix and Samsung corporate filings; TrendForce memory market reports, 2025.

**Japan -- Materials and Equipment**
[TIER 2]

Japan dominates semiconductor materials (photoresists, silicon wafers, specialty chemicals) and key equipment segments. Tokyo Electron is the world's third-largest semiconductor equipment maker. Japanese firms control an estimated 50%+ of critical semiconductor materials. Japan is a constitutional monarchy with parliamentary democracy (Freedom House: Free). - Miller, *Chip War*, 2022; industry estimates.

### B. Is This Distribution Accidental?

**Core argument for the chapter:** It is not accidental that every major chokepoint in the semiconductor supply chain is held by a democracy. The nature of frontier semiconductor technology -- requiring decades of iterative R&D, deep cross-border collaboration, open scientific exchange, massive private investment protected by rule of law, and tolerance for failure -- selects for the institutional characteristics that democracies provide.

**Supporting evidence from authoritarian failure:**

**Soviet Semiconductor Collapse.**
[TIER 1]

The Soviet Union recognized semiconductors' strategic importance in the late 1950s and built an entire city (Zelenograd, 1958) around semiconductor production. But central planning proved fatally incompatible with semiconductor innovation. Soviet scientists were "increasingly ordered to copy Western designs without any changes" -- a strategy "poorly suited to the fast-evolving world of chip manufacturing." The decision to clone IBM 360 architecture led to "uncontrollable and irreversible addiction to imported technology," and "Soviet computer innovation stopped self-generating." By 1959, the CIA assessed Soviet semiconductors were "often crude copies of Western designs" and 5-7 years behind. The planned economy could not accommodate the trial-and-error, "fail faster, fail often, fail better" logic essential to semiconductor innovation.

- Yale Review of International Studies. "Failures, Successes, and Challenges in Advanced Semiconductor Manufacturing: Lessons from the Soviet Union, China, and Taiwan."
- Miller, *Chip War*, 2022, chapters on Soviet semiconductor programs. [VERIFY specific chapters]

**China's Ongoing Struggle.**
[TIER 1]

Despite massive state investment -- the most recent Big Fund iteration dedicated $47.5 billion, exceeding the U.S. CHIPS Act's $39 billion -- China remains far from self-sufficiency. Goldman Sachs estimated Chinese domestic suppliers met only about 14% of China's semiconductor demand by value in 2024, projected to rise to 37% by 2030 -- far short of the Made in China 2025 target of 70% self-sufficiency. Key bottlenecks persist: China cannot produce EUV machines, and its most advanced foundry (SMIC) achieves only ~30% yield rates compared to TSMC's 90%, producing chips at 40-50% higher cost with one-third the volume. China is "unlikely to achieve full chip self-sufficiency any time soon because of high technological hurdles in producing advanced manufacturing equipment and materials."

- Oxford Economics. "Roadblocks to China's Chip Self-Sufficiency Dream." 2024.
- CNBC. "China's Ambitions for Chip Self-Sufficiency Thwarted by Lack of Chipmaking Tools." September 27, 2024.
- Goldman Sachs Research, semiconductor self-sufficiency estimates, 2024.
- Economics Observatory. "What's Happening in China's Semiconductor Industry?" 2024.

**The structural explanation:** Authoritarian systems can mobilize capital but cannot replicate the decentralized, trust-based, failure-tolerant collaboration ecosystem that produced ASML's 30-year EUV bet, TSMC's customer-trust foundry model, or the U.S. EDA oligopoly. As Miller argues in *Chip War*: "for all its manifest flaws and failures, the American capitalist system has repeatedly outperformed other systems and in the process has done much to bolster the security of democracy." [TIER 1]

## III. Autocracies as Dependent Consumers -- The Strategic Logic

### A. Copeland's Trade Expectations Theory

**Dale Copeland, *Economic Interdependence and War* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2015).**
[TIER 1]

Copeland's central insight: what matters is not current trade levels but "a state's expectations of the future trade and investment environment." When states expect continued access to trade and investment, interdependence promotes peace. When states expect future trade to be cut off, interdependence can actually drive preventive war -- states strike before losing access to critical resources.

This framework has direct application to the semiconductor supply chain:

- **Positive expectations scenario:** As long as China expects continued access to TSMC chips, ASML-enabled fabs, and Western EDA tools, the opportunity cost of military conflict (especially over Taiwan) remains prohibitively high. Dependency generates restraint.
- **Negative expectations scenario:** If China concludes that access will be permanently severed -- through total decoupling, complete export bans, or aggressive containment -- Copeland's logic predicts increased willingness to consider preventive action, including military options.

- Copeland, Dale C. *Economic Interdependence and War.* Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2015.

### B. The Asymmetry Argument

The chapter's central policy claim rests on a deliberate asymmetry:

1. **Democracies hold chokepoint roles** -- they supply the irreplaceable inputs (EUV machines, advanced foundry capacity, EDA tools, critical materials).
2. **Autocracies participate as dependent consumers** -- they purchase finished chips, use democratic supply chain outputs, and benefit from integration.
3. **This asymmetry is strategic:** dependent consumers are deterred from aggression by the costs of losing access. But because they lack chokepoint status, they cannot weaponize their position against the democratic coalition.

The right policy: **integrate autocracies as consumers (dependency = restraint), but prevent them from becoming chokepoint suppliers (autarky = danger).** Keep China buying TSMC chips; prevent China from becoming the next TSMC.

### C. Why Total Exclusion Backfires -- Historical Evidence

**Japan 1941: The Oil Embargo and Pearl Harbor.**
[TIER 1]

The most vivid example of total economic exclusion triggering preventive war. In July 1941, the U.S. froze Japanese assets and embargoed oil exports. Japan imported 94% of its oil; the Imperial Japanese Navy estimated it had less than two years of fuel reserves. Faced with economic strangulation and "unable to retreat," Japan's leaders concluded they had to strike before their military capacity degraded further. The Pearl Harbor attack was a direct consequence of negative trade expectations under total exclusion. This case is a canonical illustration of Copeland's theory.

- History.state.gov. "Japan, China, the United States and the Road to Pearl Harbor."
- Independent Institute. "How U.S. Economic Warfare Provoked Japan's Attack on Pearl Harbor."

**Interwar Sanctions and Fascist Aggression.**
[TIER 2]

The League of Nations' sanctions against Italy during the Italo-Abyssinian War (1935-1936) failed to restrain Mussolini and instead pushed Italy toward alignment with Nazi Germany. More broadly, "Fascist Italy, Nazi Germany, and militarist Japan all responded to sanctions not by backing down but by becoming more aggressive. Territorial expansion was a path, in their eyes, to secure control over the key material resources that sanctions threatened to sever them from: grain, coal, iron ore, oil." Total exclusion drove these regimes toward autarky and preventive conquest.

- Mulder, Nicholas. *The Economic Weapon: The Rise of Sanctions as a Tool of Modern War.* New Haven: Yale University Press, 2022. [VERIFY exact citation]

**Sanctions and Preventive War -- General Findings.**
[TIER 2]

Research on sanctions severity finds a U-shaped relationship with conflict: sanctions that are too weak fail to alter behavior; sanctions that are too severe "force the sanctioned country to rapidly increase military power or preemptively attack to prevent pending sanctions." Only moderately severe sanctions produce conflict reduction. This supports the chapter's argument for calibrated integration rather than total exclusion.

- Joshi, Shashank, et al. "Sanctions and Preventive War." *Journal of Conflict Resolution* 61, no. 8 (2017): 1711-1739. [VERIFY exact authors]

### D. The Middle Path -- Access Without Structural Power

The trade policy literature supports a middle ground between full engagement and total containment:

**"Change through trade" (Wandel durch Handel)** -- the idea that economic integration would liberalize autocratic regimes -- has largely failed. As CEPA analysis notes: "the Chinese party state and Russia's klepto-regime have become more authoritarian, and autocrats have managed to reverse engineer economic integration to suit their particular interests." [TIER 2]

But the alternative -- complete decoupling -- generates the Copeland dynamic (negative expectations leading to preventive action).

The chapter's proposed resolution: **conditional integration at the consumer level, exclusion from chokepoint roles.** Autocracies retain access to finished products and downstream participation (maintaining positive trade expectations and dependency-based restraint), but are excluded from the upstream positions that would grant them structural leverage over the democratic coalition. This is neither naive engagement nor reckless containment -- it is strategic asymmetry.

## IV. The Lock-In Problem -- Engagement with Drago and Laine

### A. The Intelligence Curse Framework

**Luke Drago and Rudolf Laine, "The Intelligence Curse" (April 2025), published at intelligence-curse.ai.**
[TIER 2]

Drago and Laine develop a resource curse analogy for AI: once artificial intelligence makes non-human factors of production (capital, compute, energy, data centers) overwhelmingly more important than human labor, "powerful actors -- like states and companies -- no longer have an incentive to care about regular people." The parallel to oil-rich rentier states is explicit: just as resource wealth allows authoritarian governments to avoid taxing citizens (and therefore avoid accountability to them), AI-generated wealth could allow powerful actors to bypass the social contract entirely.

Key claims from the essay:

- "AI will obsolete even outlier human talent. Social mobility will stop." [TIER 2]
- Non-human factors of production becoming "overwhelmingly more important than humans" could result in "the gradual -- or sudden -- disempowerment of the vast majority of humanity." [TIER 2]
- The authors explicitly reference the risk of "AI-enabled coups" and rapid power seizure when institutional checks weaken. [TIER 2]

Their proposed solutions: (1) **Avert** catastrophic risks through technical safety measures; (2) **Diffuse** AI capabilities widely, aligning AI to individual users so economic value remains tied to people; (3) **Democratize** institutions by channeling excess rents into citizen wealth funds and preventing AI from owning assets or holding institutional power.

- Drago, Luke, and Rudolf Laine. "The Intelligence Curse." April 2025. https://intelligence-curse.ai/intelligence-curse.pdf

### B. The Lock-In Mechanism Applied to Supply Chains

The Drago-Laine framework raises a genuine challenge for the democratic supply chain thesis:

**The problem:** If the world becomes dependent on a particular nation's chokepoint role, the international community develops a strong interest in that nation's political stability -- regardless of its regime type. Once a country becomes an indispensable supplier, regime change becomes a threat to global supply chain continuity. The same structure that creates deterrence through dependency also *locks in* the existing political system of the chokepoint holder.

**Applied to the chapter's argument:**

1. If an autocracy were to achieve chokepoint status (e.g., if China became the dominant advanced chip manufacturer), the world would have incentives to stabilize that autocratic regime -- effectively freezing its political system in place.
2. The resource curse analogy applies: just as oil wealth locks in Gulf autocracies (the world needs their oil and therefore tolerates their governance), AI supply chain centrality could lock in whatever regime controls the chokepoints.
3. This lock-in is a *real cost* even when the chokepoint holder is a democracy -- international dependence on Taiwan's semiconductor production, for example, constrains the international community's willingness to support Taiwanese sovereignty in ways that might destabilize TSMC's operations.

### C. Resolution -- Why This Strengthens the Democratic Supply Chain Argument

The lock-in problem is precisely why chokepoint roles must be reserved for democracies. The chapter should make this argument in three steps:

**Step 1:** Acknowledge the lock-in effect honestly. Dependence on any chokepoint holder -- democratic or autocratic -- creates incentives to preserve the status quo. This is a structural feature, not a bug to be wished away.

**Step 2:** Argue that lock-in under democratic chokepoint holders is categorically preferable. When the locked-in regime is a democracy, the frozen system at least preserves democratic governance, rule of law, and institutional checks. When the locked-in regime is an autocracy, the frozen system preserves repression. The lock-in effect is regime-neutral in its mechanism but not in its moral or strategic consequences.

**Step 3:** Emphasize the temporal window. The period *before* a country becomes a chokepoint supplier is the last practical window for political reform or regime change. Once a nation achieves irreplaceable supply chain status, external pressure for political change becomes nearly impossible (the world cannot afford to destabilize the supplier). This means the composition of the chokepoint coalition should be settled -- in favor of democracies -- before lock-in occurs. Allowing autocracies to reach chokepoint status forecloses future democratization.

## V. Why Integration with Adversaries Still Matters

### A. The Copeland Dynamic Under Total Exclusion

As established above, Copeland's theory predicts that when states expect future trade to be severed, interdependence no longer restrains -- it provokes. Total exclusion of autocracies from the semiconductor supply chain would:

1. Signal that access will be permanently denied, triggering negative expectations.
2. Incentivize autarky-seeking behavior -- massive investment in indigenous capabilities outside democratic oversight.
3. In the extreme case, incentivize preventive military action to seize chokepoint assets (the Taiwan scenario).

### B. The Right Middle Path: Consumer Integration, Chokepoint Exclusion

The chapter should advocate a two-tier architecture:

| Tier | Access Level | Examples | Strategic Logic |
|------|-------------|----------|-----------------|
| **Chokepoint suppliers** | Full integration, deep collaboration | Netherlands, Taiwan, Japan, South Korea, Germany, United States | Democratic institutions enable credible commitments; lock-in preserves democratic governance |
| **Consumer participants** | Access to finished products, downstream integration | China, Russia, other autocracies | Dependency generates restraint; positive trade expectations prevent preventive action |
| **Excluded** | No access | States under comprehensive sanctions (North Korea, etc.) | Only for the most extreme cases where even consumer access poses unacceptable proliferation risks |

### C. Policy Implications

1. **Maintain consumer access:** Continue selling advanced chips and downstream products to China (within security limits). This preserves the dependency relationship that generates restraint.
2. **Block chokepoint ascension:** Use export controls on manufacturing equipment (EUV machines, advanced EDA tools) to prevent China or other autocracies from developing independent advanced manufacturing capability. The October 2022 and subsequent U.S. export controls on semiconductor equipment to China are the operative example.
3. **Strengthen democratic supply chain resilience:** Diversify manufacturing within the democratic coalition (CHIPS Act investments, European Chips Act, Japan's semiconductor revival) to reduce single-point-of-failure risks (especially Taiwan dependency) while keeping chokepoints within democratic hands.
4. **Avoid the Japan 1941 mistake:** Calibrate restrictions to maintain positive trade expectations. The goal is to prevent chokepoint acquisition, not to strangle all economic participation. Total decoupling is self-defeating.
