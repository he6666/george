# Research Brief: Chapter 8 — An AI NATO

## The Intelligence Peace Thesis | Liu He
### Research compiled: March 6, 2026

## Overview

This brief supports Chapter 8, the policy capstone of the essay series. The chapter argues that the AI supply chain's peace-inducing properties (established in earlier chapters) require deliberate institutional architecture — a collective security organization modeled on NATO but organized around semiconductor supply chain nodes rather than geography. The chapter should acknowledge and critically engage with Pax Silica (launched December 2025) as a first step in this direction, while arguing it is insufficient.

---

## 1. WHY COLLECTIVE DEFENSE IS NECESSARY

### 1.1 The Silicon Shield Is Incomplete

The "Silicon Shield" concept — the idea that Taiwan's semiconductor dominance deters Chinese aggression — was popularized by Taiwanese President Tsai Ing-wen and analyzed extensively in Chris Miller's *Chip War* (2022). Miller himself is skeptical of its sufficiency:

> "I hope that President Tsai is right, but I'm not sure that she is. Germany bet heavily in recent years that economic and energy interdependence would make Russia more peaceable. That gamble didn't work out, as Russia has chosen to ruin the future of its gas industry in pursuit of geopolitical gain."
> — Chris Miller, interview (CFR, 2022) [Tier 1: direct quote from published source]

**Key insight for chapter:** Raw economic dependency is not the same as institutional deterrence. The Silicon Shield relies on an *implicit* threat (attacking Taiwan destroys the chip supply) but lacks *explicit* commitment mechanisms. The Russia-Germany gas analogy is powerful but must be handled carefully — the chapter should argue that the analogy actually *supports* the case for formal architecture (Germany's mistake was not formalizing energy interdependence into a security commitment, not that interdependence itself failed).

### 1.2 Informal Deterrence vs. Formal Alliances — The Literature

Glenn Snyder's *Alliance Politics* (Cornell Studies in Security Affairs, 1997) provides the theoretical backbone. Snyder's core argument: alliances involve an inherent security dilemma between **abandonment** (the ally won't show up) and **entrapment** (being dragged into an ally's conflict). Formal commitments reduce abandonment risk at the cost of increased entrapment risk — but in the semiconductor context, the interdependence already exists, so the entrapment cost is minimal. Formalization primarily reduces abandonment uncertainty.

> "Alliance commitments are always somewhat in doubt; hence — especially during periods of rising tension — there are tendencies to strengthen and expand commitments in order to guard against abandonment."
> — Glenn H. Snyder, *Alliance Politics* (1997) [Tier 1: published academic monograph]

**Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman's "Weaponized Interdependence" framework** (International Security, 2019, Vol. 44, No. 1, pp. 42-79) provides the structural analysis. They identify two mechanisms by which states exploit network centrality: the **panopticon effect** (surveillance through hub control) and the **chokepoint effect** (denial of network access). The semiconductor supply chain exhibits extreme chokepoint concentration — but currently without institutional governance.
[Tier 1: peer-reviewed, International Security]

**The gap:** Farrell and Newman describe the *offensive* use of chokepoints (how states weaponize them). The chapter should argue for *defensive* institutionalization — turning chokepoints into collectively defended assets rather than unilateral weapons.

### 1.3 Diversification Is Dismantling the Peace Mechanism

The current policy consensus — diversify away from Taiwan, onshore production, reduce dependencies — is well-intentioned but strategically counterproductive. If successful, diversification would *reduce* the cost of attacking Taiwan by making the world less dependent on it. The chapter should frame this as a paradox: **the policy designed to reduce risk actually increases it by weakening the deterrent.**

The correct approach is not diversification *away from* allies but redundancy *within* the alliance — building backup capacity in allied nations while maintaining and defending the primary nodes.

## 2. WHAT AN AI NATO WOULD LOOK LIKE

### 2.1 Membership — Supply Chain Node States

Membership should be determined by structural importance to the AI supply chain, not by geography or population size. Core members:

| Nation | Population | Critical Role | Chokepoint Asset |
|--------|-----------|---------------|------------------|
| **Taiwan** | 24M | Advanced foundry (90%+ leading-edge) | TSMC |
| **Netherlands** | 17M | EUV lithography monopoly | ASML |
| **Japan** | 125M | Photoresists, silicon wafers, equipment | Tokyo Electron, JSR, Shin-Etsu |
| **South Korea** | 52M | Memory, advanced foundry | Samsung, SK Hynix |
| **United States** | 330M | Chip design (EDA), AI models, GPUs | Nvidia, AMD, Cadence, Synopsys |
| **Germany** | 84M | Specialty chemicals, optics (for ASML) | Zeiss (EUV optics), BASF |
| **United Kingdom** | 67M | Chip design (ARM architecture) | ARM Holdings |
| **Israel** | 9M | Chip design, Intel fabs | Intel Haifa, Tower Semiconductor |
| **Singapore** | 6M | Packaging, testing, trade hub | GlobalFoundries fab |
| **Malaysia** | 33M | Back-end assembly and testing | Major OSAT hub |

[Tier 2: composition derived from supply chain analysis across multiple sources; specific roles are well-documented but the grouping is the author's proposal]

**Key argument:** Geography and population are irrelevant. The Netherlands (17M people) matters more to global AI capability than most nations with 10x its population. This is a new kind of alliance logic.

### 2.2 Article 5 Equivalent — Collective Response Trigger

An attack on a member's AI infrastructure — whether military invasion, economic coercion, or cyber sabotage — triggers collective response. This could include:

- **Military response** (in cases of kinetic attack, e.g., invasion of Taiwan)
- **Economic retaliation** (coordinated export controls, trade sanctions)
- **Supply chain isolation** (cutting the aggressor off from the allied supply chain)
- **Cyber defense** (collective response to cyberattacks on fabs or design centers)

Victor Cha's "collective resilience" framework is the closest existing academic model:

> Target states could "band together in a collective resilience alliance and practice economic deterrence by promising to retaliate against China's high-dependence trade should Beijing act against any one of the alliance members."
> — Victor D. Cha, "Collective Resilience: Deterring China's Weaponization of Economic Interdependence," *International Security* 48, no. 1 (Summer 2023): 91-124. [Tier 1: peer-reviewed, top IR journal]

Cha's data: Previous and current targets of Chinese economic coercion collectively export over $46.6 billion worth of goods to China on which China is more than 70% dependent as a proportion of its total imports of those goods. This is the retaliatory leverage.

Cha's forthcoming book: *China's Weaponization of Trade: Resistance through Collective Resilience* (Columbia University Press, 2026) [Tier 1: announced academic press publication]

### 2.3 Intelligence Sharing on Supply Chain Threats

Modeled on NATO's intelligence-sharing infrastructure but focused on:
- Cyber threats to semiconductor fabs and design houses
- Supply chain disruption early warning (natural disasters, political instability)
- Detection of illicit technology transfer
- Monitoring of adversary attempts to develop parallel supply chains

### 2.4 Coordinated Export Control Policy

Current approach: The U.S. unilaterally imposes export controls and then pressures allies to follow. This creates friction (e.g., U.S. pressure on the Netherlands regarding ASML's China sales, where ASML derived 36% of its 2024 revenue from China).

**Proposed approach:** Coordinated, multilateral export control regime with:
- Joint decision-making (reducing perception of U.S. unilateralism)
- Revenue-sharing or compensation mechanisms for allies bearing disproportionate costs
- Defensive framing (protecting allied infrastructure) rather than offensive framing (containing China)

Note: ASML has been unable to ship EUV machines to China since 2019 due to Dutch export restrictions, but DUV sales continued until further restrictions in 2024. The unilateral-then-multilateral approach creates resentment and compliance gaps.
[Tier 1: multiple news sources confirm ASML export timeline]

### 2.5 Joint Investment — Redundancy Within the Alliance

The principle: Build backup capacity *within* allied nations, not as a substitute for existing nodes but as insurance. If Taiwan's fabs were damaged, allied fabs in Arizona, Japan (Kumamoto), and Europe could scale up — but only if investment is coordinated.

### 2.6 Governance Structure

Options:
- **Unanimous consent** (NATO model): Every member has a veto. Maximizes buy-in but creates decision paralysis.
- **Weighted voting** (IMF/World Bank model): Votes proportional to supply chain contribution. More efficient but risks smaller members being overruled.
- **Consensus with opt-out** (EU model): Decisions proceed unless a member formally objects. Balances efficiency and sovereignty.

**Recommendation for the chapter:** Argue for a hybrid — unanimous consent on Article 5 triggers (too important for majority rule), weighted voting on investment allocation and export control coordination.

## 3. WHY YOU CAN'T DIVERSIFY AWAY FROM TAIWAN

### 3.1 TSMC Arizona: The Numbers

**Total planned investment:** $165 billion for a "gigafab cluster" of six fabs, two advanced packaging facilities, and an R&D center.
[Source: TSMC press release, March 2025; Blackridge Research project profile]

**Current capacity:**
- Fab 1 (N4/4nm): Began production early 2025 at 10,000 wafers/month, scaling to 30,000 wafers/month
- Fab 2 (N3/3nm): Structure completed 2025, volume production targeted for 2028
- Fab 3 (N2/A16): Ground broken April 2025, production targeted end of decade
- Fabs 4-6: Planned but no specific timeline

**TSMC global capacity:** Exceeded 17 million 12-inch equivalent wafers annually in 2025.

**Arizona as share of global:** Even at full Phase 1 capacity (30,000 wafers/month = 360,000/year), Arizona represents approximately **2.1% of TSMC's total global capacity**. The completed six-fab cluster would account for **30% of TSMC's 2nm and more advanced capacity** — but that is 30% of *one process node*, not 30% of total production.
[Tier 1: TSMC official data and filings]

**Critical point for the chapter:** $165 billion buys you a backup, not a replacement. And even this backup is years away from meaningful scale.

### 3.2 CHIPS Act: Significant but Insufficient

- **Total appropriation:** $52.7 billion for semiconductor activities, FY2022-FY2027
- **Goal:** 20% of world's most advanced chips by end of decade; nearly 30% of leading-edge chips by 2032
- **Current starting point:** Effectively 0% domestic leading-edge production when the Act was signed
- **Key awards:** $6.6B to TSMC, $7.86B to Intel, $6.6B to Samsung, $6.1B to Micron (Biden administration, late 2024)

**Reality check from Carnegie Endowment:** "The CHIPS Act, by itself, will not fully accomplish any of [its three main] goals. The act is a major step forward, but it leaves multiple gaps that require additional government action."
[Source: Carnegie Endowment for International Peace, November 2022] [Tier 1]

**Implementation challenges:** Delays in grant disbursement, skilled worker shortages, congressional funding cuts to research provisions, and higher-than-expected construction costs.
[Source: Conference Board policy backgrounder; multiple news sources]

### 3.3 Intel 18A: Progress but Not Parity

- Intel 18A is in risk production with volume manufacturing scheduled for later in 2025 (delayed twice — first to 2026, then mid-2026)
- Lead products (Panther Lake, Clearwater Forest) have powered on and booted operating systems
- **Yields:** Sufficient for product shipments but "not yet high enough to deliver normal profit margins" — industry-standard yields not expected until **2027**
- **External foundry adoption:** Unclear whether customers will use 18A; Intel discovered the technology "would only serve a narrow base of high-end applications"
- **Competitive gap:** TSMC's 2nm node on track for mass production by end of 2025, giving TSMC a critical head start
- Nvidia's delayed trials of Intel 18A have "cast a shadow" over foundry ambitions

[Sources: Tom's Hardware, Nasdaq, Intel Newsroom, multiple industry analysts] [Tier 1-2]

### 3.4 Samsung Taylor, Texas: Delays Compound

- **Original construction deadline:** April 2024
- **Revised deadline:** End of October 2025, then pushed further
- **Current status:** Over 90% structurally complete; opening target revised to end of 2026
- **Root cause:** Weak demand; originally planned 4nm process "no longer aligns with current customer needs" after delays
- **Pivot:** Upgrading to 2nm production to stay competitive
- **One bright spot:** Tesla committed to using Samsung Foundry's Texas capacity for AI chips through 2033
- **CHIPS Act support:** Up to $6.6 billion in subsidies
- **Local impact:** Taylor, TX reduced incentive package from $25M to $9M due to delays
- Samsung reportedly further delayed the 2nm fab to **2027** (as of March 2026 reports)

[Sources: TrendForce, Tom's Hardware, Samsung, Taylor Press, Sammyfans] [Tier 1-2]

### 3.5 The Bottom Line

> **"Securing Taiwan is securing AI."**

Taiwan produces ~92% of the world's leading-edge semiconductors. Even with $165B from TSMC, $52.7B from CHIPS Act, and billions more from Intel and Samsung, meaningful U.S. self-sufficiency in advanced chips is **5-10+ years away** — and even then, it would be partial. The realistic option is not replacing Taiwan; it is defending Taiwan within a collective security framework.

## 4. EXISTING PROPOSALS AND PRECEDENTS

### 4.1 Pax Silica (December 2025 — Present)

**What it is:** A U.S.-led, non-binding international initiative focused on coordinating "trusted" supply chains for advanced technologies — semiconductors, AI infrastructure, critical minerals, advanced manufacturing, logistics, and energy. Launched December 12, 2025, at a summit in Washington, D.C. convened by Under Secretary of State for Economic Affairs Jacob Helberg.

**Initial signatories:** Japan, South Korea, Singapore, Israel, Qatar, UK, United Arab Emirates, Australia. India signed February 20, 2026. Guest contributions from Taiwan, the EU, Canada, and the OECD.

**Cooperation areas:** Connectivity and data infrastructure; compute and semiconductors; advanced manufacturing; logistics; mineral refining and processing; energy.

[Sources: U.S. Department of State; Wikipedia; Fortune; Asia Times; DCD] [Tier 1]

**Critical limitations (for the chapter's argument):**

1. **Non-binding:** It is a declaration of intent, not a treaty. No enforcement mechanism, no Article 5 equivalent.

2. **Notable exclusions:** The Netherlands (home to ASML), Germany (Zeiss optics for EUV), and Taiwan are not signatories — only "guest contributors." As CEPA analysis noted: "The big omission is the EU, and specifically the Netherlands, home to ASML, the only company in the world able to make the lithography systems needed to produce the most advanced chips."
[Source: Science|Business; CEPA] [Tier 1]

3. **Vague commitments:** "Pressed during a briefing in December on what obligations signatory countries would have, Helsberg gave few details, although he did mention information sharing." (CEPA)

4. **No security guarantee:** Pax Silica addresses supply chain coordination but contains no mutual defense commitment. It is an economic coordination mechanism, not a security alliance.

5. **Timeline problem:** "Even if Pax Silica signatories take it 'seriously,' investments would only bear fruit in five or realistically ten years' time, well beyond the current Trump administration." (Real Instituto Elcano analysis)

6. **Conceptual ambiguity:** Has been described as giving "'Trump-style' framing to what was already under way" — i.e., rebranding existing trends rather than creating new architecture.

**Chapter framing:** Pax Silica is a step in the right direction — it acknowledges that the AI supply chain requires coordinated governance. But it is to an AI NATO what the Kellogg-Briand Pact was to actual collective security: an aspiration without teeth. The chapter should acknowledge it as a precursor while arguing for something far more binding and comprehensive.

### 4.2 Victor Cha's "Collective Resilience"

Published in *International Security* 48, no. 1 (Summer 2023): 91-124. Georgetown professor and CSIS Korea Chair.

**Core argument:** Reshoring and friend-shoring "insulate states against China's disruptions to the production chain while doing nothing to stop its economic coercion." Instead, target states should band together in a collective resilience alliance to practice economic deterrence — threatening coordinated retaliation against China's high-dependence imports if Beijing coerces any single member.

**Key data point:** Targets of Chinese economic coercion collectively export >$46.6B in goods on which China is 70%+ import-dependent. This is the retaliatory leverage that makes collective resilience credible.

**Relevance to chapter:** Cha provides the economic deterrence logic. The chapter extends this into a full security architecture with military commitments. Cha focuses on retaliatory capacity; the chapter should add preventive/defensive capacity.
[Tier 1: top-tier academic source]

### 4.3 G-7 Hiroshima Declaration (May 2023)

The G-7 Leaders' Statement on Economic Resilience and Economic Security (May 20, 2023) explicitly addressed weaponized dependencies:

> "The world has encountered a disturbing rise in incidents of economic coercion that seek to exploit economic vulnerabilities and dependencies... attempts to weaponize economic dependencies by forcing G7 members and our partners including small economies to comply and conform will fail and face consequences."
[Source: G-7 Leaders' Statement, May 20, 2023] [Tier 1: official government document]

**Key outcome:** Establishment of a **Coordination Platform on Economic Coercion** to "increase our collective assessment, preparedness, deterrence and response to economic coercion" through "early warning and rapid information sharing."

**Significance:** This was the first time a major multilateral body explicitly committed to collective action against economic coercion, specifically mentioning "critical and emerging technologies such as microelectronics." The concept of weaponized interdependence moved from academic theory to policy declaration.

**Limitation:** The G-7 is a consultative body, not a security alliance. The Coordination Platform has no enforcement mechanism and no mutual defense commitment.
[Tier 1: official communique and White House fact sheet]

### 4.4 Chip 4 Alliance

**Proposed:** By the United States, comprising the US, Taiwan, Japan, and South Korea.

**Rationale:** These four nations account for **82% of global semiconductor industrial output.**

**Status:** The alliance has remained largely aspirational. Despite initial working-group meetings, it has not been formalized into a treaty or binding agreement.

**Challenges:**
- South Korea's reluctance to antagonize China (its largest trading partner)
- Competition among member companies (TSMC vs. Samsung vs. Intel)
- CHIPS Act subsidies creating tensions (subsidizing competitors on allied soil)
- No institutional secretariat, no binding commitments, no enforcement

**Relevance:** Chip 4 demonstrates both the logic of supply-chain-based alliances (the right members) and the pitfalls of informal arrangements (no binding commitments, no institutional infrastructure).
[Sources: Global Taiwan Institute; Tom's Hardware; Fortune; TechWire Asia] [Tier 1-2]

### 4.5 AUKUS

**Established:** September 2021 between Australia, UK, and US.

**Pillar 1:** Nuclear-powered submarine technology transfer to Australia.
**Pillar 2:** Advanced capabilities including AI, quantum computing, cyber, hypersonics, electronic warfare, undersea capabilities.

**Precedent value:** AUKUS demonstrates that deep technology sharing between allies is possible — the US-UK Mutual Defense Agreement has enabled nuclear technology sharing for decades. However, AUKUS Pillar 2 agreements are being "created from scratch" and face significant bureaucratic hurdles.

**Limitation as model:** Too narrow (three members), too focused on military applications, and does not address the civilian semiconductor supply chain. A recent War on the Rocks analysis (March 2026) argued: "Washington built the AI infrastructure AUKUS needs — then locked allies out."
[Sources: CSIS; Wikipedia; IBM; War on the Rocks] [Tier 1-2]

### 4.6 NATO's Own Evolution

NATO has been evolving toward technology and economic security:
- **DIANA** (Defence Innovation Accelerator for the North Atlantic): By March 2024, included 23 accelerator sites and 182 test centres across 28 allied countries.
- **NATO Innovation Fund:** EUR 1 billion for defense technology startups.
- **2025 The Hague Summit:** "NATO Rapid Adoption Action Plan" to accelerate technology adoption.
- **Defense spending:** Historic 2025 agreement to raise target from 2% to **5% of GDP by 2035**.
- **Science & Technology Trends 2025-2045 report:** Identified AI and semiconductor dependencies as critical 20-year trends.

**Limitation:** NATO's geographic scope (Euro-Atlantic) excludes the most critical supply chain nodes: Taiwan, Japan, South Korea, Singapore. NATO is evolving in the right direction but cannot be the vehicle for AI supply chain security because its membership is wrong.
[Sources: NATO PA reports; NATO official texts] [Tier 1]

### 4.7 IPEF (Indo-Pacific Economic Framework)

**Launched:** 2022 by the Biden administration. 14 member states.
**Supply Chain Agreement:** Entered into force February 24, 2024. Establishes Supply Chain Council, Crisis Response Network, and Labor Rights Advisory Board.

**Relevance:** IPEF specifically targets semiconductor supply chain resilience as a critical sector. However, it is an economic framework, not a security alliance, and lacks any defense commitment.

**Limitation:** Non-binding, no enforcement, no mutual defense obligation. The supply chain pillar focuses on information sharing and crisis coordination — useful but not sufficient.
[Sources: U.S. Department of Commerce; CSIS; State Department] [Tier 1]

## 5. THE SUPPLY CHAIN AS SECURITY ARCHITECTURE

### 5.1 The New Logic: Structural Importance Over Size

Traditional security alliances are built on geography and military mass. An AI NATO would be built on **structural indispensability** — the degree to which a nation hosts irreplaceable nodes in the AI supply chain.

This inverts conventional power metrics:
- The Netherlands (17M people) matters more than Brazil (215M) or Nigeria (220M)
- Taiwan (24M) matters more than India (1.4B) for advanced chips (though India is growing in importance — hence its Pax Silica accession)
- Israel (9M) punches above its weight through chip design capabilities

### 5.2 Chokepoint Status as Rational Security Strategy

**Thesis:** For small or threatened nations, becoming indispensable to the AI supply chain is a rational security strategy — perhaps the most effective available.

**Historical precedents for small-state structural leverage:**

1. **Switzerland — Banking neutrality:** After the 1815 Congress of Vienna established Swiss neutrality, Switzerland built a banking empire that made it structurally indispensable to global finance. Its security derived not from military power but from the catastrophic cost of disrupting its financial role. Swiss banks managed $2.4 trillion in foreign assets as of 2022.
[Tier 2: well-established historical narrative, specific figure from Financial Secrecy Index data]

2. **Singapore — Trade chokepoint:** A city-state of 6 million that sits astride the Strait of Malacca (through which ~25-30% of global trade passes). Singapore's security strategy has always been to make itself so essential to global commerce that disrupting it would be unthinkable. It has extended this into semiconductors (GlobalFoundries fab, major packaging/testing hub).
[Tier 2: widely cited strategic analysis]

3. **Turkey — Montreux Convention (1936):** Controls the Turkish Straits between the Black Sea and Mediterranean. The Montreux Convention gives Turkey decisive authority over naval passage — a structural chokepoint that gives a mid-sized power leverage over great-power naval operations.
[Tier 1: established international law]

4. **Panama / Egypt — Canal states:** Small nations whose structural importance to global trade confers outsized geopolitical significance.
[Tier 2: common strategic analysis]

**Application to AI supply chain:** Nations facing security threats should actively pursue chokepoint status in the semiconductor supply chain as a form of structural deterrence. This is not simply economic development — it is a national security strategy.

**The ASML case study:** The Netherlands, a nation of 17 million, holds a global monopoly on EUV lithography through ASML. Every advanced chip in the world requires ASML machines. This gives the Netherlands extraordinary leverage: it can deny EUV access to any nation (as it has done with China since 2019). But it also makes the Netherlands a target — and it lacks the military capacity to defend ASML independently. This is precisely the argument for collective defense.
[Tier 1: extensively documented]

### 5.3 The Farrell-Newman Framework Applied

Farrell and Newman's "Weaponized Interdependence" (2019) describes how states exploit **panopticon** and **chokepoint** effects in global networks. The semiconductor supply chain is the most extreme example of chokepoint concentration in any global industry:

- **EUV lithography:** One company (ASML), one country (Netherlands)
- **Advanced foundry:** One company dominates (TSMC), one island (Taiwan) — 92% of leading-edge
- **EDA tools:** Two companies (Synopsys, Cadence), one country (US)
- **Advanced memory:** Two countries (South Korea, Japan)
- **GPU design:** One company (Nvidia), one country (US)

The chapter should argue: this extreme concentration is both a vulnerability and an opportunity. The vulnerability is obvious (single points of failure). The opportunity is that it creates the structural basis for the most powerful collective security arrangement in history — because every member is genuinely indispensable.

## 6. COUNTERARGUMENTS AND RESPONSES

### 6.1 "An AI NATO Would Provoke China"

**The concern:** Formalizing a semiconductor security alliance would be seen by China as an anti-China bloc, potentially triggering the very conflict it seeks to prevent.

**Response framework:**
- **Framing matters:** The alliance should be framed as *defensive* (protecting supply chain infrastructure) not *offensive* (containing China). The G-7 Hiroshima language provides a template: opposing "economic coercion" rather than naming China directly.
- **China already perceives containment:** U.S. export controls, AUKUS, Quad, Chip 4, and Pax Silica are already perceived by Beijing as containment. A formal alliance would clarify the rules rather than add ambiguity. Clarity can be stabilizing.
- **Deterrence requires credibility:** An informal, ambiguous posture is *more* dangerous than a formal one because it invites miscalculation. NATO's Article 5 has prevented great-power war in Europe for 75 years precisely because its commitment is unambiguous.
- **Consumer access can be preserved:** An AI NATO would not necessarily restrict China's access to *chips* (consumer products), only its ability to coerce *producers* (the supply chain itself). This consumer/producer distinction is critical.

[Tier 3: author's analytical framework, drawing on deterrence theory]

### 6.2 "It's Too Exclusionary"

**The concern:** An alliance of supply chain nodes would exclude most of the world, creating a new technology apartheid.

**Response:**
- **The exclusion already exists:** The supply chain is already concentrated in a handful of nations. An AI NATO doesn't create exclusion; it institutionalizes an existing reality.
- **Consumer vs. supplier distinction:** Most nations are *consumers* of chips, not *producers*. Consumer access would not be restricted. The alliance protects the production infrastructure, not the product distribution.
- **Membership is open to new entrants:** Any nation that develops supply chain relevance can join. This creates positive incentives for nations to invest in semiconductor capabilities.
- **Compare to NATO itself:** NATO doesn't include every nation; it includes nations that share a security commitment. Non-members still benefit from the stability it provides.

### 6.3 "Existing Institutions Are Sufficient"

**Why they're not:**

| Institution | Limitation |
|------------|-----------|
| **NATO** | Wrong membership (no Taiwan, Japan, South Korea, Singapore). Euro-Atlantic focus. |
| **Quad** (US, Japan, India, Australia) | No Taiwan, no South Korea, no Netherlands. India's semiconductor role is nascent. Has not produced concrete semiconductor security outcomes. |
| **AUKUS** | Only 3 members. Military focus. Excludes key supply chain states. |
| **Chip 4** | Never formalized. No binding commitments. No institutional infrastructure. Competitive tensions among members. |
| **Pax Silica** | Non-binding. Excludes Netherlands (ASML) and Taiwan as signatories. No security guarantee. Vague commitments. |
| **IPEF** | Economic framework, no security dimension. Non-binding. |
| **G-7** | Consultative only. No enforcement. No Taiwan, South Korea, Singapore. |

**The gap:** No existing institution combines (a) the right membership (all critical supply chain nodes), (b) binding security commitments (Article 5 equivalent), and (c) enforcement mechanisms. An AI NATO fills this gap.

### 6.4 "It's Politically Unrealistic"

**What it would take:**
- **A crisis catalyst:** NATO itself was founded in response to the Soviet threat. An AI NATO would likely require a precipitating event — a serious supply chain disruption, a credible military threat to Taiwan, or a major act of economic coercion.
- **U.S. leadership:** As with NATO, the U.S. would need to be the driving force, offering security guarantees in exchange for supply chain commitments.
- **Congressional ratification:** A treaty-level commitment would require Senate ratification (2/3 majority). This is the highest political bar.
- **Domestic politics in member states:** South Korea's balancing act with China, the Netherlands' commercial interests with ASML's China revenue, Japan's constitutional constraints on collective defense — all create domestic obstacles.
- **Incremental path:** Start with Pax Silica-level coordination, deepen to binding agreements, eventually formalize into a treaty organization. The chapter should argue this is a 5-10 year project, not an overnight creation.
