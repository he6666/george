# Research Document: Commercial Peace Literature & Copeland's Trade Expectations Theory

## For "The Intelligence Peace Thesis" — Foreign Affairs Essay

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document surveys the academic literature most relevant to the essay's core theoretical challenge: if AI supply chain chokepoints create dependency, does dependency deter war — or, under certain conditions, provoke it? The single most important source is Dale Copeland's trade expectations theory, which holds that what matters is not current interdependence but **expectations about future access**. If a dependent state believes it will be locked out, interdependence becomes a *cause* of preventive war rather than a constraint on it. This has direct implications for how the essay frames exclusionary AI export control regimes.

## SECTION 1: DALE COPELAND'S TRADE EXPECTATIONS THEORY

### 1A. The Original Article (1996)

**Citation:** Copeland, Dale C. "Economic Interdependence and War: A Theory of Trade Expectations." *International Security* 20, no. 4 (Spring 1996): 5-41.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed, top-3 IR journal)
- **URL:** https://muse.jhu.edu/article/447422/summary

**Core Argument:**

Copeland bridges the liberal claim (trade promotes peace) and the realist claim (interdependence creates vulnerability that incentivizes war) by introducing a dynamic variable: a state's **expectations of future trade and investment**. The theory generates two predictions:

1. **Positive expectations + high interdependence = peace.** When leaders expect continued access to trade, they want to remain at peace to secure economic benefits that enhance long-term power.

2. **Negative expectations + high interdependence = war.** When leaders expect to be cut off from vital resources, markets, or supply chains, the anticipated economic decline makes war appear as "the rational lesser of two evils."

**Key passage (from Copeland 1996):**

> "But if state A is dependent and has negative expectations for future trade with B, then the expected value of trade will be very low or negative. If the expected value for trade is lower than the expected value for invasion, war becomes the rational choice, and this is so even when the expected value of invasion is itself negative: war becomes the lesser of two evils."

**Mechanism:** The theory connects negative trade expectations to **preventive war** behavior. If economic decline is anticipated to be severe, leaders begin to view war as preferable to allowing their state to fall to a point where rising states can attack it later or coerce it into submission. Options include preventive war against the rising trading partner before it grows stronger, or a war of expansion to seize sufficient resources and markets to reduce dependency.

**How the essay should use this:** This is the essay's strongest theoretical antagonist. The Intelligence Peace Thesis argues that AI chokepoints create deterrence through dependency. Copeland's theory warns that **the same chokepoints become war triggers if the dependent state expects to be locked out.** The essay must engage this directly — arguing either that (a) AI supply chain architecture makes positive expectations credible because suppliers profit from continued access (unlike zero-sum territorial disputes), or (b) the essay's policy recommendations must include credible commitment mechanisms to maintain access, or (c) both.

### 1B. The Book (2015)

**Citation:** Copeland, Dale C. *Economic Interdependence and War.* Princeton: Princeton University Press, 2015.

- **Tier:** 1 (academic press, ISA Best Book Award 2017)
- **URL:** https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691161594/economic-interdependence-and-war

**Key additions beyond the 1996 article:**

- Tests the theory against the full historical record of great-power conflict from 1790-1991
- Case studies include the origins of WWI (German fears of being cut off from resources), WWII (Japanese fears of oil embargo triggering Pearl Harbor), and Cold War crises
- Applies the framework to the future of U.S.-China relations, predicting that as long as Beijing expects continued access to trade, peace is likely — but technology decoupling or exclusionary policies could flip expectations negative
- Introduces a more sophisticated treatment of how leaders form expectations and the role of signals and commitments in shaping them

### 1C. Copeland in Foreign Affairs (2023)

**Citation:** Copeland, Dale C. "When Trade Leads to War: China, Russia, and the Limits of Interdependence." *Foreign Affairs*, June 2023.

- **Tier:** 2 (policy journal, not peer-reviewed, but the target venue for the essay)
- **URL:** https://www.foreignaffairs.com/china/when-trade-leads-war-china-russia

**Key findings:**

- On China: suggests that optimistic economic expectations should outweigh pessimistic ones "for years to come," but warns that technology decoupling could shift this calculus

**How the essay should use this:** This is the single most directly relevant precedent for the essay. Copeland has already published in *Foreign Affairs* applying trade expectations theory to technology competition. The essay should explicitly cite this piece and position its argument as extending Copeland's framework to the AI/AGI domain — showing that AI supply chains create a new category of dependency with distinctive characteristics (networked chokepoints, high switching costs, knowledge complementarities) that alter the expectations calculus.

### 1D. ISSF Roundtable on Copeland (2016)

**Citation:** H-Diplo/ISSF Roundtable, Volume VIII, No. 11 (2016). Reviewers: Patrick Shea, Richard Maass, Benjamin Fordham. With author response by Dale C. Copeland.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer review roundtable, H-Diplo/ISSF)
- **URL:** https://issforum.org/ISSF/PDF/ISSF-Roundtable-8-11.pdf

**Key critiques from reviewers:**

1. **Shea:** Copeland does not adequately answer how far states look into the future when making decisions related to war and peace. The time-horizon problem is unresolved — do leaders discount expectations at 5 years, 10 years, 50 years?

2. **Maass:** The theoretical chapters "overemphasize several factors that ultimately leave them less satisfying than they should be." Identifies issues with how Copeland operationalizes expectations.

3. **Fordham (most critical):** Copeland's "ambitious theoretical and methodological claims make for arresting reading, but they are not persuasive." Questions whether the historical evidence truly supports the theory over alternative explanations.

**How the essay should use this:** Acknowledge Copeland's framework is contested — particularly the time-horizon problem (Shea's critique). The essay can turn this into an advantage: AI supply chains have **observable, measurable indicators** of future access (ASML order books, TSMC capacity allocations, open-source model availability) that make expectations formation more tractable than in historical commodity-trade cases.

## SECTION 2: THE COMMERCIAL PEACE LITERATURE

### 2A. Oneal & Russett — The Kantian Peace

**Citation:** Russett, Bruce, and John R. Oneal. *Triangulating Peace: Democracy, Interdependence, and International Organizations.* New York: W.W. Norton, 2001.

- **Tier:** 1 (academic book, foundational text)
- **URL:** https://wwnorton.com/books/9780393976847

**Core argument:** Building on Kant, three variables produce peace: (1) representative democracy, (2) economic interdependence, and (3) membership in international organizations. These three legs form a "virtuous cycle" — each reinforces the others.

**On interdependence specifically:**

- States in highly interdependent economic relationships tend to refrain from fighting with their commercial partners
- Economic interdependence creates peace through two mechanisms: (a) commercial interests averse to war (rationalist), and (b) development of shared identity and values (constructivist)
- Among the three Kantian variables, economic interdependence has the most explanatory power, while international organizations have the least

**Citation (related article):** Oneal, John R., and Bruce Russett. "Assessing the Liberal Peace with Alternative Specifications: Trade Still Reduces Conflict." *Journal of Peace Research* 36, no. 4 (1999): 423-442.

- **Tier:** 1
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0022343399036004003

**Key finding:** Economic interdependence and democracy have "important pacific benefits." The pacific effects of trade are strongest among "politically relevant dyads" (those including a major power or two contiguous states).

**How the essay should use this:** Oneal & Russett provide the statistical backbone for the claim that interdependence generally promotes peace. The essay should cite this as the baseline finding, then argue that AI supply chains create an even stronger version of this mechanism due to the high switching costs, knowledge intensity, and network effects involved.

---

### 2B. Gartzke — The Capitalist Peace

**Citation:** Gartzke, Erik. "The Capitalist Peace." *American Journal of Political Science* 51, no. 1 (2007): 166-191.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed, top political science journal)
- **URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/j.1540-5907.2007.00244.x

**Core argument:**

- "Capitalism, and not democracy, leads to peace"
- Economic development, free markets, and similar interstate interests all reduce militarized disputes
- The "capitalist peace" subsumes and accounts for the democratic peace effect — once you control for financial openness and market integration, the regime-type variable loses significance
- Mechanism: as capital markets deepen, leaders and investors become more sensitive to economic disruption, increasing the opportunity cost of war
- "The rise of global capital markets creates a new mechanism for competition and communication for states that might otherwise be forced to fight"

**Key finding:** Nations that are financially open to the global economy and that face constrained or interdependent monetary policies experience fewer interstate crises.

**How the essay should use this:** Gartzke's argument strengthens the essay's hand by identifying the specific mechanism (capital market integration, investor sensitivity to disruption) that makes economic ties pacifying. AI supply chains combine multiple Gartzke mechanisms: financial openness (cross-border investment in AI), similar interests (shared economic gains from AI deployment), and high disruption costs.

---

### 2C. Barbieri — The Critique: Interdependence Increases Conflict

**Citation:** Barbieri, Katherine. "Economic Interdependence: A Path to Peace or a Source of Interstate Conflict?" *Journal of Peace Research* 33, no. 1 (1996): 29-49.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed)
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0022343396033001003

**Core argument:**

- After controlling for confounding influences, "extensive economic interdependence increases the likelihood that dyads will engage in militarized interstate disputes"
- The relationship is **curvilinear**: low to moderate interdependence reduces conflict, but extreme interdependence increases it
- **Asymmetric dependence** is especially dangerous — when trade benefits are unevenly distributed, the more dependent partner faces vulnerability that can generate friction

**Key finding:** "Interdependence actually increases the likelihood of conflict" (Barbieri 1996: 40).

**Related:** Barbieri, Katherine, and Gerald Schneider. "Globalization and Peace: Assessing New Directions in the Study of Trade and Conflict." *Journal of Peace Research* 36, no. 4 (1999): 387-404.

- **Tier:** 1
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0022343399036004001

**How the essay should use this:** Barbieri is a necessary engagement. Her finding that **asymmetric** interdependence is especially dangerous aligns with Copeland's expectations theory. The essay must address this: AI supply chains are inherently asymmetric (a few supplier nations control chokepoints). The essay should argue that this asymmetry is precisely what creates deterrence — BUT only if accompanied by credible assurances of continued access (managing expectations).

---

### 2D. McDonald — Free Trade, Not Just Trade

**Citation:** McDonald, Patrick J. "Peace through Trade or Free Trade?" *Journal of Conflict Resolution* 48, no. 4 (2004): 547-572.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed)
- **URL:** https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.1177/0022002704266117

**Core argument:**

- A shift from "trade" to "free trade" as the independent variable resolves some of the strongest critiques of the commercial peace
- Free trade promotes peace by removing protective barriers that enhance the domestic power of societal groups likely to support war
- Higher levels of free trade, rather than trade volumes alone, reduce military conflict between states

**How the essay should use this:** McDonald's distinction matters for AI policy. Open, rules-based AI technology sharing (analogous to free trade) is more peace-promoting than closed, bilateral AI deals (analogous to managed trade). The essay's policy recommendations should favor multilateral AI governance frameworks over bilateral exclusion.

### 2E. Systematic Literature Review (2025)

**Citation:** [Authors]. "Economic Interdependence and the Likelihood of War — A Systematic Literature Review." *Cogent Social Sciences* (2025). DOI: 10.1080/23311886.2025.2488114.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed systematic review)
- **URL:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/23311886.2025.2488114

**Key findings from reviewing 45 years of literature (1977-2022):**

- The peace-inhibiting effects of trade asymmetry are **reduced** if the dependent state can compensate via extra-dyadic trade relations (i.e., alternative suppliers)
- If the more dependent country can escape coercion by switching to other trading partners, the likelihood of warfare is reduced despite asymmetric trading ties
- In symmetric dyads, extra-dyadic trade ties actually **reduce** the pacifying effect, because alternative partners make cutting any single bilateral relationship less costly
- Moderator variables are crucial and have been neglected in prior work

**How the essay should use this:** This is a critical finding. The essay should argue that AI supply chain chokepoints are distinctive precisely because **switching costs are astronomically high** — you cannot easily substitute for ASML lithography machines, TSMC fabrication expertise, or Nvidia's CUDA ecosystem. This means the "exit option" that normally mitigates asymmetric dependency is unavailable, making the dependency more binding and (per the liberal view) more peace-promoting — but also (per Copeland) more dangerous if expectations turn negative.

## SECTION 3: THE PRE-WWI COUNTEREXAMPLE

### 3A. Gartzke & Lupu — "Trading on Preconceptions" (2012)

**Citation:** Gartzke, Erik, and Yonatan Lupu. "Trading on Preconceptions: Why World War I Was Not a Failure of Economic Interdependence." *International Security* 36, no. 4 (Spring 2012): 115-150.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed, top IR journal)
- **URL:** https://direct.mit.edu/isec/article-abstract/36/4/115/12042/Trading-on-Preconceptions-Why-World-War-I-Was-Not

**Core argument:**

The conventional wisdom that WWI proves economic interdependence cannot prevent war is **wrong** — or at least deeply misleading. Gartzke and Lupu argue:

1. **WWI began in the weakly integrated portion of Europe.** The Balkans crisis that triggered the war involved states (Serbia, Austria-Hungary, Russia) that were **not** among the most economically interdependent.

2. **Crises among highly interdependent powers were generally resolved peacefully.** The major Western European powers (Britain, France, Germany) — who were deeply integrated — managed multiple crises in the decades before 1914 without war.

3. **Alliance entanglement dragged integrated states into a non-integrated conflict.** The alliance system created commitments that bound Western powers to the decisions of less interdependent Eastern European partners. "In attempting to make these alliances more credible, Western powers shifted foreign policy discretion to the very states that lacked strong economic disincentives to fight."

4. **Counterfactual:** "Had globalization pervaded Eastern Europe, or if the rest of Europe had been less locked into events in the east, Europe might have avoided a 'Great War.'"

**How the essay should use this:** This is extremely valuable. When critics invoke WWI as proof that interdependence fails, the essay can cite Gartzke & Lupu to show that the actual historical evidence is more nuanced — war began where interdependence was *weak*, not where it was strong. The analogy to AI supply chains: the danger is not that AI-interdependent states will fight each other, but that alliance commitments might drag them into conflicts originating in regions *outside* the AI dependency network.

### 3B. Norman Angell — The Historical Backdrop

**Citation:** Angell, Norman. *The Great Illusion.* London: Heinemann, 1910.

- **Tier:** Historical primary source
- **URL:** https://www.gutenberg.org/files/38535/38535-h/38535-h.htm

**Key distinction:** Angell is routinely mischaracterized. He did **not** argue that war was impossible. He argued that war was **futile** — economically irrational — which is a different claim. "War is not impossible... it is not the likelihood of war which is the illusion, but its benefits."

**How the essay should use this:** The essay should preempt the "Norman Angell objection" (that pre-WWI thinkers also thought trade would prevent war) by noting (a) Angell's actual argument was about futility not impossibility, (b) Gartzke & Lupu show WWI is not actually a clean counterexample, and (c) AI supply chain dependencies are qualitatively different from pre-WWI commodity trade in their concentration, complexity, and switching costs.

## SECTION 4: THE RUSSIA-UKRAINE CASE

### 4A. Copeland's Framework Applied to Russia-Ukraine

**Citation (application):** Hari P., Shyam. "Economic Interdependence and Realistic Interests: A Review of the Russia-Ukraine Conflict." *Strategic Analysis* 47, no. 3 (2023).

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed)
- **URL:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09700161.2023.2247745

**Key findings applying Copeland's framework:**

- Russia's trade expectations turned negative as Ukraine moved toward EU/NATO integration — Moscow expected to lose its economic leverage over Ukraine
- "Expectations in Moscow that Russia might be losing its economic leverage over Ukraine contributed to Putin's sense that it was 'now or never'"
- Ukraine's eastward-facing energy and industrial infrastructure (90%+ of natural gas reserves east of the Dnieper) made the stakes concrete
- The theory of trade expectations "may also partly explain why Russia chose to invade Ukraine" — declining trade expectations functioned as a trigger for preventive military action

**How the essay should use this:** This is a powerful case for the essay. Russia-Ukraine **confirms** Copeland's theory: it was not current interdependence that failed, but the *expectation of future exclusion* that triggered war. The essay should argue that the lesson is not "interdependence doesn't work" but "exclusionary signals are dangerous." Policy implication: AI export controls must be designed to maintain positive expectations about future access, not to permanently exclude.

### 4B. Energy Interdependence as Failed Deterrent

**Citation:** Kulakevich, Tatsiana. "The War in Ukraine: The Deterrent Effect of Weaponized Interdependence." *Nationalities Papers* (2024). DOI: 10.1017/nps.2024.75.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed, Cambridge University Press)
- **URL:** https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/nationalities-papers/article/war-in-ukraine-the-deterrent-effect-of-weaponized-interdependence/E4F18CC87644B3DDAF0B38D17CE2B5B7

**Core argument:**

- Putin's confidence to invade was based on calculations that the West would be reluctant to change established economic structures
- "Once established, economic network structures are hard to challenge due to potential costs of changing them"
- An aggressor can prevent third-party involvement by making it more costly for them to displace established economic structures than to intervene
- Extended deterrence game analysis shows how Russia weaponized Europe's energy dependence as a shield against Western retaliation

**How the essay should use this:** This source shows a crucial asymmetry problem. Russia weaponized Europe's dependency on *Russia* (as supplier) to deter *Europe* from intervening. This is the **inverse** of the essay's argument — in the AI case, it is the *supplier* nations (US, Netherlands/ASML, Taiwan/TSMC) that would be protected by dependency, not the dependent consumer states. The essay must be clear about the directionality of the deterrence mechanism.

### 4C. Limits of Weaponized Interdependence

**Citation:** Vieira, Vinicius G. Rodrigues. "The Limits of Weaponised Interdependence after the Russian War against Ukraine." *Contemporary Security Policy* 44, no. 4 (2023): 642-660.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed)
- **URL:** https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/13523260.2023.2256065

**Core argument:**

- Many believed Russia would not invade because weaponized interdependence (e.g., SWIFT sanctions) would impose prohibitive costs
- However, Russia mitigated sanctions through "backdoor channels in trade and financial-monetary matters" — connections beyond the Western financial system
- Sanctions effectiveness was limited because Russia had alternative networks (China, India, Turkey)
- Key insight: weaponized interdependence only works when there are **no alternative networks**

**How the essay should use this:** Directly relevant. The essay can argue that AI supply chains are different from energy supply chains in a critical respect: **there are no alternative networks.** You cannot route around ASML's EUV lithography monopoly the way Russia routed around SWIFT via Chinese banks. The concentration of AI chokepoints (unlike energy, which has multiple producers) makes the weaponized interdependence mechanism more robust in the AI domain.

## SECTION 5: WEAPONIZED INTERDEPENDENCE & SUPPLY CHAIN CHOKEPOINTS

### 5A. Farrell & Newman — Foundational Framework

**Citation:** Farrell, Henry, and Abraham L. Newman. "Weaponized Interdependence: How Global Economic Networks Shape State Coercion." *International Security* 44, no. 1 (Summer 2019): 42-79.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed, top IR journal)
- **URL:** https://direct.mit.edu/isec/article/44/1/42/12237/Weaponized-Interdependence-How-Global-Economic-Networks-Shape-State-Coercion

**Core concepts:**

- States with political authority over the central "hubs" in global economic networks can exploit this position for strategic advantage
- Two mechanisms: (1) the **panopticon effect** (surveillance through data flowing through controlled hubs), and (2) the **chokepoint effect** (ability to limit or deny access to the network)
- Global networks often have centralized nodes caused by how networks emerge and grow — these become strategic assets
- Challenges liberal theories that viewed economic interdependence as inherently peace-promoting

**How the essay should use this:** This is the conceptual foundation for the essay's argument about AI supply chain chokepoints. Farrell & Newman provide the theoretical vocabulary (chokepoint effect, panopticon effect, hub control) that the essay should adopt. The essay extends their framework by arguing that AI chokepoints create not just coercive leverage but **deterrence** — states that control AI chokepoints are protected from invasion because destroying or disrupting them would impose catastrophic costs on the attacker's own AI capabilities.

---

### 5B. Semiconductor Chokepoints

**Citation:** Beaumier, Guillaume, et al. "Cross-Network Weaponization in the Semiconductor Supply Chain." *International Studies Quarterly* 68, no. 1 (2024): sqae003.

- **Tier:** 1 (peer-reviewed)
- **URL:** https://academic.oup.com/isq/article/68/1/sqae003/7578750

**Core argument:**

- Maps the semiconductor supply chain as four interrelated networks: (1) design, (2) raw material, (3) manufacturing equipment, and (4) assembled chips
- The U.S. has a chokepoint in the **design network** and has used this hub position to restrict access to Chinese companies across all four networks — "cross-network weaponization"
- Key concept: a state can leverage centrality in *one* network to enact chokepoints in a *second* network where it does not have direct centrality
- The October 2022 U.S. export controls demonstrate this mechanism in practice

**How the essay should use this:** Directly relevant to the AI supply chain argument. The essay can extend the semiconductor case to AI more broadly: AI chokepoints span compute (chips), data (training corpora), talent (researchers), and algorithms (model architectures), and control over any one of these networks can cascade to restrict access across others. This "cross-network" feature makes AI dependency even more binding than single-network dependencies.

## SECTION 6: SYNTHESIS — HOW THE ESSAY SHOULD USE THESE SOURCES

### The Central Tension

The essay faces a productive tension between two literatures:

| **Liberal Commercial Peace** (Oneal & Russett, Gartzke) | **Trade Expectations Theory** (Copeland) |
|---|---|
| Interdependence raises the cost of war, promoting peace | What matters is not current interdependence but expectations about *future* access |
| AI dependency = deterrence | If a state expects to be locked out of AI, dependency becomes a war trigger |
| Policy: maximize integration | Policy: maximize integration **AND** manage expectations |

### The Essay's Optimal Theoretical Position

The essay should:

1. **Embrace Copeland as the strongest challenge**, not try to dismiss it. This shows theoretical sophistication for *Foreign Affairs* readers.

2. **Argue that AI supply chains have distinctive features** that shift the expectations calculus toward positive expectations:
   - Suppliers profit enormously from continued sales (unlike zero-sum territorial disputes)
   - AI capabilities advance rapidly, creating ongoing demand for next-generation products (unlike static commodities)
   - The knowledge ecosystem is collaborative — cutting off access hurts the supplier's own R&D network effects
   - Multiple chokepoints are distributed across allied nations (US design, Dutch lithography, Taiwanese fabrication, Japanese chemicals), creating collective security incentives

3. **Use the Russia-Ukraine case as a foil**, not an analogy:
   - Russia-Ukraine confirms Copeland: negative expectations about Ukraine's integration into the West triggered preventive war
   - But energy is **fungible** (Russia found alternative buyers); AI chips are **not** (no alternative to ASML EUV machines)
   - Russia had alternative networks (China, India); AI-dependent states do not
   - Therefore AI interdependence is more binding and the deterrence mechanism more robust

4. **Use Gartzke & Lupu on WWI** to preempt the historical objection:
   - WWI began where interdependence was weak, not where it was strong
   - The danger is alliance entanglement with non-interdependent regions
   - Implication: AI-based deterrence is strongest between interdependent states; the risk is that alliance commitments drag AI-hub states into conflicts in regions outside the AI dependency network

5. **Use Barbieri's asymmetry finding** to acknowledge the risk honestly:
   - AI supply chains are inherently asymmetric
   - Asymmetric dependence can generate resentment and friction
   - The essay's policy recommendations must include mechanisms to reduce the coercive potential of asymmetry (multilateral governance, rules-based access, credible non-exclusion commitments)

6. **The policy punchline** (drawing on Copeland + McDonald):
   - Exclusionary AI export controls are **self-defeating** if they signal permanent lockout
   - The essay should advocate for conditional access regimes that maintain positive expectations
   - "Free trade" in AI (per McDonald) is more peace-promoting than managed bilateral deals
   - The goal is to create a system where every major state has a credible pathway to AI access, so that no state faces the negative trade expectations that Copeland identifies as the trigger for preventive war
