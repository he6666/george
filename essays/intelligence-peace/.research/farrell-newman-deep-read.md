## SOURCE 1: The Original Paper (2019)

**Full Citation:**
Henry Farrell and Abraham L. Newman, "Weaponized Interdependence: How Global Economic Networks Shape State Coercion," *International Security* 44, no. 1 (Summer 2019): 42-79.

**URL:** https://direct.mit.edu/isec/article/44/1/42/12237/Weaponized-Interdependence-How-Global-Economic

**Also available via:**
- Henry Farrell's site: https://henryfarrell.net/weaponized-interdependence-how-global-economic-networks-shape-state-coercion/
- Belfer Center: https://www.belfercenter.org/publication/weaponized-interdependence-how-global-economic-networks-shape-state-coercion
- Semantic Scholar (PDF): https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Weaponized-Interdependence:-How-Global-Economic-Farrell-Newman/6b8d5620ef676ac629771fe459a002b9ceeb55bc

**Tier:** Tier 1 -- Gold Standard
> Justification: Peer-reviewed article in *International Security*, the top journal in security studies. Has become one of the most cited IR papers of the 2020s and spawned an entire research program. The foundational text for the concept.

### Core Argument

Farrell and Newman challenge the liberal international relations claim that globalization has led to fragmented, decentralized networks of power. Instead, they argue that key global economic networks have converged toward asymmetric "hub-and-spoke" systems. States with political authority over the central hubs in these networks -- and with appropriate domestic institutions -- can weaponize their structural position for two forms of coercion:

1. **The Panopticon Effect:** States can use their position at network hubs to extract strategically valuable information. Just as Bentham's panopticon allowed a single guard to observe all prisoners, states controlling information chokepoints can surveil financial flows, communications, and data passing through their jurisdiction. The key case: the US Treasury's access to SWIFT data after 9/11 gave Washington unprecedented visibility into global financial transactions.

2. **The Chokepoint Effect:** States can deny adversaries access to global network flows by leveraging control over central nodes. The key case: US-led exclusion of Iranian banks from SWIFT, which severed Iran's access to the global financial system.

### Theoretical Foundation

The paper draws on network science to argue that many global economic networks exhibit "scale-free" properties -- they grow asymmetrically so that some nodes become far more connected than others. This is not a natural law but the result of network effects, efficiency gains, and path dependence. Once a hub emerges (e.g., SWIFT for financial messaging, the US dollar for clearing, major internet exchange points for data flows), all participants have incentives to use it, reinforcing its centrality.

**Key analytical move:** Farrell and Newman argue that liberals are wrong to treat networks as inherently decentralized and pacifying. Networks with hub-and-spoke topology concentrate power in the states that have jurisdiction over those hubs. Globalization does not diffuse power; it concentrates it in new ways.

### Two Conditions for Weaponization

A state can weaponize a network hub if it has:
1. **Effective jurisdiction** over the central economic node (the hub is physically located or legally incorporated within the state's territory)
2. **Appropriate domestic institutions and norms** that allow the state to compel the hub entity to cooperate with intelligence or coercive operations

### Case Studies

**SWIFT (Society for Worldwide Interbank Financial Telecommunication):**
- SWIFT, based in Belgium, processes the vast majority of international financial messaging. Transactions over $10,000 are routed through SWIFT for trust and verification.
- After 9/11, the US Treasury demanded and received access to SWIFT data, gaining a real-time view into global financial flows (panopticon effect).
- The US then used its leverage to exclude Iranian banks from SWIFT, devastating Iran's ability to conduct international trade (chokepoint effect).
- This created a "playbook" subsequently applied to North Korea, Sudan, Belarus, Myanmar, and Russia.

**Internet Communications:**
- Key internet infrastructure (root servers, major exchange points, cloud platforms) is disproportionately located in or controlled by US-based entities.
- NSA surveillance programs (revealed by Snowden) exploited this infrastructure to monitor global communications (panopticon effect).
- The US has used control over internet infrastructure for targeted denial and sanctions enforcement (chokepoint effect).

### Key Passages (from abstract and accessible summaries)

- "Liberals claim that globalization has led to fragmentation and decentralized networks of power relations. This does not explain how states increasingly 'weaponize interdependence' by leveraging global networks of informational and financial exchange for strategic advantage."
- "Key global economic networks have converged toward 'hub and spoke' systems, with important consequences for power relations."
- "Highly asymmetric networks allow states with (1) effective jurisdiction over the central economic nodes and (2) appropriate domestic institutions and norms to weaponize these structural advantages for coercive ends."
- States can "weaponize networks to gather information or choke off economic and information flows, discover and exploit vulnerabilities, compel policy change, and deter unwanted actions."

### Relevance to the IPT

**Where Farrell & Newman VALIDATE the IPT:**
- Their entire framework confirms the IPT's foundational premise: chokepoints in global economic networks confer real, exercisable power. If ASML, TSMC, and other AI supply chain nodes are chokepoints in the Farrell-Newman sense, then the states hosting them have genuine coercive leverage.
- Their network topology argument (hub-and-spoke, scale-free properties) provides the theoretical scaffolding for why AI supply chain chokepoints exist and persist. The IPT can cite this directly.
- Their concept of "effective jurisdiction over central economic nodes" maps perfectly onto the IPT's claim that nations hosting supply chain chokepoints gain security through their structural position.

**Where Farrell & Newman CHALLENGE the IPT:**
- Their framework is fundamentally about *offensive* use of chokepoints -- surveillance and coercion. They show that chokepoints are weapons, not shields. The IPT's claim that chokepoints generate *defensive* collective security is the mirror image of their argument, and the IPT needs to explain why the same structural feature produces different outcomes.
- They demonstrate that chokepoint power is exercised *unilaterally* by the state controlling the hub. The IPT's claim about *multilateral collective defense* of chokepoint nations requires additional theoretical machinery that goes beyond Farrell & Newman.
- Their work implies that chokepoint nations are powerful *aggressors*, not vulnerable *targets needing protection*. The IPT needs to flip this framing.

## SOURCE 2: "The Weaponized World Economy" (2025)

**Full Citation:**
Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman, "The Weaponized World Economy: Surviving the New Age of Economic Coercion," *Foreign Affairs* 104, no. 5 (September/October 2025).

**URL:** https://www.foreignaffairs.com/united-states/weaponized-world-economy-farrell-newman

**Farrell's announcement:** https://henryfarrell.net/the-weaponized-world-economy-cover-story-for-foreign-affair/

**Tier:** Tier 2 -- Strong
> Justification: Cover story in *Foreign Affairs*, the most prestigious policy journal in IR. Not peer-reviewed in the academic sense but editorially vetted to FA's high standards. By the leading scholars on the exact topic. The IPT essay is targeting the same publication and same readership -- this is the piece to engage directly.

### Core Argument

The article's central claim is that "the age of weaponized interdependence" has arrived, and the United States is now "discovering what it is like to have others do unto it as it has eagerly done unto others." After two decades of unilateral American weaponization of economic chokepoints (in finance, information, and technology), other powers -- principally China -- have developed counter-chokepoints of their own.

### Key Arguments and Findings

**1. The Unilateral Era Is Over:**
For over two decades, the United States unilaterally weaponized chokepoints in finance, information flows, and technology for strategic advantage. But this era is ending. "Market exchange has become hopelessly entangled with national security, and the United States must now defend its interests in a world in which other powers can leverage chokepoints of their own."

**2. Counter-Chokepoints Have Emerged:**
China has developed its own economic weapons, particularly in rare-earth minerals (controlling over 90% of processing). The Trump administration had to make concessions on semiconductor export controls in exchange for China easing restrictions on rare-earth minerals that were "crippling the United States' auto industry." This reciprocal weaponization is a new development that the original 2019 paper only anticipated.

**3. The Arms Control Analogy:**
The article's most important policy recommendation: "As during the nuclear era, the United States needs to turn away from unilateralism, toward detente and arms control, and, perhaps in the very long term, toward rebuilding an interdependent global economy on more robust foundations." Farrell and Newman explicitly draw the analogy between nuclear weapons and economic weapons, arguing that just as nuclear arms races needed to be managed through bilateral and multilateral agreements, economic weaponization needs its own "arms control" regime.

**4. The Economic Security State:**
The US needs to build a comprehensive economic security apparatus -- what Farrell and Newman call "the economic security state" -- to manage the intersection of market exchange and national security. This requires institutional transformation, not just new policies.

### Key Passages (from accessible summaries and search results)

- "The age of weaponized interdependence [has arrived], in which the United States is discovering what it is like to have others do unto it as it has eagerly done unto others."
- "This new era will be shaped by weapons of economic and technological coercion -- sanctions, supply chain attacks, and export measures -- that repurpose the many points of control in the infrastructure that underpins the interdependent global economy."
- "[The US needs to turn] away from unilateralism, toward detente and arms control, and, perhaps in the very long term, toward rebuilding an interdependent global economy on more robust foundations."

### Relevance to the IPT

**Where the 2025 FA Piece VALIDATES the IPT:**
- Farrell and Newman's arms control analogy strongly supports the IPT's policy recommendation to stop weaponizing the supply chain offensively. Both arguments converge on the conclusion that unilateral coercion is counterproductive.
- Their observation that counter-chokepoints have emerged (China's rare earths) supports the IPT's claim about the *reciprocal* and *multilateral* nature of supply chain dependency -- it is not just the US that controls chokepoints.
- Their call for rebuilding "an interdependent global economy on more robust foundations" is compatible with the IPT's vision of supply chain interdependence as a peace mechanism -- provided the interdependence is structured for collective defense rather than unilateral coercion.

**Where the 2025 FA Piece CHALLENGES the IPT:**
- Farrell and Newman's entire framing treats weaponized interdependence as a *problem* to be managed through arms control, not a *peace mechanism* to be cultivated. They see chokepoint power as inherently destabilizing, not stabilizing. The IPT needs to explain why Farrell and Newman are right about the problem but wrong about the solution -- or rather, why their proposed solution (arms control) is necessary but insufficient, and the deeper solution is structural (making the supply chain itself the peace mechanism).
- Their argument that "the United States is discovering what it is like to have others do unto it" implies that chokepoint power invites retaliation and escalation, not collective defense. This is the Copeland dynamic -- negative expectations about future access triggering countermeasures -- which the IPT must address head-on.
- The article's implicit pessimism about the trajectory (economic weaponization spiraling out of control) cuts against the IPT's optimism about chokepoints as peace guarantors.

**Critical distinction the IPT must make:**
Farrell and Newman analyze chokepoints being used for *selective denial* (sanctions, export controls -- "I cut you off but keep the network running for everyone else"). The IPT's deterrence logic depends on the threat of *total destruction* (invading Taiwan destroys the fabs for everyone, including the attacker). These are categorically different scenarios. Selective denial is an offensive weapon. Total destruction is a collective defense trigger. The IPT must make this distinction explicitly and argue that Farrell & Newman's framework, while correct about selective denial, does not address the deterrence logic of total destruction.

## SOURCE 3: Underground Empire (2023)

**Full Citation:**
Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman, *Underground Empire: How America Weaponized the World Economy* (New York: Henry Holt/Macmillan, 2023).

**URL:** https://us.macmillan.com/books/9781250840554/undergroundempire/

**Tier:** Tier 2 -- Strong
> Justification: Trade press book from a major publisher by the leading scholars on the topic. Received wide attention including a Paul Krugman review in *Foreign Affairs*, LA Review of Books review, and extensive policy discussion. Not peer-reviewed in the journal sense, but builds directly on their peer-reviewed work.

### Core Argument

Underground Empire is the book-length elaboration of the weaponized interdependence thesis. The key insight: the infrastructure that allows international exchange -- financial networks, internet protocols, semiconductor supply chains, cloud platforms -- was largely built by private actors pursuing efficiency and profit. These networks were not designed as instruments of state power. But their hub-and-spoke topology created chokepoints that the US government discovered and exploited, first accidentally (SWIFT post-9/11) and then systematically (Iran sanctions, Huawei restrictions, export controls).

### Key Arguments

**1. The Accidental Empire:**
The "underground empire" was not designed by the US government. The infrastructure of globalization -- SWIFT, dollar clearing, internet backbone, semiconductor design tools -- was built by market actors. "Weaponised interdependence is not a system or policy designed by the US government. Instead, this infrastructure was largely built by accident, though it was eventually ideologized as a pathway to a Hayekian dreamworld of borderless free flowing money and information outside government control." The US discovered it could weaponize this infrastructure only after the infrastructure already existed.

**2. The SWIFT Origin Story:**
After 9/11, the Treasury Department realized that SWIFT data could reveal financial links to terrorist networks. SWIFT, based in Belgium, had previously resisted government access. But post-9/11, it acquiesced. This gave the US "an unprecedented real-time look into clandestine financial transactions." Within a year, the US used this access against Iran's nuclear program, creating a playbook for sanctions enforcement.

**3. Semiconductor Chokepoints:**
The book covers the semiconductor industry extensively, focusing on TSMC and the design tool chokepoint. "The US lost its edge in manufacturing to TSMC (Taiwan) and Samsung (South Korea) but kept its lead in terms of design and manufacturing tools through companies like Qualcomm, Nvidia, Synopsys, or Cadence." The US dominates "the software used to design complex new semiconductors" -- the EDA (electronic design automation) tools without which no advanced chip can be designed.

**4. Power Without Discipline:**
"Power without discipline is dangerous, and the United States has slipped into a pattern of economic coercion as a first resort, often without thinking through the long-term consequences." The US uses its chokepoint power reflexively, which erodes the networks that give it that power in the first place.

### Reviews and Reception

**Paul Krugman (Foreign Affairs):** Reviewed the book favorably, endorsing its core argument about the hidden power structures in the global economy.
- URL: https://www.foreignaffairs.com/reviews/american-way-economic-war-paul-krugman

**LA Review of Books ("Weaponized Plumbing"):**
- URL: https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/weaponized-plumbing-on-krzysztof-pelcs-underground-empire/

**E-International Relations:**
Noted gaps: "The book deals at length with the ways the US government keeps watch over TSMC, but the authors never explain how the world's most advanced chipmaker came to find itself on a contested island 80 miles off the coast of China in the first place."
- URL: https://www.e-ir.info/2025/10/21/review-underground-empire/

**Australian Institute of International Affairs:**
- URL: https://www.internationalaffairs.org.au/australianoutlook/book-review-underground-empire-how-america-weaponized-the-world-economy/

### Relevance to the IPT

**Where Underground Empire VALIDATES the IPT:**
- Its detailed empirical work on semiconductor chokepoints provides the evidentiary foundation the IPT needs. The book demonstrates that TSMC, ASML, and EDA tool makers are genuine chokepoints with real coercive power.
- The "accidental empire" narrative is useful for the IPT: it shows that chokepoint power emerges from market dynamics (network effects, efficiency gains), not deliberate state strategy. This supports the IPT's claim that the AI supply chain's hub-and-spoke structure is a durable feature, not something that can be easily engineered away.

**Where Underground Empire CHALLENGES the IPT:**
- The "power without discipline" argument implies that chokepoint power is inherently tempting to abuse. If the US can't resist weaponizing its chokepoints offensively, this undermines the IPT's claim that chokepoints will generate collective defense rather than unilateral coercion.
- The book's TSMC coverage focuses on how the US *monitors and controls* TSMC, not on how TSMC's position *protects Taiwan*. This is the offensive vs. defensive framing gap that the IPT must bridge.

## SOURCE 4: "The New Economic Security State" (2023)

**Full Citation:**
Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman, "The New Economic Security State: How De-Risking Will Remake Geopolitics," *Foreign Affairs* 102, no. 6 (November/December 2023).

**URL:** https://www.foreignaffairs.com/united-states/economic-security-state-farrell-newman

**Farrell's page:** https://henryfarrell.net/the-new-economic-security-state-how-de-risking-will-remake-geopolitics-with-abraham-newman/

**Tier:** Tier 2 -- Strong
> Justification: Foreign Affairs article by the leading scholars. Directly precedes the 2025 FA cover story and establishes the "economic security state" concept.

### Core Argument

The US and other countries are shifting from a market-oriented to a security-oriented approach in economic policy ("de-risking"). The conventional free-trade consensus "hollowed out U.S. industry, welcomed a rising adversary (China) into free-trade arrangements, and riddled global supply chains with critical security vulnerabilities."

### Key Arguments

- Economic interdependence has created new security vulnerabilities that require a comprehensive policy response.
- "Nothing less than a transformation of the U.S. government" is necessary to ensure that security measures don't also weaken the global economy.
- The US can learn from the EU's experience in building economic security bureaucracy.
- Washington must tread carefully with sanctions and export controls: "if Washington relies too heavily on tools of coercion, miscalculations and overreactions could dangerously imperil the world's economic system."

### Relevance to the IPT

This article serves as a bridge between the 2019 theoretical paper and the 2025 cover story. Its key relevance: the warning that over-reliance on coercion could "dangerously imperil the world's economic system" is precisely the dynamic the IPT identifies. The IPT can cite this as evidence that even the architects of the weaponized interdependence framework recognize the risks of offensive chokepoint use -- strengthening the case for the IPT's alternative vision of defensive collective security.

**URL:** https://www.programmablemutter.com/p/the-enshittification-of-american

The essay can argue: "Farrell is right that American hegemonic enshittification drives allies away. The IPT offers the alternative: chokepoint power deployed for collective security rather than unilateral extraction."

## SOURCE 6: The Uses and Abuses of Weaponized Interdependence (2021)

**Full Citation:**
Daniel W. Drezner, Henry Farrell, and Abraham L. Newman, eds., *The Uses and Abuses of Weaponized Interdependence* (Washington, DC: Brookings Institution Press, 2021).

**URL:** https://www.brookings.edu/books/the-uses-and-abuses-of-weaponized-interdependence/

**Introduction chapter (free PDF):** https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/9780815738374_ch1.pdf

**Tier:** Tier 2 -- Strong
> Justification: Edited volume from Brookings Institution Press. Contains reprinted 2019 paper plus new chapters by leading IR scholars. Not a single peer-reviewed article but a curated academic volume from a top think tank press.

### Structure

The volume is organized in six sections:
1. **Theory** -- reprints the original 2019 *International Security* paper
2. **Hegemony and Revisionism** -- conditions under which great powers weaponize interdependence
   - Michael Mastanduno, "Hegemony and Fear: The National Security Determinants of Weaponized Interdependence"
   - Stacie E. Goddard, "The Road to Revisionism: How Interdependence Gives Revisionists Weapons for Change"
3. **Finance and Monetary Systems**
4. **Internet and Telecommunications** (including the Huawei/5G chapter)
5. **Energy and Weapons Systems**
   - "Weaponizing Energy Interdependence"
   - "Russia's Gazprom: A Case Study in Misused Interdependence"
   - Thomas P. Cavanna, "Weaponized Weapons: The U.S. F-35 and European Eurofighter Networks"
6. **Global South and Research Agenda**
   - Bruce W. Jentleson, "Coercion Unbound? China's Belt and Road Initiative"
   - "The Weak Suffer What They Must? The Global South in a World of Weaponized Interdependence"
   - Farrell & Newman, "Weaponized Interdependence and Networked Coercion: A Research Agenda"

### Key Findings from the Volume

**The research agenda chapter** (by Farrell & Newman) notes: "We did not anticipate the reception [the original paper] would receive or how dramatically the changes we wanted to understand would accelerate, thanks to factors including the deterioration of relations between the United States and China, the eagerness of the Donald Trump administration to deploy coercion where it could, and contestation over global supply chains in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic."

**Goddard's chapter on revisionism** is particularly relevant: it examines how revisionist actors (states seeking to change the international order) may exploit weaponized interdependence. This is relevant to the IPT's concern about potential aggressors on the supply chain.

**The Gazprom chapter** is directly relevant to the Russia-Ukraine comparison the IPT must make: it documents how Russia attempted to weaponize energy interdependence offensively, supporting the structural comparison table in the session notes.

**Victor D. Cha's collective resilience concept** (discussed in responses to the volume): Cha advocates for "collective resilience" -- a public diplomacy strategy in which allies treat economic coercion against one as an attack on all. "This shared stance deters aggressors by threatening multilateral retaliation and reinforces diplomatic solidarity through coordinated responses such as supply chain adjustments and alternative financial systems." This is essentially the institutional form of what the IPT predicts will emerge organically from supply chain dependency.

### Relevance to the IPT

The edited volume is a goldmine for the IPT because it contains:
- The Gazprom case (directly relevant to why energy interdependence failed where semiconductor interdependence might succeed)
- The Goddard chapter on revisionism (relevant to the "rogue super-node" problem)
- Cha's collective resilience concept (an institutional analog of the IPT's emergent collective defense)
- Farrell & Newman's own acknowledgment that weaponized interdependence dynamics are accelerating

## SOURCE 7: Scholarly Responses and Critiques

### 7a. J. Benton Heath, "Constructing a Global Panopticon" (2025)

**Full Citation:**
J. Benton Heath, "Constructing a Global Panopticon: Toward a Jurisprudence of Weaponized Interdependence," *Journal of International Economic Law* 28, no. 3 (2025): 505-522.

**URL:** https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=5371379

**Tier:** Tier 1 -- Gold Standard
> Justification: Peer-reviewed article in a major law journal. Provides legal analysis of the weaponized interdependence framework.

**Key Arguments:**
- Uptake of weaponized interdependence in legal literature has been "largely superficial, limited to the observation that economic interdependence [is] coming under increased strain."
- Identifies four legal processes that create conditions for weaponizing a network: "herding, enclosure, legibility, and jurisdictional politics."
- Reopens the SWIFT case study to show how law "intervenes at critical points to create the conditions for weaponization."

**Relevance to IPT:** Heath's work shows that weaponization is not just a structural feature of networks but requires active legal construction. This is relevant to the IPT because it suggests that the defensive (rather than offensive) use of chokepoints would also require deliberate institutional design -- supporting the IPT's call for a "NATO for AI" or security layer.

### 7b. "The Decoupling Dilemma" (Cambridge, 2025)

**Full Citation:**
"The Decoupling Dilemma: How US Sanctions Erode Global Economic Governance," *International Organization* (Cambridge University Press, 2025).

**URL:** https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/international-organization/article/decoupling-dilemma-how-us-sanctions-erode-global-economic-governance/8FA0868798CE6033CBAF995F0E763084

**Tier:** Tier 1 -- Gold Standard
> Justification: Peer-reviewed article in *International Organization*, a top-3 IR journal.

**Key Arguments:**

**Relevance to IPT:** This is a critical challenge. If offensive use of chokepoints erodes the interdependence itself, then the IPT's peace mechanism -- which depends on deep, durable interdependence -- is threatened by the very policies the US is currently pursuing. The IPT should cite this as evidence for why offensive weaponization is self-defeating and why the shift to collective defense is urgent.

### 7d. Critiques Noted in Wikipedia and Secondary Sources

**Critique 1 -- Democratic Accountability:**
"While Farrell and Newman effectively show how states weaponize global networks, they say little about the democratic accountability of this power." Who watches the watchers when the Treasury can secretly monitor financial flows or the NSA taps into internet infrastructure?

**Critique 2 -- The View from the Global South:**
"The primary experience of weaponized interdependence for most states is not one of coercive power, but of defensive navigation, a continuous effort to mitigate systemic vulnerabilities that they did not create and cannot control." This critique underscores that weaponized interdependence is experienced as a threat by most nations, not an opportunity -- which is relevant to the IPT's argument that collective defense of chokepoints would be broadly supported.

**Critique 3 -- Counterstrategies and Resilience:**
Research on counterstrategies to economic warfare identifies "reconstruction" -- attempts to restore access to choked-off supply lines -- as producing "a form of deterrence by denial since the aim is to limit the effectiveness of weaponising the economy and thus deter the weaponising state from engaging." This is a response *to* weaponized interdependence, not a feature of it.

**Relevance to IPT:** These critiques collectively suggest that:
1. Chokepoint power needs institutional constraints (supports IPT's call for collective security framework)
2. Most nations experience chokepoint vulnerability rather than chokepoint power (supports IPT's claim that broad coalitions would rally to defend supply chain nodes)
3. The current trajectory of offensive weaponization generates countermeasures that erode interdependence (supports IPT's argument that offensive use is self-defeating)

### 7e. China's Rare Earth Counter-Chokepoint

**Key Finding:**
China controls over 90% of rare earth element processing. Beijing weaponized this in 2010 (halting exports to Japan over the Senkaku Islands dispute) and in 2024 (new regulations restricting dual-use exports). The Trump administration made concessions on semiconductor export controls in exchange for China easing rare-earth mineral restrictions.

**The G-7 Response:**
At the 2023 Hiroshima summit, the G-7 agreed to "work together to ensure that attempts to weaponize economic dependencies by forcing G-7 members and our partners including small economies to comply and conform will fail and face consequences." This led to US-Japan-South Korea technology coordination initiatives.

**Relevance to IPT:** China's rare-earth counter-chokepoint is the best current example of the reciprocal dynamics the IPT predicts. It shows that:
1. Counter-chokepoints emerge naturally (supporting IPT's multilateral framing)
2. Mutual vulnerability creates incentives for negotiation and restraint (supporting IPT's deterrence logic)
3. Collective responses to weaponization are already forming (the G-7 commitment is an embryonic version of the collective defense the IPT envisions)

## SYNTHESIS: How the IPT Essay Should Engage Farrell & Newman

### The Strategic Framing

Farrell & Newman are both the IPT's **strongest allies** and its **most sophisticated challengers**. The essay must engage them with precision, conceding where they are right and distinguishing where the IPT offers something genuinely new. The engagement should occur primarily in **Section 3 (The Theorem)** and **Section 5 (The Copeland Problem)** of the essay.

### Three Moves the Essay Should Make

**Move 1: "Farrell & Newman confirm that chokepoints are real power"**

The IPT should open its engagement with Farrell & Newman by citing them as authority for the claim that supply chain chokepoints confer genuine coercive leverage. Their empirical work on SWIFT, Huawei, semiconductor export controls, and internet surveillance demonstrates that hub-and-spoke network topology creates real, exercisable power. The IPT builds on this foundation: if chokepoints in financial networks and internet infrastructure can be weaponized for surveillance and sanctions, then chokepoints in the AI supply chain -- which is far more critical than either finance or internet infrastructure alone -- can generate even more powerful strategic dynamics.

**Suggested passage for the essay:**
> Farrell and Newman have shown conclusively that the hub-and-spoke structure of global economic networks creates genuine coercive power -- what they call the "panopticon effect" and the "chokepoint effect." The question is not whether supply chain chokepoints confer power. It is what kind of power they confer, and to whom.

**Move 2: "But they analyze the wrong use case -- selective denial, not total destruction"**

The critical distinction the IPT must draw: Farrell & Newman analyze chokepoints being used for *selective denial* -- cutting specific actors off from network access while keeping the network running for everyone else (Iran excluded from SWIFT, Huawei excluded from US chips). This is offensive weaponization. The IPT's deterrence logic depends on a categorically different scenario: the threat of *total destruction* of a chokepoint node through military invasion. When China considers invading Taiwan, the risk is not that Taiwan will be selectively denied access to something -- it is that TSMC's fabs will be destroyed, and every nation that depends on TSMC (including China itself) loses access.

This distinction maps onto a fundamental difference in deterrence theory:
- **Selective denial** = a weapon wielded by the chokepoint controller against specific targets. This is offensive, unilateral, and generates the counter-weaponization dynamics Farrell & Newman describe.
- **Total destruction** = a threat that the chokepoint itself is destroyed, harming everyone. This is defensive, multilateral, and generates the collective security dynamic the IPT describes.

**Suggested passage for the essay:**
> Farrell and Newman show that chokepoints can be weaponized -- but the weapon they describe is a scalpel, not a bomb. Export controls and sanctions are forms of selective denial: the network keeps running, but specific actors are cut off. The deterrence that protects supplier nations operates through a different mechanism entirely. An invasion of Taiwan would not selectively deny anyone access to TSMC; it would destroy TSMC. And when the threat is total destruction of a node that everyone depends on, the rational response is not counter-weaponization but collective defense.

**Move 3: "Their policy recommendation validates the IPT's direction of travel"**

Farrell and Newman's 2025 FA argument -- that the US should move "toward detente and arms control" -- converges with the IPT's policy direction. But the IPT goes further. Farrell & Newman want arms control for economic weapons (restraining offensive use). The IPT argues for transforming the supply chain's structural properties into a collective defense mechanism. Arms control manages the symptom (offensive weaponization); the IPT addresses the root cause (making chokepoint power serve collective rather than unilateral interests).

The essay can also point to Farrell's own "enshittification" argument: if the US enshittifies its hegemonic platforms, allies will seek to escape -- eroding the interdependence that generates both American power and global stability. The IPT offers the alternative: a supply chain structure that generates collective security for everyone, including the chokepoint hosts, making the system self-reinforcing rather than self-defeating.

**Suggested passage for the essay:**
> Farrell and Newman are right that the age of unilateral weaponization must end. But their proposed remedy -- arms control for economic weapons -- treats the symptom rather than the disease. The Intelligence Peace Thesis suggests a more fundamental reorientation: chokepoint power should not be restrained but redirected. Instead of using supply chain position to coerce adversaries into compliance, the international community should recognize that supply chain position already generates collective security for the nations that host critical nodes -- and build institutions that formalize and strengthen this emergent dynamic.

For the essay's Section 3 (The Theorem), the IPT should cite:
1. **Farrell & Newman 2019** -- for the theoretical framework (panopticon/chokepoint effects, network topology) [TIER 1]

3. **Farrell 2025 "Enshittification of American Hegemony"** -- for the platform degradation dynamic [TIER 3-4, supporting color]

### The Key Sentence

If the essay has space for only one sentence engaging Farrell & Newman, it should be this:

> Farrell and Newman have demonstrated that chokepoints in global economic networks create real coercive power; the Intelligence Peace Thesis argues that in a post-AGI world, that same structural feature -- chokepoint dependency that cannot be replicated or substituted -- generates not offensive leverage for the hub controller, but collective defense for the hub host, because the threat at stake is not selective denial but total destruction.
