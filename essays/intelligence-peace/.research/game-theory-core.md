# The Core Argument: Why War Against AI Supply Chain Nations Is Irrational

> A stylised game-theoretic derivation of the Intelligence Peace Thesis. This is a prediction about the long-term post-AGI steady state, not a description of the present world.

---

## The World in the Post-AGI Steady State

Assume two conditions hold:

**Condition 1 — Existential dependency on AI.** The world has developed an existential dependency on AI services. AI mediates economic production, military capability, scientific research, governance, and critical infrastructure. Loss of AI access is not a setback — it is civilizational regression. No geopolitical gain (territorial, political, strategic) compensates for it.

**Condition 2 — Single, integrated supply chain with chokepoints.** AI services are produced through a single global supply chain. This chain has chokepoints: specific facilities in specific nations where single companies or countries dominate irreplaceable niche markets. The chain breaks if any chokepoint is destroyed.

These two conditions are all the argument requires.

---

## The Argument

There are N nations. Some are chokepoint nations (they host critical supply chain nodes). All are consumers of AI services. Consider any nation contemplating an attack on a chokepoint nation.

### Mechanism 1: Mutually Assured Destruction

If the attacker depends on the AI supply chain — and in the steady state, every nation does — then attacking a chokepoint nation destroys a link in the chain the attacker itself depends on. The attack is self-defeating.

This is not the ordinary cost of war. In ordinary war, an aggressor weighs territorial or strategic gains against military and economic costs, and sometimes the gains win. Here, the cost is the loss of AI services — which, by Condition 1, is existentially large and outweighs any conceivable gain. The attacker cannot benefit from the attack because the attack destroys what the attacker's own civilisation runs on.

This holds even if the attacker could somehow capture the chokepoint facility intact, because:
- The facility cannot operate without its global supply chain (upstream inputs, specialised consumables, skilled workforce, software dependencies)
- Other nations would immediately cut the captured facility off from the rest of the chain
- The chain is a *network*, not a single asset — capturing one node does not give you the network

### Mechanism 2: Collective Security

Every other nation that depends on the AI supply chain has an overwhelming individual incentive to defend the chokepoint nation under attack. This is not altruism or alliance solidarity — it is pure self-interest. If the chokepoint falls, every dependent nation loses AI access. The cost of losing AI access exceeds the cost of military intervention to defend it, for every dependent nation individually.

This produces collective security without the usual collective action problem:

- **No free-rider problem.** In standard collective security (NATO, the UN), each member prefers others to bear the cost of defence. Here, every dependent nation is individually harmed by the chokepoint's destruction. Defence of the supply chain is not a public good that others can provide on your behalf — it is a private necessity. Each nation has sufficient unilateral reason to act.

- **No credibility problem.** Nuclear deterrence requires the credible willingness to retaliate — which may not be credible (would France really launch nuclear weapons over Estonia?). Supply chain collective security requires only that nations act in their own material self-interest, which is always credible.

- **No coordination problem.** The incentive to defend is individually rational and does not require prior agreement, institutional machinery, or a security guarantor. Nations do not need a treaty to defend the supply chain — they need only to recognise that losing it is catastrophic.

### The Result

The combination of these two mechanisms means:

1. **The attacker is deterred by self-harm** (Mechanism 1): attacking a chokepoint destroys what the attacker needs.
2. **The attacker faces a coalition of all dependent nations** (Mechanism 2): every other country will intervene to defend the chokepoint.
3. **No rational nation attacks a chokepoint nation.** The attack fails on both the individual calculus (self-harm) and the strategic calculus (overwhelming opposition).
4. **Any nation that is a chokepoint on the AI supply chain is too important to fail.** It is protected not by its own military strength, not by alliance membership, not by geography — but by its structural position in the supply chain.

This is the Intelligence Peace: a durable, self-reinforcing equilibrium in which war against supply chain nations is irrational for all parties.

## Why This Is Not Just Another "Trade Prevents War" Argument

The IPT is often confused with the liberal peace thesis (trade interdependence promotes peace). It is fundamentally different in three ways.

**1. Existential vs. substitutable dependency.** Ordinary trade interdependence involves goods that can be sourced elsewhere, stockpiled, or done without. The cost of disruption is a recession — painful but survivable. AI dependency in the post-AGI world is existential: there is no substitute, no stockpile, no doing without. The magnitude of what is at stake is qualitatively different.

**2. Chokepoints vs. diffuse trade.** Ordinary trade is distributed across many partners and products. No single trade relationship is critical enough to generate robust deterrence. The AI supply chain has chokepoints — single points of failure where one company or nation is irreplaceable. This concentration is what generates the "too important to fail" logic. The relevant variable is not "how much trade" but "is there a single point of failure."

**3. No free-rider problem.** In the liberal peace, third-party nations are weakly affected by a bilateral trade disruption — so they have weak incentive to intervene. In the IPT, all nations depend on the *same* supply chain. An attack on a chokepoint harms everyone, not just the bilateral trading partners. This eliminates the free-rider problem and produces genuine collective security.

The standard counterexample to the liberal peace — "pre-WWI Europe was deeply interdependent and went to war anyway" — does not apply to the IPT because it fails on all three distinctions. Pre-WWI trade was substitutable (not existential), diffuse (not concentrated in chokepoints), and bilateral (third parties were not directly harmed by any specific trade disruption).

## Why This Is Stronger Than Nuclear Deterrence

Nuclear deterrence protects A from B because B fears A's retaliation. The Intelligence Peace protects a chokepoint nation from *everyone* because *everyone* — including the potential attacker — is individually harmed by the chokepoint's destruction.

| | Nuclear Deterrence | Intelligence Peace |
|---|---|---|
| **Who is deterred?** | State B, by state A's arsenal | Every state, by its own dependency |
| **Who provides the deterrent?** | The target state (must have weapons) | The supply chain structure (no weapons needed) |
| **Structure** | Bilateral (A ↔ B) | Multilateral (all dependent states) |
| **Credibility problem?** | Yes — would you really retaliate? | No — self-interest is always credible |
| **Small states?** | Need their own arsenal or a patron | Protected by structural position alone |
| **Scales with** | Arsenal size | Depth of AI dependency |

The most important difference: nuclear deterrence requires the *willingness to act* (retaliation). The Intelligence Peace requires only the *existence of dependency*. It is structural, not volitional.

## When the Argument Breaks: Failure Conditions

The argument holds if and only if both conditions hold. It fails in exactly these cases:

### Condition 1 fails: AI dependency is not existential

If AI is important but not existential — if a nation can survive losing AI access — then the cost of losing the supply chain may not exceed the geopolitical gains from attacking. This is the **present situation**: AI matters but is not yet the substrate of civilisation. The IPT does not describe the present. It describes the steady state that emerges once AI dependency becomes existential. The transition to that state is the dangerous period.

### Condition 2 fails: The single chain breaks

**Autarky.** If a nation builds a complete, independent AI supply chain, it no longer depends on the global chain. It can attack a chokepoint nation without self-harm (Mechanism 1 fails). It still faces collective opposition from all other dependent nations (Mechanism 2 holds), but the deterrence is weaker. This is why AI autarky — a second, independent supply chain — is dangerous for global peace and should be actively prevented.

**Replicability.** If destroyed chokepoints can be quickly rebuilt, the cost of disruption drops and the deterrence weakens. The argument depends on chokepoints being non-replicable on policy-relevant timescales.

**Irrationality.** If a nation's leadership does not respond to rational cost-benefit calculation, no deterrence works — nuclear, conventional, or supply chain. This is a limitation of all deterrence theory, not specific to the IPT. But note: in the IPT, the cost is *automatic* (you destroy the fabs by invading) rather than *discretionary* (someone must choose to retaliate). This makes it more robust against miscalculation than nuclear deterrence.

## The Silicon Shield: An Embryonic, Incomplete Form

The Silicon Shield protecting Taiwan today is an embryonic form of the IPT. China is deterred from invading Taiwan by the same two mechanisms:

1. **MAD:** An invasion would destroy TSMC's fabs, shattering China's own supply of advanced semiconductors.
2. **Collective security:** The US and allies, who depend on TSMC, would intervene.

But the Silicon Shield is incomplete because the two conditions are not yet fully met:
- **Condition 1 is partial:** The world depends on semiconductors but is not yet existentially dependent on AI. The cost of losing TSMC is enormous but survivable.
- **Condition 2 is eroding:** The US is actively onshoring advanced chip production (CHIPS Act, TSMC Arizona). China is pursuing semiconductor self-sufficiency. Both are weakening the single-chain structure.

The IPT generalises the Silicon Shield to the post-AGI steady state, where Condition 1 is fully met (existential dependency) and the argument for maintaining Condition 2 (single chain) is a matter of deliberate policy rather than historical accident.

**Crucially: the fact that the US and China are spending billions to escape the Silicon Shield *confirms the logic*.** Both great powers recognise the peace trap and must first reduce supply chain dependency to regain geopolitical optionality. The enormous cost and difficulty of this escape is itself evidence for how powerful the mechanism is.

## The Transition: Maximum Danger

The IPT describes a steady state. The transition to that state is the most dangerous period, because:

1. **Condition 1 is not yet met.** AI dependency is growing but not yet existential. A state might judge that the geopolitical gains from military action exceed the cost of losing AI access — a calculation that becomes impossible once AI is truly the substrate of civilisation.

2. **Nations are racing for position.** Countries are competing for seats on the post-AGI supply chain, knowing that those who are locked out may be locked out permanently. This race creates security risks and uncertainties that do not exist in the steady state.

3. **Negative expectations trigger preventive action.** If a nation believes it will be permanently excluded from the AI supply chain (through export controls, sanctions, or a "democracy-only club"), it has incentive to act *now* — while AI is not yet existential and while it still has military options — rather than wait until the steady state locks in and it has no leverage. This is the most dangerous dynamic: exclusionary policies create the very insecurity they aim to prevent.

The policy implication: **during the transition, keep potential adversaries integrated into the supply chain rather than excluding them.** Offensive weaponisation of supply chain chokepoints (export controls designed to permanently lock out rivals) is the most dangerous policy, because it generates incentives for preventive action before the peaceful steady state can emerge.

## Implications

If the core argument is sound, several striking consequences follow:

**1. Geography and population become irrelevant to national security.** The Netherlands (17 million people) is more strategically important than most large nations because ASML is a chokepoint. In the post-AGI steady state, small nations with chokepoint facilities will be safer than large military powers without them.

**2. Nations facing security threats should join the supply chain.** If hosting a chokepoint generates collective security, then becoming indispensable to the AI supply chain is a rational national security strategy. This is the IPT playbook: not deterrence through weapons, but deterrence through indispensability.

**3. The peace mechanism strengthens over time.** As AI dependency deepens (Condition 1 strengthens), the cost of disruption grows and the incentive to defend chokepoint nations grows with it. Unlike military alliances (which erode) or institutions (which decay), this mechanism becomes more robust as the dependency deepens. It is a self-reinforcing equilibrium.

**4. Autarky is the enemy of peace.** A second, independent supply chain allows a major power to attack without self-harm. Policies that push nations toward AI autarky (aggressive export controls, permanent exclusion) weaken the peace mechanism. The single-chain structure is not just economically efficient — it is a security asset.

**5. Inclusion, not exclusion, is the security-maximising strategy.** The counter-intuitive conclusion: keeping adversaries dependent on the supply chain makes the world safer, not more dangerous. Excluding them eliminates the structural incentive that restrains them and drives them toward autarky.

**6. Autocratic lock-in is a real cost.** Once an autocracy is on the AI supply chain, the world has incentive to keep it stable — locking in its political system. The period before the steady state may be the last window for political regime change in countries that have not yet joined the supply chain.

---

## The Argument in One Sentence

In a post-AGI world where all nations depend on the same irreplaceable AI supply chain, war against supplier nations is irrational for every party: the attacker destroys what it needs to survive, and every other dependent nation will intervene to prevent that destruction.
