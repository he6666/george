## 1. Supply Chain Concentration Data

### ASML: The EUV Monopoly

**Market Position (2024-2025):**
- ASML holds **100% monopoly on EUV lithography** -- no other company in the world produces EUV machines (Nikon and Canon only make older DUV/KrF/i-line systems)
- ASML commands **~83% of worldwide lithography machine sales** and **90%+ of the advanced lithography market** (2025)
- 2024 record revenue: EUR 28.3 billion in net sales, EUR 7.6 billion net profit
- Q1 2025: net sales up 46% YoY to EUR 7.74 billion; net income up 92%
- 2025 full-year guidance: EUR 30-35 billion in net sales

**EUV Machine Specifications:**
- Standard EUV system (Twinscan NXE series): ~$200 million per unit
- **High-NA EUV (Twinscan EXE:5200B): ~$350-380 million per unit**
- Each machine contains **100,000+ components** (some sources cite 700,000+ individual parts; a car has ~30,000)
- Weight: **~180 tons** (High-NA is even larger, described as "the size of a double decker bus")
- ASML produces only ~15% of parts in-house; relies on **5,150+ supplier companies globally**

**Non-Replicability Evidence (Critical for Axiom 3):**
- EUV development began in the **1980s**; ASML began serious R&D in the **1990s**
- ASML invested **over EUR 6 billion in EUV R&D over 17+ years** (some accounts say 30 years total development)
- ASML spends **15-20% of annual revenue** on R&D
- Carl Zeiss SMT (Germany) is the **sole supplier** of EUV mirror optics -- took **15 years to develop**; ASML owns 24.9% stake (acquired for EUR 1 billion)
- Zeiss mirrors are "some of the flattest surfaces ever made by humans" -- deviations measured in fractions of a nanometer
- **No country or company has replicated this capability.** China's best domestic lithography (SMEE) produces i-line equipment at the 90nm node -- roughly **15-20 years behind ASML's cutting edge**

**High-NA EUV Status (2025-2026):**
- Intel has installed first commercial High-NA EUV (EXE:5200B) at D1X facility in Oregon (late 2025)
- TSMC expected to receive High-NA systems for 2nm production
- ASML has "10 to 20" orders; plans to build 20 per year by 2028
- Volume production inflection point: **2026-2027**

### TSMC: Advanced Logic Dominance

**Overall Foundry Market Share:**
- TSMC: **64% in 2024**, climbing to **~66-67% in 2025** (Q4 2024: 67.1%)
- Samsung: **~12%** (second place)
- All others: small single-digit shares

**Advanced Node Dominance (Critical for Axiom 2):**
- TSMC holds **~90% market share at 3nm and 5nm nodes**
- Taiwan produces **60% of all global semiconductors** and **90%+ of the most advanced chips**
- Advanced nodes (7nm and below) generate **over 56% of total foundry revenues** in 2025
- 3nm projected revenue: ~$30 billion; 5/4nm: ~$40 billion (2025)

**Advanced Packaging (CoWoS) -- Another Chokepoint:**
- TSMC CoWoS capacity: ~75-80K wafers/month (2025), targeting 120-130K by end of 2026
- Global CoWoS demand: 370K wafers (2024) -> 670K (2025) -> 1 million (2026)
- **CoWoS is fully booked through 2025 and into 2026**
- NVIDIA secures **60%+ of total CoWoS capacity**
- TSMC outsourcing 240-270K wafers annually to OSAT partners (Amkor, SPIL)

**2nm Technology:**
- TSMC entering 2nm mass production in H2 2025 at Hsinchu and Kaohsiung fabs
- Arizona Fab 2 targeting 2nm production by 2027

### HBM Memory Concentration (Samsung/SK Hynix)

**HBM Market Share (2025):**
- SK Hynix: **54-62%** (depending on quarter; 54% full-year 2024, peaked at 62% in Q2 2025)
- Samsung: **17-39%** (dropped from 41% in Q2 2024 to 17% in Q2 2025 due to NVIDIA qualification failures)
- Micron: **7-21%** (rising)
- SK Hynix overtook Samsung in annual operating profit for the first time in 2025

**Concentration Risk:** South Korea (Samsung + SK Hynix) produces **90%+ of global HBM** -- another critical AI supply chain chokepoint

### Fab Cost and Construction Data

**Costs:**
- Leading-edge fab (3nm capable): **$15-20 billion** (in Taiwan/Asia)
- In the United States: **~$40 billion** (roughly 2x Taiwan cost)
- TSMC total Arizona investment: **$165 billion** (for 6 fabs + packaging + R&D)
- ASML High-NA EUV machine alone: **$350-380 million per unit**

**Timelines:**
- Taiwan: fabs often built and operational within **~2 years**
- United States: significantly longer -- **3-5+ years** typical
- Micron's New York fab: originally planned for end-2025, now delayed to Q2 2026 groundwork
- Intel Ohio: first fab delayed until **2030**

**Workforce:**
- Advanced fabs require **3,000-5,000 engineers** per facility
- TSMC Arizona: 6,000 jobs created so far
- Chronic shortage of semiconductor engineers globally

### Global Semiconductor Industry Scale

- 2024 global revenue: **$627.6 billion** (up 19.1% from 2023)
- 2025 global revenue: **$791.7 billion** (up 25.6%)
- 2026 projected: **~$1 trillion**
- Logic products: $301.9 billion (2025) -- largest category
- Memory: $223.1 billion (2025)

## 2. Silicon Shield Assessments

### Pro-Silicon Shield Arguments

**The Diplomat -- "Silicon Shield 2.0: A Taiwan Perspective" (September 2024)**
- Author frames an updated Silicon Shield concept that goes beyond simple deterrence
- Taiwan's semiconductor dominance has become its "most critical geopolitical asset"
- Taiwanese government pursuing "Silicon Fortress" vision: maintain 40% of global foundry market share by 2030
- TSMC's most advanced chips will continue to be fabricated in Taiwan even as global expansion proceeds
- The new Silicon Shield 2.0 is about maintaining **irreplaceability**, not just market share

**Astute Group -- "Silicon Shield 2.0: Taiwan's Semiconductor Strategy for Deterrence" (2025)**
- Taiwan's foundries have cornered **78% of global market share** and **92% of manufacturing capacity for the most advanced chips**
- Taiwan exports 93.4% of its semiconductors to surrounding countries
- China alone purchased 53.8% of Taiwanese semiconductor exports in 2023 (though declining since 2020)

**TSMC as Enduring Shield (Taiwan Insight, May 2025)**
- Despite $100 billion+ US investment, TSMC will keep most advanced production in Taiwan
- Taiwan retains the human capital, institutional knowledge, and supply chain ecosystem advantages

### Anti-Silicon Shield Arguments

**Defense Priorities -- "Semiconductors Are Not a Reason to Defend Taiwan"**
*Tier 2 -- Established think tank*

This is the **strongest version of "the Silicon Shield is overstated"** and makes three key claims:

1. **Weak incentive for invasion:** China cannot successfully absorb Taiwan's chip industry and continue production -- it lacks upstream inputs from the US/allies. Capture of TSMC would be a "hollow prize."

2. **Weak deterrent against conflict:** Beijing may accept enormous economic costs if national reunification is at risk. If China behaves rationally, it will simply wait until achieving self-sufficiency. If it is willing to accept catastrophic costs, semiconductors won't deter it.

3. **Wrong framing for US policy:** The danger of losing semiconductor access in a Taiwan conflict is outweighed by the far more severe costs of great-power war. Semiconductors shouldn't drive US defense commitments. Additionally, Taiwan's belief in the Silicon Shield has **contributed to its low defense spending** despite growing threats.

**SSRN Academic Paper -- Tim Horster, "Silicon Shield or Silicon Trap?" (May 2025)**
*Tier 1 -- Academic/peer-review track*

- Uses deterrence theory to argue Taiwan's semiconductor centrality creates **both security and vulnerability**
- US diversification efforts and Chinese perceptions of a "closing window" may transform the asset into a **catalyst for conflict** rather than a deterrent
- "While interdependence has thus far preserved peace, shifting geopolitical conditions...may transform this asset into a catalyst for conflict"

**CFR Task Force Report -- US-Taiwan Relations in a New Era (2024-2025)**
*Tier 1 -- Major institutional report*

- "Although China's reliance on chips manufactured in Taiwan would raise the costs of a conflict, **it does not act as a 'silicon shield' that can protect Taiwan from an attack**"
- If the US successfully onshores production, it "may no longer care about Taiwan's security"
- 60% of Taiwanese respondents did not see the US as a trustworthy country (up 10% from 2024)

**ISDP -- "The Silicon Shield Erosion" (2025)**
*Tier 2 -- European think tank*

- Argues the shield is eroding as production disperses
- TSMC's global expansion weakens Taiwan's unique leverage

**Stimson Center -- "Why Taiwan Fears 'America First' Risks Eroding Its Silicon Shield" (2025)**
*Tier 2 -- Established think tank*

- US Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick proposed a **50-50 split** in advanced chip production (US/Taiwan)
- TSMC's $100B+ US investment came with **no security guarantees for Taiwan**
- $100K H-1B visa fee makes it harder for TSMC to staff Arizona fabs
- Narrative in Taiwan that TSMC is becoming "effectively an American company"
- "Washington's demands are shaking Taiwan's confidence in the United States"

**Matthew Pottinger (2022):** Challenged Silicon Shield, arguing China "does not behave in ways which appear rational to audiences in democratic countries"

**MIT Technology Review -- "Taiwan's Silicon Shield Could Be Weakening" (August 2025)**
*Tier 3 -- Quality journalism*

- Taiwan's opposition party has accused the DPP of "gambling with the future of the island"
- TSMC's investments in US came with no guarantees for Taiwan in return
- "It remains to be seen whether TSMC's shift to the US will ultimately protect or weaken Taiwan"

**Georgetown CSET -- "Taiwan's Silicon Shield Is Turning Into a Target" (2025)**
- The shield may paradoxically make Taiwan a **more attractive** target rather than a deterrent

### Synthesis: The Essay's Best Framing

The evidence strongly supports the essay's position that the Silicon Shield is **"embryonic and incomplete."** The data shows:

1. The **economic deterrence is real** -- no rational actor would want to destroy TSMC's fabs and lose access to 90% of advanced chips
2. But the **political deterrence is fragile** -- it may not stop an irrational or desperate actor, and US onshoring may erode it
3. The essay's Intelligence Peace Thesis represents a **more robust version** of the Silicon Shield argument because it focuses on the **structural impossibility of replication** (Axiom 3) rather than just economic cost

## 3. China's Self-Sufficiency Progress

### SMIC: Actual Capabilities

**Current Process Technology:**
- SMIC achieved **7nm-class (N+2)** production using DUV multi-patterning (the Huawei Kirin 9000s chip, 2023)
- SMIC's latest: **N+3 process** (marketed as "5nm-class") used in Huawei Kirin 9030 (December 2025)
- **Critical caveat:** Tom's Hardware and PhoneArena assessments note N+3 is "not a true generational leap, but an incremental stretch of SMIC's existing 7nm-class technology, with front-end scaling largely exhausted"
- BusinessKorea reports: "Huawei's 5nm Chip Actually 7nm" -- the marketing vs. reality gap is significant

**Yield Challenges:**
- SMIC's "5nm" yield: estimated at **20-50%** (industry sources via Taiwanese media)
- Viable mass production typically requires **70%+ yield**
- DUV multi-patterning at these nodes requires extreme precision; "BEOL yield can collapse abruptly once overlay and variability budgets are exceeded"
- Compare: TSMC Arizona 4nm yields at **92%** (4% higher than Taiwan)

**Financial Health:**
- 2024 gross margin: **18%** (low for the industry)
- Net margin: **9.1%**
- Government subsidies of **$411 million accounted for 87% of SMIC's 2024 operating profit**
- SMIC implemented aggressive **40% price cut** for 28nm process (January 2025) -- suggests overcapacity at mature nodes

### Huawei/HiSilicon Kirin Chips

**Kirin 9030 (December 2025):**
- Powers Mate 80 series and Mate X7 foldable
- Manufactured on SMIC N+3
- Performance falls short of comparable 5nm chips from TSMC
- Represents a workaround, not a breakthrough -- cannot match EUV-produced chips in power efficiency or transistor density

**Key Limitation:** Without EUV, Huawei/SMIC face a hard ceiling. Each DUV multi-patterning step adds cost, reduces yield, and increases defect risk. The gap between SMIC's best and TSMC's best is **at least 2 full nodes** and arguably more in practical performance terms.

### China's EUV Development Efforts

**Prototype Status (late 2025):**
- China has reportedly built an **EUV prototype** in a high-security Shenzhen laboratory
- The machine can generate EUV light but **has not yet produced functional chips**
- Huawei is coordinating development across companies and state research institutes, involving thousands of engineers
- Two parallel teams working on EUV light sources (solid-state laser approach, different from ASML's CO2 laser)
- Target: chip production by **2028** (analysts say **2030 is more realistic**)

**CSIS Assessment -- "Breakthroughs or Boasts?" (2025):**
- SMEE (Shanghai Micro Electronics Equipment) produces only **i-line and some DUV tools**
- SMEE holds only **4% of the global market** (and only in legacy i-line equipment)
- China's much-publicized e-beam lithography machine (Xizhi) is **unsuitable for high-volume manufacturing** -- rasters point-by-point, far too slow for production
- **"Announcements of Chinese breakthroughs in chipmaking should be treated with caution. They project ambition but do not fundamentally alter the strategic reality"**
- Two reasons for skepticism: Chinese R&D funding incentive structures, and central-local policy signaling aimed at both internal and external audiences

**Critical Gap:** China still **cannot produce the ultra-precision optical systems** needed for real EUV production. Zeiss's monopoly on EUV optics represents a separate, equally formidable barrier to replication.

### Big Fund III

**Scale:** RMB 344 billion (~**$47.5 billion**), launched May 2024
- Larger than Big Fund I ($21.4B) and Big Fund II ($28.9B) combined
- Duration: **15 years** (through 2039) -- acknowledging the long-term nature of the challenge
- Focus has shifted from fabrication capacity to **supply chain bottlenecks**: lithography tools, inspection systems, etching platforms, EDA software, photoresists, specialty gases, wafer materials
- Additional AI-specific fund: RMB 60 billion (~$8.2B) launched January 2025

**Honest Assessment:** Big Fund III is targeting the right chokepoints, but the scale of the challenge is enormous. ASML alone invested EUR 6B+ over 17 years just for EUV. China is throwing $47.5B at the **entire semiconductor equipment and materials ecosystem simultaneously** -- spread across lithography, etching, deposition, inspection, materials, and more. The per-problem investment is substantial but may not be sufficient for the most challenging areas (particularly lithography optics).

### DeepSeek: Implications for Compute Dependency

**Key Facts:**
- DeepSeek trained its models using **NVIDIA chips** (not Chinese-made chips)
- Claimed training cost: **<$6 million using ~2,000 chips** (vs. Meta: 16,000+ chips)
- Innovations were **algorithmic/architectural** (mixture-of-experts, etc.), not hardware-based
- DeepSeek has since launched models with native support for Chinese chips (Huawei CANN, Cambricon, Hygon) -- but primarily for **inference**, not training

**CSIS Assessment:**
- DeepSeek's success reflects the "lagging impact" of flawed October 2022 export controls
- "The amount of compute still matters" -- efficiency gains enable more with less, but don't eliminate the need for advanced hardware
- Senior executives at key Chinese AI firms explicitly stated **"lack of access to advanced AI chips is the most significant challenge they face"**
- The computing resource disparity is **"almost certainly the largest single advantage that the United States enjoys over China"**
- Greater long-term concern: China developing a **domestic ecosystem for producing its own AI chips at scale**

**Implications for the Essay:**
DeepSeek demonstrates that software innovation can partially compensate for hardware restrictions, but **does not eliminate the hardware dependency**. If anything, it shows that the compute chokepoint is real -- Chinese firms are expending enormous ingenuity to work around it precisely because they cannot replicate the hardware supply chain. This supports Axiom 2.

### Overall China Self-Sufficiency Assessment

**Equipment Self-Sufficiency Rate (2024):** ~13.6% (TrendForce)
- Target of 50% by 2025 appears unrealistic
- Original "Made in China 2025" targets (70% self-sufficiency by 2025, 100% by 2030) have been **effectively abandoned**
- Strong progress in: etching, cleaning, photoresist stripping, CMP equipment (double-digit self-sufficiency)
- Weak areas: **metrology, coating/developing, lithography, ion implantation** -- the most critical technologies

**Honest Bottom Line:**
China is making real but **incremental progress** at mature nodes (28nm and above). At the leading edge (sub-7nm), China remains **fundamentally dependent** on Western/allied technology:
- Cannot produce EUV lithography machines (prototype only, years from production)
- Cannot produce EUV-quality optics
- Cannot match TSMC yields even with multi-patterning workarounds
- Government subsidies prop up financially unsustainable operations
- The gap at the cutting edge is **not closing** -- it may be widening as TSMC/ASML advance to 2nm/High-NA

## 4. CHIPS Act & Diversification Progress

### TSMC Arizona

**Fab 1 (Operational):**
- **Now in high-volume production** of 4nm chips (started early 2025)
- Clients include Apple and NVIDIA
- Yield rate: **92%** (4% higher than comparable Taiwan fabs per Commerce Secretary Raimondo)
- Capacity: ~10,000 wafer starts per month (rumored)
- CHIPS Act funding: **$6.6 billion**

**Fab 2 (Under Construction):**
- Construction complete; equipment installation underway for **3nm and 2nm** production
- Production target: **2027**
- Equipment installation reportedly **months ahead of schedule**

**Fab 3 (Groundbreaking):**
- Broke ground in 2025
- Will produce **2nm or more advanced** processes
- Production: **by end of decade**

**Total TSMC US Investment:** **$165 billion** (initially $65B for 3 fabs, expanded by $100B for 3 more fabs + advanced packaging + R&D center)
- Announced by President Trump and TSMC CEO C.C. Wei on March 3, 2025
- Described as "largest single foreign direct investment in U.S. history"
- Total planned: **6 fabs, 2 advanced packaging facilities, 1 R&D center**

### Intel

**Fab 52 (Arizona -- Ocotillo/Chandler):**
- Entered high-volume manufacturing using **Intel 18A (1.8nm-class) process**
- First US-based facility to surpass the 2nm threshold
- Yields: **65-70%** as of December 2025 (below the 70-80% profitability threshold)
- CHIPS Act funding: **$7.86 billion**
- Key customer: Microsoft (confirmed); NVIDIA and Broadcom testing stalled due to yield/performance concerns

**Intel 18A Challenges:**
- Delayed twice -- from late 2025 to mid-2026 for full HVM ramp
- Yields at 55-65% -- insufficient for high-volume manufacturing
- NVIDIA and Broadcom evaluations have **stalled** due to performance/yield shortfalls
- External foundry revenue not expected in significant volumes until 2026

**Intel Ohio:**
- **Delayed until 2030** due to strategic capital management and labor constraints
- Originally planned for much earlier production

### Samsung (Taylor, Texas)

- CHIPS Act funding: **$4.75 billion**
- Focused on **2nm Gate-All-Around (GAA)** technology
- Mass production pushed to **late 2026**
- Has secured "anchor" AI customers

### Other CHIPS Act Projects

- **Micron (New York):** $100B megafab; groundwork delayed from end-2025 to Q2 2026
- Total CHIPS Act funding allocated across major projects: ~$19.2 billion (Intel + TSMC + Samsung)

### Diversification Assessment

**Progress Is Real But Slow:**
- TSMC Arizona Fab 1 is a genuine success -- **operational, high yields, producing for major clients**
- But current US capacity is a **tiny fraction** of Taiwan's output (10K wafers/month vs. TSMC's total of ~2M+ wafers/month across all nodes)
- Building in the US costs **~2x more** and takes **~2x longer** than in Taiwan
- Workforce shortage is a persistent constraint
- TSMC will keep the **most advanced nodes** in Taiwan (2nm production started in Taiwan in H2 2025; Arizona Fab 2 won't have 2nm until 2027)

**Critical Timeline Gap:**
Even with aggressive expansion, the US won't have meaningful advanced chip production independence for **5-10+ years**. If a Taiwan contingency occurred in 2026-2028, the loss of TSMC's Taiwan fabs would be catastrophic regardless of Arizona operations.

## 7. Assessment: How Strong Is the Evidence?

### Axiom 2: Frontier AI Requires Hardware Producible Only at a Small Number of Irreplaceable Locations

**Evidence Strength: VERY STRONG**

The data overwhelmingly supports this axiom:
- ASML's 100% EUV monopoly is undisputed fact
- TSMC's ~90% advanced logic share is well-documented by multiple independent sources
- HBM concentration in South Korea (90%+ Samsung/SK Hynix) adds another chokepoint
- CoWoS advanced packaging is an additional bottleneck (fully booked, TSMC-dominated)
- These are not just market share figures -- they reflect genuine technical monopolies that no competitor can currently challenge

**Potential Weakness:** The essay should note that "irreplaceable" is strongest for EUV lithography and weakest for mature-node chips. China is making real progress at 28nm+ nodes. The chokepoint argument is specifically about **frontier AI hardware**, not all chips.

### Axiom 3: These Locations Cannot Be Quickly Replicated

**Evidence Strength: STRONG**

Multiple independent data points confirm non-replicability:
- ASML's 17-30 year EUV R&D timeline with EUR 6B+ investment
- Zeiss optics: sole supplier, 15 years to develop, surfaces flat to fractions of a nanometer
- Fab construction: 2-5+ years, $15-40B+ per facility
- TSMC Arizona: even with $165B and full TSMC cooperation, only one fab operational after 5 years; most advanced nodes still 2+ years away
- China's best lithography (SMEE) is ~15-20 years behind; EUV prototype targets 2028-2030 at earliest
- China's equipment self-sufficiency: 13.6% overall

**Potential Weakness:**
- China's Big Fund III ($47.5B over 15 years) is a serious investment, and breakthroughs cannot be ruled out
- The EUV prototype in Shenzhen, while far from production-ready, represents faster-than-expected progress
- Some analysts argue that **with unlimited money and political will**, China could potentially close the gap in 10-15 years (though not "quickly" by any reasonable definition)
- TSMC Arizona's success (92% yield) shows that replication **is possible** given sufficient resources and cooperation from the incumbent -- it's just very slow

### Axiom 5: Destroying Supply Chain Nodes Harms Everyone Including the Attacker

**Evidence Strength: STRONG**

- China purchased 53.8% of Taiwanese semiconductor exports in 2023
- Global semiconductor industry: $791.7B (2025), projected $1T (2026)
- Even Defense Priorities (the strongest Silicon Shield critics) **concedes this point** -- they argue that capturing TSMC would be a "hollow prize" because production couldn't continue without Western inputs
- DeepSeek's innovations were driven precisely by compute scarcity -- demonstrating that lack of chips is genuinely constraining

**Potential Weakness:**
- Defense Priorities' key counterargument: if a state is determined enough (nationalism, security imperatives), it may accept economic devastation. The "rational actor" assumption may not hold.
- Matthew Pottinger's point: "China does not behave in ways which appear rational to audiences in democratic countries"
- The "Silicon Trap" theory argues that China might see a **closing window** and act before the shield becomes irrelevant (via US onshoring) or before its own self-sufficiency reduces the cost

### Overall Assessment for the Essay

The evidence strongly supports the core thesis that semiconductor supply chain concentration creates powerful collective security incentives. The data is especially compelling for the **structural/technical** arguments (Axioms 2 and 3) and somewhat less definitive for the **behavioral/deterrence** arguments (Axiom 5's practical effectiveness).

**Recommended Framing:**
The essay should present the Silicon Shield as providing **necessary but not sufficient** conditions for peace -- the economic incentives are real and powerful, but they require institutional mechanisms (the "Intelligence Peace" framework) to translate structural interdependence into reliable deterrence. This is precisely what distinguishes the essay's thesis from the simpler Silicon Shield argument: the Silicon Shield demonstrates the raw material (chokepoints create mutual vulnerability), while the Intelligence Peace Thesis describes the institutional architecture needed to make that vulnerability function as stable deterrence.

**Key Data Gaps:**

4. How many NVIDIA H100/A100 chips China actually has (estimates range from 100K to 1M+)

**Evidence Quality Note:** Most data comes from Tier 2-3 sources (think tanks, industry analysts, quality journalism). Peer-reviewed academic literature on Silicon Shield is limited but growing (Horster 2025 is the most rigorous recent contribution). Financial data (ASML, TSMC, SIA) is highly reliable. Chinese capability assessments should be treated with appropriate uncertainty -- there is incentive to both overstate (Chinese sources) and understate (Western hawk sources) progress.
