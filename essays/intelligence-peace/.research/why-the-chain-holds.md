## 1. Trade Theory Applied to Semiconductors & AI

### 1.1 Comparative Advantage in the Semiconductor Supply Chain

The semiconductor supply chain is one of the most geographically specialized industrial ecosystems ever created. Different countries dominate different segments not by accident but because of deep, path-dependent comparative advantages:

- **United States**: Chip design (fabless model), EDA software tools, advanced R&D. US firms (Qualcomm, Nvidia, AMD, Apple, Broadcom) captured ~47% of global semiconductor revenue as of 2023, overwhelmingly through design rather than fabrication.
  - *Source: SIA (Semiconductor Industry Association) annual factbook data, 2023-2024.* **Tier 2** [VERIFY for latest year]

- **Taiwan (TSMC)**: Leading-edge fabrication. TSMC alone manufactured ~90% of the world's most advanced chips (sub-7nm) as of 2023.
  - *Source: Multiple — frequently cited figure from Trendforce, SIA, and Chris Miller's "Chip War." The 90% figure is widely reported.* **Tier 2/3**

- **Netherlands (ASML)**: Monopoly on EUV lithography equipment. ASML is the sole manufacturer of extreme ultraviolet lithography machines, without which sub-7nm chips cannot be manufactured.
  - *Source: ASML corporate disclosures; widely reported. See also Miller, Chris, "Chip War" (2022).* **Tier 2/3**

- **Japan**: Critical materials (photoresists, silicon wafers, specialty chemicals). Japanese firms supply ~56% of semiconductor materials globally. Key companies include Shin-Etsu (silicon wafers, ~30% global share), JSR, Tokyo Ohka Kogyo (photoresists), and SUMCO.
  - *Source: METI (Japan Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry) data; SEMI industry reports.* **Tier 2** [VERIFY exact share]

- **South Korea (Samsung)**: Memory chips (DRAM, NAND). Samsung and SK Hynix together hold ~70%+ of global DRAM market share.
  - *Source: Trendforce, IC Insights market share data.* **Tier 2**

- **Germany (Zeiss, Trumpf)**: Optical systems and laser sources critical to ASML's EUV machines.
  - *Source: ASML supplier disclosures; Miller, "Chip War."* **Tier 2/3**

**Why this specialization exists — comparative advantage analysis:**

The economics literature identifies several reinforcing factors:

1. **Ricardian comparative advantage** (factor endowments): Taiwan's initial advantage in fabrication arose from a deliberate industrial policy (Morris Chang founding TSMC in 1987 with government backing) combined with a large, well-trained engineering workforce willing to work in the intensive shift patterns that fabs require. Labor cost differentials were significant in the 1990s-2000s, though they have narrowed.

2. **Increasing returns to scale and learning-by-doing**: Semiconductor fabrication exhibits extreme learning curves. Each successive node requires cumulative knowledge from the previous one. TSMC's advantage is not merely capital — it is the accumulated tacit knowledge of hundreds of thousands of engineers over 35+ years of continuous process improvement. This is a classic example of what economists call **dynamic increasing returns**.
   - *Source: Arthur, W. Brian, "Increasing Returns and Path Dependence in the Economy" (1994), University of Michigan Press.* **Tier 1** (seminal academic work on increasing returns)
   - *Source: Krugman, Paul, "Increasing Returns and Economic Geography," Journal of Political Economy, 1991.* **Tier 1** (peer-reviewed, foundational new trade theory)

3. **Agglomeration effects**: The clustering of related firms, specialized labor pools, and supporting industries in specific locations (Hsinchu Science Park in Taiwan, Silicon Valley for design, Eindhoven for ASML's ecosystem) creates self-reinforcing advantages that are extremely difficult to replicate.
   - *Source: Marshall, Alfred, "Principles of Economics" (1890) — original theoretical framework for industrial districts. Modern application: Saxenian, AnnaLee, "Regional Advantage: Culture and Competition in Silicon Valley and Route 128" (1994), Harvard University Press.* **Tier 1**

### 1.2 Is Semiconductor Manufacturing a Natural Monopoly / Winner-Take-Most Market?

**Yes, at the leading edge, it demonstrates strong winner-take-most dynamics:**

- **Capital intensity**: A single leading-edge fab costs $15-20 billion (as of 2024). TSMC's total capital expenditure in 2024 alone was approximately $28-32 billion. Only three companies globally (TSMC, Samsung, Intel) have even attempted to manufacture at the leading edge (sub-5nm), and Intel has fallen significantly behind. [VERIFY latest capex figures]
  - *Source: TSMC quarterly earnings reports; IC Insights capital expenditure surveys.* **Tier 2**

- **Yield as moat**: The critical competitive variable is yield — the percentage of functional chips per wafer. TSMC's yields at new nodes consistently outperform competitors. Higher yields mean lower per-unit costs, which fund further R&D investment, creating a virtuous cycle. This is a textbook example of what Sutton (1991) calls **endogenous sunk costs** creating natural oligopoly.
  - *Source: Sutton, John, "Sunk Costs and Market Structure" (1991), MIT Press.* **Tier 1** (peer-reviewed academic monograph)

- **EDA software duopoly**: Electronic design automation is dominated by Synopsys, Cadence, and Mentor (Siemens) — all US-headquartered. This is a natural oligopoly due to massive R&D costs and compatibility lock-in.
  - *Source: SIA industry data; company financial disclosures.* **Tier 2**

- **Equipment concentration**: Beyond ASML's EUV monopoly, Applied Materials, Lam Research, KLA, and Tokyo Electron dominate specific equipment categories with 50-90%+ market shares in their segments.
  - *Source: VLSI Research equipment market share data; SEMI industry reports.* **Tier 2**

### 1.3 Gains from Trade in High-Tech Industries

The economics literature strongly supports the proposition that high-tech industries generate outsized gains from trade due to:

- **Intra-industry trade in differentiated goods** (Helpman & Krugman, 1985): The semiconductor industry exemplifies the "new trade theory" prediction that countries trade in differentiated products within the same industry (US designs chips, Taiwan manufactures them, Japan supplies materials), generating welfare gains beyond classical Ricardian predictions.
  - *Source: Helpman, Elhanan, and Paul Krugman, "Market Structure and Foreign Trade" (1985), MIT Press.* **Tier 1**

- **Technology diffusion through trade**: Grossman and Helpman (1991) show that international trade facilitates technology spillovers, which increase total factor productivity growth across trading partners.
  - *Source: Grossman, Gene, and Elhanan Helpman, "Innovation and Growth in the Global Economy" (1991), MIT Press.* **Tier 1**

- **Quantitative estimates of losses from fragmentation**: The BCG/SIA report (2021) estimated that building fully self-sufficient semiconductor supply chains would require $1 trillion+ in incremental investment globally and would increase chip prices by 35-65%.
  - *Source: BCG and SIA, "Strengthening the Global Semiconductor Supply Chain in an Uncertain Era" (April 2021).* **Tier 2** [VERIFY exact figures]

## 2. Why Frontier Technology Requires Global Collaboration

### 2.1 ASML's EUV: A Machine No Single Country Can Build

ASML's EUV lithography machine is perhaps the most complex device ever manufactured. Key facts:

- **Components from ~5,000 suppliers across multiple countries**: ASML sources components from the United States, Germany, Japan, and other nations. A single EUV machine contains ~100,000 components and weighs ~180 tons. It ships in 40+ freight containers.
  - *Source: ASML corporate presentations and annual reports; widely reported in Miller, "Chip War" (2022), Scribner.* **Tier 2/3**

- **Critical subsystems by country:**
  - **Germany (Carl Zeiss SMT)**: The mirror optics system. Zeiss produces the multi-layer mirrors that focus EUV light. These mirrors are polished to sub-atomic precision — if scaled to the size of Germany, the largest imperfection would be a fraction of a millimeter. Zeiss is the sole supplier; no alternative exists.
  - **Germany (Trumpf)**: The CO2 laser system that powers the EUV light source. Trumpf supplies the high-powered industrial lasers.
  - **United States (Cymer, an ASML subsidiary)**: The light source system — tin droplets are struck by the laser to produce EUV photons. Originally a San Diego company, acquired by ASML in 2013.
  - **Japan**: Various precision components, including stages and measurement systems.
  - *Source: ASML annual report 2023; Zeiss corporate disclosures; Miller, "Chip War."* **Tier 2/3**

- **Why replication is essentially impossible in the near term**: The EUV program took approximately 20 years of development (concept to commercial viability) and billions of dollars in R&D across ASML and its partners. The tacit knowledge embedded in the supply chain cannot simply be purchased or copied. Even ASML itself cannot quickly increase production — it produces roughly 50-60 EUV machines per year and is capacity-constrained. [VERIFY latest production numbers]
  - *Source: ASML earnings calls; industry analyst reports.* **Tier 2/3**

### 2.2 TSMC's Fabrication: Foreign Input Dependencies

TSMC, despite being the world's most important manufacturer, depends critically on foreign inputs:

- **Equipment**: >80% of TSMC's equipment comes from non-Taiwanese sources: ASML (Netherlands), Applied Materials, Lam Research, KLA (US), Tokyo Electron (Japan).
  - *Source: SEMI equipment market data; TSMC supplier disclosures.* **Tier 2**

- **Materials**: Ultra-high-purity silicon wafers (Shin-Etsu and SUMCO, Japan — combined ~55-60% global share), photoresists (JSR, TOK — Japan), specialty gases (multiple US and European suppliers), CMP slurries (Cabot Microelectronics, US).
  - *Source: SEMI materials market reports; company disclosures.* **Tier 2** [VERIFY exact shares]

- **Design tools**: TSMC's process design kits (PDKs) are built to work with EDA software from Synopsys and Cadence (US). Without these tools, chip designers cannot create layouts compatible with TSMC's fabs.
  - *Source: Industry knowledge; SIA reports.* **Tier 2**

- **IP cores**: ARM (UK, now owned by SoftBank/Japan) provides the instruction set architecture used in the majority of mobile and increasingly server chips manufactured at TSMC.
  - *Source: ARM corporate data.* **Tier 2**

### 2.3 AI Research Talent: A Fundamentally Global Enterprise

- **Foreign-born researchers dominate US AI**: Georgetown's Center for Security and Emerging Technology (CSET) found that approximately **65% of top-tier AI researchers** working in the US were foreign-born (primarily from China, India, and Europe). Among AI PhD students at US universities, the figure was even higher (~70%+).
  - *Source: Zwetsloot, Remco, et al., "Keeping Top AI Talent in the United States," CSET, Georgetown University, December 2019.* **Tier 2** [VERIFY — CSET has published updated data since]

- **MacroPolо/Paulson Institute's "Global AI Talent Tracker" (2020/2023 updates)**: Found that of researchers publishing at NeurIPS (top AI conference), ~29% did their undergraduate studies in China, ~20% in the US, ~18% in Europe, and the remainder across dozens of countries. However, ~59% of these researchers *currently work* in the US, demonstrating massive talent attraction.
  - *Source: MacroPolo, Paulson Institute, "The Global AI Talent Tracker," multiple years.* **Tier 2** [VERIFY for latest edition]

- **DeepMind, OpenAI, and other frontier labs**: Their research teams are drawn from dozens of nationalities. Demis Hassabis (DeepMind founder) is British-Greek. Ilya Sutskever (former OpenAI Chief Scientist) was born in Russia, raised in Israel, trained in Canada. The Transformer paper ("Attention Is All You Need," 2017) had 8 authors from multiple national backgrounds working at Google Brain.
  - *Source: Vaswani et al., "Attention Is All You Need," NeurIPS 2017; biographical information from public sources.* **Tier 1** (for the paper) / **Tier 3** (for biographical details)

- **China's AI talent**: China produces the largest number of STEM PhD graduates globally (~77,000 STEM PhDs per year vs. ~40,000 in the US as of 2020), but many of the most accomplished go abroad. The "brain drain" dynamic has been a major concern for Chinese policymakers.
  - *Source: Georgetown CSET; NSF Science & Engineering Indicators.* **Tier 1/2** [VERIFY latest figures]

## 3. The Economics of Onshoring

### 3.1 Cost Differentials in Fab Construction

The BCG/SIA (2021) report provides the most widely cited data on regional cost differentials:

- **US fabs cost 30-50% more** than equivalent fabs in Taiwan, South Korea, or Singapore over a 10-year total cost of ownership period.
  - Drivers: higher construction labor costs, higher operating labor costs, lack of existing supply chain clustering, more expensive utilities, regulatory compliance costs.
  - *Source: BCG/SIA, "Strengthening the Global Semiconductor Supply Chain" (2021).* **Tier 2**

- **Breakdown of the cost differential (approximate)**:
  - Construction costs: 30-40% higher in the US
  - Ongoing operational costs (labor, utilities): 20-40% higher
  - Government incentives in Asia partially offset base costs (Taiwan, South Korea, and Singapore all offer significant tax breaks, subsidized land/utilities, and workforce training)
  - *Source: BCG/SIA (2021); SEMI industry analyses.* **Tier 2**

- **Europe is even more expensive** than the US for fab construction, due to stricter environmental regulations, higher energy costs, and less established semiconductor manufacturing ecosystems.
  - *Source: European Commission semiconductor strategy documents; industry analysis.* **Tier 2/3**

### 3.2 TSMC Arizona: A Case Study in Onshoring Difficulty

TSMC's Arizona fab is the single most important test case for semiconductor onshoring:

- **Original announcement**: TSMC announced its Arizona fab in May 2020, with initial plans for a $12 billion facility producing 5nm chips, operational by 2024.

- **Expansion and cost escalation**: By December 2022, TSMC announced an expansion to three fabs with total investment increasing to $40 billion. The first fab was to produce 4nm chips (N4 process), the second 3nm/2nm, and a third planned for 2nm or beyond. [VERIFY — further updates likely since May 2025]
  - *Source: TSMC press releases; multiple Tier 3 journalism sources (NYT, Bloomberg, Nikkei Asia).* **Tier 2/3**

- **Delays**: The first fab, originally scheduled for 2024, was delayed multiple times. As of early 2025, volume production was projected for 2025. Key reasons for delays:
  - Shortage of workers experienced in semiconductor fab construction (required importing thousands of Taiwanese workers, which generated labor union disputes)
  - Cultural and management frictions between TSMC's Taiwanese management style and American workers
  - Regulatory and permitting processes slower than Taiwan
  - Lower productivity of US construction labor compared to Taiwanese counterparts
  - *Source: Nikkei Asia, Bloomberg, Reuters reporting throughout 2023-2024; TSMC earnings calls.* **Tier 3** [VERIFY for latest status]

- **Cost per wafer implications**: Industry analysts estimated that chips produced at TSMC Arizona would cost 30-50% more per wafer than equivalent chips from Taiwan, even after CHIPS Act subsidies.
  - *Source: Industry analyst estimates (Bernstein, Jefferies); widely cited.* **Tier 3**

- **Mark Liu (TSMC Chairman) quote**: Reportedly stated that the company's most advanced processes would remain in Taiwan for the foreseeable future, and that Arizona would not replicate the full ecosystem.
  - *Source: Nikkei Asia interview; TSMC shareholder communications.* **Tier 3** [VERIFY exact quote]

### 3.3 Intel's Struggles with Advanced Nodes

Intel's difficulties illustrate why leading-edge fabrication cannot easily be created de novo:

- **Intel fell behind TSMC starting ~2016**: Intel's 10nm process (roughly equivalent to TSMC's 7nm) was delayed multiple times, finally entering volume production in 2019 — years behind TSMC's 7nm.

- **Intel 4 / Intel 3 / Intel 20A**: Intel attempted to leapfrog back to competitiveness through aggressive roadmapping under CEO Pat Gelsinger (2021-2024). Results were mixed — Intel 4 (equivalent to ~TSMC 5nm) entered production, but Intel continued losing market share in advanced manufacturing.

- **Intel Foundry Services**: Intel's attempt to compete with TSMC as a contract manufacturer has struggled to attract major customers, who are reluctant to trust unproven processes when TSMC's yields are consistently superior.

- **Financial distress**: By 2024-2025, Intel was reporting significant losses in its manufacturing division, had undertaken major layoffs, and was considering strategic options including separation of its design and manufacturing businesses. [VERIFY — this was rapidly evolving as of May 2025]
  - *Source: Intel quarterly earnings reports; industry reporting (Bloomberg, FT, WSJ).* **Tier 2/3**

- **Key lesson**: Even with $50+ billion in planned investment, decades of manufacturing experience, and the full weight of US government support (CHIPS Act allocated ~$8.5 billion to Intel), catching the leading edge is extraordinarily difficult. Manufacturing know-how is a **stock variable** (accumulated over time), not a **flow variable** (purchasable in any given year).
  - *Source: Analytical framework; Intel financial disclosures.* **Tier 2**

### 3.4 Samsung's Difficulties Outside Korea

- Samsung's Austin, Texas fab has had persistent yield problems. Samsung's foundry business has consistently trailed TSMC in yield at comparable nodes, even at its Korean facilities.
  - *Source: Industry analyst reports; Korea Economic Daily reporting.* **Tier 3** [VERIFY]

- Samsung's Taylor, Texas fab (announced 2021, ~$17 billion investment) has also faced delays.

## 4. Historical Precedents of Attempted Technological Autarky

### 4.1 The Soviet Union's Semiconductor Failure

The Soviet experience is the most instructive historical case:

- **Initial competitiveness (1950s-1960s)**: The Soviet Union developed transistor and early integrated circuit technology roughly in parallel with the West, drawing on a strong domestic physics tradition.

- **Falling behind (1970s-1980s)**: By the 1970s, a gap had opened. The Soviets attempted to close it through a combination of:
  - Domestic R&D programs
  - Industrial espionage (copying Western designs, notably reverse-engineering Intel processors)
  - Importing equipment through front companies to circumvent COCOM export controls
  - *Source: Hanson, Philip, "The Rise and Fall of the Soviet Economy" (2003), Longman.* **Tier 1** (academic monograph)
  - *Source: Goodman, Seymour E., "The Information Technologies and Soviet Society" in Loren Graham, ed., "Science and the Soviet Social Order" (1990).* **Tier 1**

- **Why autarky failed**:
  1. **Lack of competitive pressure**: Without market competition, Soviet chip designers had no incentive to optimize for yield, cost, or performance.
  2. **Isolation from the global knowledge ecosystem**: Semiconductor manufacturing depends on continuous incremental improvement informed by global exchange of ideas. The Soviets were cut off from conferences, publications, and the tacit knowledge embedded in supplier relationships.
  3. **Equipment gap**: The Soviets could not manufacture the precision equipment needed for advanced lithography. Attempts to reverse-engineer Western equipment consistently produced inferior results because the tacit manufacturing knowledge could not be extracted from the physical artifact.
  4. **Scale disadvantage**: The Soviet domestic market was too small and too insulated to generate the volume needed to drive down costs via learning curves.
  - *Source: Miller, Chris, "Chip War: The Fight for the World's Most Critical Technology" (2022), Scribner — Chapters on Soviet semiconductor efforts.* **Tier 3** (well-researched popular history, based on extensive primary sources)

- **Result**: By the mid-1980s, Soviet microprocessors were estimated to be approximately **10-15 years behind** Western equivalents. This gap contributed to the broader technological stagnation that undermined Soviet economic and military competitiveness.
  - *Source: CIA declassified estimates of Soviet technology levels; academic analyses.* **Tier 1/2** [VERIFY exact declassified source]

### 4.2 China's "Made in China 2025" Semiconductor Push

China's ongoing effort is the contemporary test case:

- **Stated targets (2015)**: The "Made in China 2025" plan targeted 40% semiconductor self-sufficiency by 2020 and 70% by 2025.
  - *Source: State Council of China, "Made in China 2025" (2015).* **Tier 1** (government document)

- **Actual progress (as of ~2024-2025)**: China's semiconductor self-sufficiency rate was estimated at approximately **20-23%** by various analysts — far short of the 70% target. Moreover, much of this "domestic" production was from foreign firms operating in China (Samsung, SK Hynix, Intel fabs in China), not purely Chinese capabilities.
  - *Source: IC Insights; SIA estimates; Rhodium Group analysis.* **Tier 2/3** [VERIFY — this figure changes rapidly]

- **SMIC's progress**: China's leading foundry, SMIC, achieved 7nm-equivalent production (N+2 process) by 2023, demonstrated in Huawei's Mate 60 Pro chip (Kirin 9000s). However:
  - This used older DUV (deep ultraviolet) lithography via multi-patterning — a much slower, less efficient, and more expensive method than ASML's EUV
  - Yields were reportedly low (~50% vs. TSMC's >90% at comparable maturity)
  - SMIC cannot access EUV equipment due to US-led export controls
  - Scaling to 5nm and below without EUV is theoretically possible but economically questionable
  - *Source: TechInsights teardown of Kirin 9000s; analyst reports from Bernstein, Jefferies; Nikkei Asia reporting.* **Tier 2/3** [VERIFY for any SMIC progress beyond 7nm since mid-2025]

- **China's Big Fund (National IC Industry Investment Fund)**:
  - Phase I (2014): ~$21 billion
  - Phase II (2019): ~$29 billion
  - Phase III (2024): ~$47 billion — the largest tranche yet [VERIFY exact amount]
  - Total committed: $100+ billion across all phases plus provincial/local funds
  - Plagued by corruption scandals (multiple Big Fund executives arrested in 2022)
  - Returns have been disappointing relative to investment
  - *Source: Chinese government disclosures; Caixin reporting; Reuters/Bloomberg on corruption investigations.* **Tier 2/3**

- **Key barriers China faces**:
  1. **EUV blockade**: Without ASML EUV machines, China cannot manufacture at the cutting edge using efficient methods
  2. **EDA software**: Chinese EDA tools are generations behind Synopsys/Cadence
  3. **Talent**: Despite massive investments in university programs, China faces a shortage of experienced process engineers — estimated at 200,000-300,000 person gap
  4. **Materials and chemicals**: Many specialty chemicals remain import-dependent
  - *Source: CSIS (Center for Strategic and International Studies) semiconductor reports; industry analyses.* **Tier 2**

### 4.3 Other Autarky Attempts

- **India**: Announced semiconductor manufacturing ambitions (2022-2023), including Tata Group partnerships. Progress has been slow; India has no leading-edge fab under construction. The Tata-PSMC (Powerchip) fab in Gujarat targets mature nodes (28nm and above).
  - *Source: Indian government announcements; industry reporting.* **Tier 3** [VERIFY]

- **Japan's revival effort**: Japan launched Rapidus (2022), a public-private consortium aiming for 2nm chip production by 2027 in partnership with IBM. This is an extraordinarily ambitious timeline given Japan has not manufactured leading-edge logic chips in over a decade.
  - *Source: Rapidus corporate announcements; METI policy documents; Nikkei Asia.* **Tier 2/3** [VERIFY progress]

- **European Chips Act**: The EU committed ~43 billion euros (public + private) to double Europe's global semiconductor market share to 20% by 2030. Intel's planned Magdeburg, Germany fab (delayed/uncertain as of 2024-2025) was a centerpiece. Progress has been slow.
  - *Source: European Commission; Intel press releases; FT/Reuters reporting.* **Tier 2/3** [VERIFY Intel Magdeburg status]

### 4.4 What History Tells Us

**Pattern**: Every attempt at semiconductor autarky has either failed outright (Soviet Union), fallen dramatically short of targets (China), or remained in early/uncertain stages (India, Japan Rapidus, EU). The common thread is that **the semiconductor supply chain's complexity exceeds what any single nation can replicate within a relevant timeframe**.

## 5. Why Specialization Deepens Over Time

### 5.1 Increasing Concentration Over Time

The semiconductor industry has shown a clear trend toward **greater concentration** at the leading edge:

- **Number of companies at the leading edge**:
  - 2001 (~130nm): ~25 companies could manufacture at the leading node
  - 2010 (~32nm): ~10 companies
  - 2017 (~10nm): 4 companies (TSMC, Samsung, Intel, GlobalFoundries)
  - 2018: GlobalFoundries abandoned leading-edge development
  - 2024 (~3nm): 2 companies (TSMC, Samsung), with Intel attempting to rejoin
  - *Source: Widely cited industry data; SIA; IC Insights historical data.* **Tier 2** [VERIFY exact counts — minor variations exist by source]

- **This concentration is accelerating, not reversing**: Each new node requires more capital, more specialized knowledge, and more precise equipment. The barriers to entry increase with each generation.

### 5.2 Network Effects and Economies of Scale

- **Learning curve effects**: The semiconductor industry follows "Wright's Law" — costs decrease by a fixed percentage with each doubling of cumulative production. TSMC's cumulative production volume far exceeds any competitor, giving it an inherent cost advantage that compounds over time.
  - *Source: Wright, T.P., "Factors Affecting the Cost of Airplanes," Journal of the Aeronautical Sciences, 1936 (original formulation).* **Tier 1**
  - *Application to semiconductors: widely discussed in industry analyses and economic literature.*

- **Customer ecosystem lock-in**: TSMC's process design kits (PDKs) represent billions of dollars in customer-side investment. Chip designers (Apple, Nvidia, AMD, etc.) have optimized their designs for TSMC's specific processes. Switching foundries requires years of re-engineering.

- **Equipment co-development**: TSMC co-develops manufacturing processes with equipment makers (ASML, Applied Materials, etc.). These relationships involve deep IP sharing and joint R&D that cannot be replicated by a newcomer.

### 5.3 The Knowledge Ecosystem

- **Conferences and publications**: Cutting-edge semiconductor R&D is disseminated through international venues like IEDM (International Electron Devices Meeting), ISSCC, and VLSI Symposia. These are inherently global forums. Isolation from them means falling behind.

- **Multi-institution research**: The development of EUV lithography involved contributions from national labs (Sandia, Lawrence Livermore — US), universities (multiple countries), and companies (ASML — Netherlands; Cymer — US; Zeiss — Germany) over two decades.
  - *Source: Silverman, A.J., et al., history of EUV development at national labs; ASML corporate histories.* **Tier 2**

- **Tacit knowledge**: Much critical semiconductor manufacturing knowledge is tacit — embedded in the skills and experience of individual engineers and teams. This knowledge transfers through people, not documents. It is why TSMC's yield advantages persist even though the basic physics of its processes are published.
  - *Theoretical framework: Polanyi, Michael, "The Tacit Dimension" (1966).* **Tier 1** (classic academic work)
  - *Application to semiconductors: Appleyard, Melissa, et al., various papers on knowledge management in semiconductor firms.* **Tier 1**

### 5.4 Talent Mobility as Integration Mechanism

- **The "brain circulation" model**: Saxenian (2006) documented how immigrant engineers circulate between Silicon Valley and their home countries (Taiwan, India, China, Israel), creating dense networks of knowledge transfer that bind national innovation systems together.
  - *Source: Saxenian, AnnaLee, "The New Argonauts: Regional Advantage in a Global Economy" (2006), Harvard University Press.* **Tier 1**

- **TSMC's founding**: Morris Chang himself exemplifies this — born in China, educated in the US (MIT, Stanford), worked at Texas Instruments for 25 years, then returned to Taiwan to found TSMC. The entire Taiwanese semiconductor industry was built by engineers trained in the US.

- **Reverse flows are now significant**: Many US-trained Chinese and Indian engineers have returned home to found or lead semiconductor companies, creating additional nodes in the global network rather than displacing existing ones.

## 6. Counterarguments: Cases Where Onshoring Could Succeed

### 6.1 Strongest Arguments for Possible Nationalization

The essay must honestly address these counterarguments:

**A. China could achieve "good enough" self-sufficiency for many applications:**
- The vast majority of chips used in the economy are NOT leading-edge. Mature nodes (28nm and above) are used in automotive, industrial, IoT, and many military applications.
- China already has significant capacity at mature nodes and is rapidly expanding it (SMIC, Hua Hong, etc.).
- For many AI inference workloads (as opposed to training), less-than-cutting-edge chips may suffice.
- **Assessment**: This is a legitimate counterargument, but it does not eliminate dependency for frontier AI training, which requires the most advanced chips. The "peace thesis" most directly applies to frontier capabilities.
  - *Source: CSIS analyses; SIA data on chip demand by node.* **Tier 2**

**B. Technological breakthroughs could disrupt the current supply chain:**
- New computing paradigms (quantum computing, photonic computing, neuromorphic chips) could potentially bypass the current semiconductor supply chain entirely.
- Advanced packaging and chiplet architectures could reduce the importance of leading-edge node shrinks.
- AI itself could accelerate chip design and potentially optimize away some current bottlenecks.
- **Assessment**: Plausible on a 15-30 year horizon, but unlikely to fundamentally change the picture in the next 10 years. Even new paradigms will likely require globally sourced precision manufacturing.
  - *Source: Various academic and industry sources on alternative computing.*

**C. The US CHIPS Act could significantly reduce Taiwan dependency:**
- CHIPS and Science Act (2022): ~$52.7 billion in semiconductor manufacturing incentives plus additional R&D funding.
- TSMC, Samsung, Intel, Micron, and others building or expanding US fabs.
- If successful, the US could produce a meaningful share of leading-edge chips domestically by ~2030.
- **Assessment**: The CHIPS Act will increase US production but is unlikely to achieve self-sufficiency:
  - Total global semiconductor capex is ~$150-180 billion/year; $52.7 billion over 5 years is significant but not transformative
  - US fabs will still depend on ASML (Netherlands), Zeiss (Germany), Japanese materials suppliers
  - Even with domestic fabrication, the design ecosystem requires global talent
  - *Source: CHIPS Act legislation; Congressional Research Service analysis; industry commentary.* **Tier 1/2**

**D. Wartime mobilization: Nations have achieved industrial feats under existential pressure:**
- The Manhattan Project, Apollo program, and wartime industrial mobilization demonstrate that governments can achieve seemingly impossible technological goals when motivated.
- **Assessment**: Valid historical parallel, but semiconductors differ from these examples in crucial ways: the Manhattan Project required physics breakthroughs but relatively simple manufacturing (by modern standards); semiconductor manufacturing requires the opposite — well-understood physics but extraordinarily complex, globally distributed manufacturing. You cannot build an ASML EUV machine on a wartime footing if you don't have Zeiss optics, and Zeiss optics take years to produce.

### 6.2 China's Big Fund: Real Progress Assessment

- **Where China has made progress**:
  - Mature node manufacturing (28nm+): Substantial and growing
  - Chip design: Several competitive firms (HiSilicon/Huawei, Unisoc, CXMT for memory)
  - Packaging and testing: Competitive with global leaders
  - Some materials and chemicals: Gradual progress in localization
  - SMIC's 7nm achievement: Demonstrated technical possibility
  - *Source: IC Insights; Trendforce; industry analyses.* **Tier 2/3** [VERIFY]

- **Where China remains far behind**:
  - Leading-edge fabrication (sub-5nm at scale/viable yields): Not achieved
  - EUV lithography: No domestic capability; blocked from importing
  - EDA software: Chinese tools (e.g., Empyrean) are estimated to be 10-15 years behind Synopsys/Cadence
  - Advanced metrology and inspection equipment: Heavily import-dependent
  - Ultra-pure materials: Significant gaps remain
  - *Source: CSIS; Georgetown CSET; SIA.* **Tier 2**

### 6.3 What True Autarky Would Require

To achieve genuine semiconductor self-sufficiency, a country would need to domestically control:

1. Chip design tools (EDA software) — multi-decade catch-up required
2. Chip design talent — requires world-class university system + industry ecosystem
3. Leading-edge fabrication — $100+ billion investment, 10-15 years minimum
4. Equipment manufacturing (lithography, deposition, etching, metrology) — $50+ billion, 15-20 years
5. Materials (ultra-pure silicon, photoresists, specialty chemicals, gases) — $20+ billion, 10+ years
6. Packaging and testing — relatively achievable
7. IP cores and architectures — significant but potentially circumventable (RISC-V as alternative to ARM/x86)

**Estimated total cost**: Multiple hundreds of billions to over $1 trillion
**Estimated timeline**: 15-25 years for a full-spectrum effort, assuming no disruption from further export controls or technology evolution

- *Source: Synthesis of BCG/SIA (2021) estimates, CSIS analyses, and author's assessment based on available data.* **Tier 2/3**

## Summary Assessment

### Strongest Arguments That the Supply Chain Stays Globally Integrated

1. **Deepening specialization and increasing returns** (Tier 1 economic theory + Tier 2 empirical evidence): The semiconductor supply chain exhibits textbook increasing returns to scale, learning-by-doing, and agglomeration effects. These forces cause concentration to deepen, not reverse, over time. The number of companies capable of leading-edge fabrication has dropped from 25 to 2 in two decades.

2. **No single country controls all layers** (Tier 2 industry data): The US leads in design and EDA; Taiwan in fabrication; Netherlands in lithography; Japan in materials; Germany in precision optics. No country has even a plausible path to controlling all layers simultaneously.

3. **Tacit knowledge cannot be purchased or stolen** (Tier 1 academic theory + Tier 2 empirical evidence): The knowledge embedded in TSMC's processes, ASML's manufacturing, and Zeiss's optics is tacit, cumulative, and path-dependent. It transfers through people and years of experience, not through blueprints.

4. **Historical autarky has universally failed** (Tier 1-2 historical evidence): The Soviet Union fell 10-15 years behind despite massive investment. China's Made in China 2025 hit ~20-23% self-sufficiency vs. a 70% target. No country has ever successfully built a fully independent advanced semiconductor supply chain.

5. **Cost penalties are prohibitive** (Tier 2 economic analysis): Onshoring raises costs 30-65% even with massive subsidies, making autarkic production economically uncompetitive.

6. **The talent pool is inherently global** (Tier 2 data): 65%+ of top US AI researchers are foreign-born. The knowledge that drives frontier AI is produced by a globally distributed community that no single country can fully capture.

### Strongest Counterarguments (Steelman)

1. **"Good enough" sufficiency for most applications**: Leading-edge chips matter for frontier AI training, but the vast majority of economically and militarily useful chips are mature-node. China could achieve near-self-sufficiency in these segments, weakening the interdependence argument for non-frontier applications.

2. **Timeframe matters**: Over a 20-30 year horizon, determined national efforts combined with technological disruptions (new computing paradigms, alternative lithography methods) could erode current bottlenecks. The peace thesis may have a "shelf life."

3. **Wartime motivation changes calculus**: If a major power perceived existential threat, the economic cost premium of autarky would become irrelevant. Nations have historically accepted enormous costs for strategic independence.

4. **RISC-V and open-source alternatives**: The growing RISC-V ecosystem could eventually eliminate dependency on proprietary instruction set architectures (ARM, x86), removing one layer of the dependency chain.

5. **AI itself could be a wild card**: If AI dramatically accelerates materials science, chip design, and manufacturing process optimization, it could compress the timeline for catching up. The very technology the supply chain produces could disrupt the supply chain's structure. [Highly speculative but worth noting]

### Bottom Line for the Essay

The weight of evidence strongly supports the thesis that the AI supply chain will remain globally integrated for at least the next **10-15 years**, and probably longer. The economic, technological, and historical evidence all point in the same direction: semiconductor autarky is extraordinarily expensive, technically daunting, historically unprecedented, and becoming harder over time as complexity increases.

However, the thesis should be carefully scoped:
- It is strongest for **frontier AI capabilities** (training large models), which require leading-edge chips
- It is weaker for **mature-node chips** and **inference workloads**, where autarky is more feasible
- It has a **temporal dimension**: the argument is strongest for the 2025-2040 window and becomes less certain thereafter
- The essay should acknowledge that **determined autarky efforts, while failing to achieve full independence, could degrade the interdependence enough to weaken the peace mechanism** — partial decoupling is more realistic than full autarky, and partial decoupling partially undermines the thesis

### B. SMIC — Faster Than Expected Progress

**Status as of March 2026:**
- **5nm (N+3)**: SMIC completed 5nm process development by December 2025. Huawei's Kirin 9030 SoC has begun manufacturing on this node.
- **7nm yields improving**: The Ascend 910C AI chip yield rate increased from 20% to 40% (still well below TSMC's >90% at comparable maturity).
