# The Intelligence Peace Thesis

**Author:** Liu He, Research Fellow, Hoover Institution, Stanford University
**Target:** Foreign Affairs
**Proposed title:** "The Intelligence Peace Thesis: Locking In Peace In A Post-AGI World"

---

## Core Argument

The current discourse on artificial general intelligence (AGI) is dominated by fears of catastrophic risks or economic displacement. Yet, we are overlooking a potentially transformative positive outcome: the supply chain structure of AGI could replicate and surpass the stabilizing effects of nuclear deterrence and liberal international order to help bring a long-lasting, even eternal, peace to the world.

**The Intelligence Peace Thesis (IPT):** In a post-AGI world, nations that play critical roles on the global AI supply chain will be safe from foreign invasions.

---

## Part 1: The State of Global AI Supply Chain and Why It Creates Peace

The current global AI supply chain has two key characteristics:

- **Globally integrated:** Talents at frontier labs (including founders and technical staff) are often immigrants. A semiconductor chip typically crosses national borders over a hundred times before its completion.
- **Numerous chokepoints:** Single companies or nations often dominate niche markets: ASML in the Netherlands for EUV machines, Zeiss in Germany for specialised optics inside EUV machines, Samsung and SK Hynix in South Korea for high-bandwidth memory, Japan for several specialised manufacturing equipments and advanced materials, TSMC in Taiwan for advanced semiconductor chips, DeepMind in United Kingdom for leading-edge AGI research, America for chip design and several frontier labs.

Such a long, linear, vulnerable supply chain is potentially a security nightmare for AI companies, but generates powerful peace-keeping incentives in the world at large. This is because the global relationship of supply chain dependency among AI supplier and consumer nations creates a strong logic of collective security. The world already runs on semiconductors, and soon will develop an existential dependency on increasingly powerful AI services as the world transitions towards AGI, defined by OpenAI as AI systems that are highly autonomous and outperform humans at most economically valuable work. The relationship of existential dependency implies that countries around the world have an overwhelming collective interest to safeguard the global AI supply chain. So strategically, for any individual country, if they could play a critical role such that it constitutes a single point of failure on the global AI supply chain, then the country has made itself too important to fail. The IPT is the result of this momentum taken to its logical end-state conclusion. In the post-AGI world, nations worldwide will have powerful existential incentives to develop credible deterrence to prevent aggressions against AI supplier nations, and will come to aid in the event of attacks.

## Part 2: The Silicon Shield and Its Enemies

We can see an embryonic form of IPT today in the "Silicon Shield" protecting Taiwan. The Silicon Shield Argument (SSA) suggests that China is prevented from invading Taiwan for two reasons:

- **Mutually assured destruction:** An invasion would shatter China's own supply of TSMC's advanced semiconductors critical for its economic and technological development.
- **Collective security:** Since America depends on TSMC for its advanced semiconductor chips, China is deterred by the prospect of an American military intervention in the event of an invasion.

The SSA applies the same logic of mutually assured destruction and collective security to the specific case of Taiwan. IPT accepts this basic logic and takes a step forward by generalising that the global AI supply chain generates the silicon shields for all countries that form choke points on AI supply chain.

However, notice two differences between IPT and SSA:

- **IPT is a hypothesis about a long-term steady-state:** IPT is a stylised prediction about a long-term post-AGI steady state, whereas SSA is an argument about the present world. There are many reasons why transitional dynamics are more disruptive, potentially dangerous than steady-state dynamics as countries hurry to gain a critical position on the supply chain in fear of being locked out forever once AGI arrives.
- **US and China trying to break Silicon Shield lends support to IPT:** The SSA and IPT have different failure modes. The SSA may fail if either China or the US reduces dependency on Taiwan for advanced chip supply, thereby weakening the silicon shield. However, the fact that China or US may try hard to escape the 'peace trap' of Silicon Shield is a confirmation that the logic works; countries today, as powerful as China and US are, are compelled by the limits of the Silicon Shield and must first reduce supply chain dependency to regain geopolitical optionality. This only strengthens the argument for IPT as a vision and a stabilising force for peace in the world.

In the long-term, the IPT relies on two pillars to work: coordination and credible threat. Coordination is necessary for solving the collective action problem on AI-related security policy of countries around the world. That is, if America stops being the world's policeman. And coordination is only meaningful if it mobilises a credible threat to potential aggressors, backed by a decisive and recognised coalitional strength.

So far, IPT assumes a single AI supply chain in the post-AGI world, and there are economic reasons for a single AI supply chain in the long-term. But there certainly could be more than one AI supply chain. China is attempting a vision of AI autarky which onshores AI supply chain and liberates its AI capabilities from international chokepoints. This creates a second AI supply chain and makes the world more dangerous as a powerful player weans off dependency from other nations and becomes a "rogue super-node," capable of aggression without fear of supply chain severance. Such AI autarkies pose a grave risk to the international community, and should be actively prevented as a matter of global AI policy and security coordination.

For humanity at large, peace in a single-chain world is economically less costly, technologically more innovative, politically more stable and therefore preferable to alternatives. Nonetheless, IPT still holds in a two-chain or even multi-chain worlds, provided that AI blocs maintain credible threats against aggressors based on mutually assured destruction. It is helpful but not necessary for peace that China depends on TSMC for advanced chip supply; it is sufficient that the world relies on Taiwan's semiconductors and is willing to provide a security umbrella for it as a result.

## Part 3: The Transitional Dynamics and the Intelligence Peace Playbook

During the transitional period towards a post-AGI future, we may observe the following characteristics:

- **Heightened security risks and uncertainties** as countries compete for seats at the table in the post-AGI future. This is the period of maximal danger as the security logic of IPT does not yet kick in, and countries could resort to the use of force to gain competitive advantages for a post-AGI world.
- **Geographic and population sizes are irrelevant to national AI power:** Geographically small countries such as the Netherlands and South Korea can gain global importance if they dominate a niche market on the AI supply chain. Territorial and population sizes have no correlation with sovereign power in the AI age.
- **The last window for major political regime change:** Being on the AI supply chain can potentially lock in the country's political system and permanently prevent regime changes in the long term, guaranteeing the survival of AI-producing autocracies. This is because, once hooked onto the AI supply chain, autocracies make their political survival everyone else's problem too. Countries around the world will have an overwhelming reason to ensure the autocracies survive so that AI services will smoothly continue. The period before countries join the AI supply chain could be the last hope for regime change in autocracies.

The logic of IPT recommends several next steps for the world and individual countries in the interest of long-term peace:

- **Build a security layer, a 'NATO for AI', on top of critical nodes on the global AI supply chain:** A 'NATO for AI' will enhance security coordination in the global intelligence commons, not only for the post-AGI world but for the transitional period too. State Department rolled out its Pax Silica initiative, which focuses on economic integration of AI supply chain. This is laudable but insufficient. There needs to be a security layer to protect the economic and technological gains in the medium and long term.
- **Nations facing external threats should join the AI supply chain:** The Netherlands (via ASML) and Taiwan (via TSMC) have demonstrated that possessing a singular technological bottleneck provides elevated international power with other traditionally powerful states. Immediately, their playbook should offer lessons for Ukraine's grand strategy as it faces a perpetual threat from Russia and an uncertain path to NATO membership. The IPT would recommend Ukraine to spearhead a national strategy toward becoming a strong node in the AI supply chain — perhaps by leveraging its energy infrastructure for data centers or vast mineral resources for semiconductor manufacturing. Once AI companies become dependent on Ukraine through the AI supply chain, NATO membership becomes irrelevant; nations around the world will essentially have no choice but to defend Ukraine.
- **Niche market dominance is categorically superior to broadbased supply chain participation:** Nations only develop choke-point capabilities by dominating particular niche markets in the supply chain. Broad and weak participation in multiple niche markets brings few security benefits.
- **Aggressors should depend on the supply chain, but not vice versa:** Any nation on the AI supply chain has the capability to take the world hostage, so it is in the global community's interest to be extremely careful with having nations that have a history and interest in expansive territorial claims join the AI supply chain. At the same time, such nations should be incentivised to continue to rely on the global AI supply chain so that it will not attack its own AI suppliers and sink its own fate.
- **Political lock-ins and the need for a democracy-only club:** The AI supply chain should be composed of democracies only. First, democracies have a good history of maintaining peace among themselves due to trust, shared norms and values. Second, the AI supply chain can potentially lock in political systems and permanently prevent regime changes. Being subjected to a future of stable autocracy is extremely dystopian and tragic for the people living in those countries. There should be a prior to deny autocracies from joining AI supply chain in the absence of a decisive countervailing consideration.

## Part 4: Objections and Responses

**AI Weapons Objection:** The most obvious objections to IPT will point to the host of AI-enabled weaponry, AI-enabled bioengineered pandemics, and other existential nightmares from the proliferation of AGI technology. I acknowledge these challenges, many of which are existential risks. There are of course a host of ways that AGI can help, for example, in inventing better immunisation technology against weaponised viruses or better drone and missile defense systems. The essay merely points out that AGI would create powerful rational incentives to safeguard global security. Still a lot of work needs to be done to realise that vision.

**Peace For Some But Not For All Objection:** One limitation of IPT is that it secures AI supplier nations from attacks, but does not prevent them from attacking other non-AI powers. From the perspective of global peace, the supply chain should integrate as many countries as possible to ensure incentives for peace are well-aligned and security benefits are broadly shared. Therefore, it is all the more important that nations have long-standing political trustworthiness before joining the supply chain.

## Part 5: Conclusion

Ideas about a post-AGI future, the many ways it could go wrong, and also many ways it could go right if we work at them now.
