# Intake Brief: The Intelligence Peace Thesis

## Core Thesis

The global AI supply chain's chokepoint structure will generate collective security incentives powerful enough to protect supplier nations from foreign invasion — creating a durable, self-reinforcing peace mechanism that surpasses nuclear deterrence and military alliances.

### Alternative framings

- **Narrower (recommended if scope feels too large):** Taiwan's "Silicon Shield" is not a special case but the prototype of a generalizable security mechanism: any nation that achieves chokepoint status on the AI supply chain gains a collective security guarantee from the nations that depend on it.
- **More provocative:** In the post-AGI world, supply chain position will matter more for national security than military strength, geographic size, or alliance membership — and nations should act on this now.
- **More prescriptive:** The international community should deliberately engineer the AI supply chain to maximize its peace-keeping properties — integrating democracies, excluding potential aggressors, and building security institutions around supply chain nodes.

## Key Claims

1. **The AI supply chain is globally integrated with numerous chokepoints, where single companies or nations dominate irreplaceable niche markets.**
   - Confidence: **stated**
   - Evidence needed: Supply chain mapping data, market share figures for ASML/TSMC/Samsung/etc., analysis of substitutability at each node. Chris Miller's *Chip War* is the obvious starting source but we need more recent data (2024-2026) on how the supply chain has evolved.

2. **The world will develop an existential dependency on AGI services, making the AI supply chain too critical to disrupt.**
   - Confidence: **implied** — the proposal assumes this trajectory but doesn't evidence it
   - Evidence needed: Economic projections on AI's share of GDP, AI adoption trends, expert assessments of AGI timeline and societal impact. This is the foundational assumption — if AGI doesn't arrive or doesn't create existential dependency, the entire thesis weakens. Needs careful treatment: how is "existential dependency" on AGI different from current dependency on oil, financial systems, or the internet?

3. **Supply chain dependency creates deterrence analogous to nuclear deterrence, via both mutually assured destruction (self-harm from disruption) and collective security (third-party intervention to protect suppliers).**
   - Confidence: **stated**
   - Evidence needed: IR theory on deterrence and economic interdependence. This is the most contested claim — the "commercial peace" literature (Oneal & Russett, Gartzke) supports it, but there are powerful counterexamples. Pre-WWI Europe was deeply economically interdependent. Russia was integrated into European energy markets before invading Ukraine. The essay must engage with the interdependence-doesn't-prevent-war literature head-on.

4. **Taiwan's Silicon Shield is an embryonic and currently functioning form of the IPT.**
   - Confidence: **stated**
   - Evidence needed: Strategic assessments of Taiwan's security posture, analysis of whether TSMC dependency actually constrains China's military calculus, evidence from military planners and China specialists. Also: critics who argue the Silicon Shield is overstated or that China would invade regardless.

5. **Efforts by the US and China to reduce semiconductor dependency on Taiwan confirm the logic — powerful states must first escape the "peace trap" before regaining geopolitical optionality.**
   - Confidence: **stated** — this is a clever inversion where apparent failure of the Silicon Shield (countries trying to break it) is read as confirmation of its power
   - Evidence needed: US CHIPS Act results, Intel/Samsung fab progress, China's semiconductor self-sufficiency efforts (SMIC, Huawei), and honest assessment of how successful these diversification efforts have been.

6. **AI autarky — particularly China's attempt to onshore the full AI supply chain — creates "rogue super-nodes" that threaten the peace-keeping logic.**
   - Confidence: **stated**
   - Evidence needed: China's semiconductor self-sufficiency progress (realistic assessment, not hype), DeepSeek and China's AI model capabilities, analysis of how close any nation is to genuine AI autarky. Also: is full AI autarky even technically achievable, or are there irreducible dependencies?

7. **Nations can deliberately engineer chokepoint status on the AI supply chain as a security strategy (the "Intelligence Peace Playbook").**
   - Confidence: **speculative** — the Ukraine application is the boldest claim in the proposal and the most in need of evidence
   - Evidence needed: Historical precedents of nations engineering economic indispensability for security purposes, feasibility analysis of Ukraine specifically (energy infrastructure, mineral resources, workforce, timeline), analysis of whether chokepoint status can be manufactured or only emerges organically.

8. **The AI supply chain should be restricted to democracies to prevent political lock-in of autocratic regimes.**
   - Confidence: **stated**
   - Evidence needed: Democratic peace theory (Doyle, Russett), evidence on whether economic integration stabilizes or destabilizes autocratic regimes, normative political philosophy on the ethics of permanent regime lock-in. Note: this claim is in tension with the goal of broad integration — if you exclude China, you lose the mutual dependency that creates deterrence *with* China.

## Domain & Literatures

- **Primary field:** International Relations — security studies and international political economy
- **Relevant subfields:**
  - Deterrence theory (nuclear deterrence, extended deterrence, credible commitment)
  - Economic interdependence and peace (the "commercial peace" debate — Oneal & Russett vs. critics)
  - Supply chain geopolitics and economic statecraft (sanctions literature, weaponized interdependence)
  - Democratic peace theory
  - Alliance theory and collective security
  - Technology and international relations
- **Adjacent fields:**
  - Industrial organization and economics of supply chains (concentration, chokepoints, resilience)
  - AI governance and policy (NIST, EU AI Act, international AI agreements)
  - Comparative political economy (regime stability, authoritarian resilience)
  - Economic coercion literature (Farrell & Newman on "weaponized interdependence" — directly relevant and potentially challenging to the thesis, since they show that supply chain dependency can be used *offensively*, not just defensively)
- **Key journals/venues:** *International Security*, *International Organization*, *Journal of Conflict Resolution*, *Foreign Affairs* (prior pieces on Silicon Shield, semiconductor geopolitics, AI governance), Brookings/RAND/CSIS reports on semiconductor supply chain

## Open Questions

1. **Does economic interdependence actually prevent war?** This is the make-or-break question. The proposal treats supply chain dependency as inherently peace-producing, but IR scholarship is divided. The Russia-Ukraine case is the most recent and damaging counterexample: Russia was deeply integrated into European energy markets, and it invaded anyway. The essay needs a clear argument for why AI supply chain dependency is different — not just in degree, but in kind — from oil/gas dependency or pre-WWI trade integration. Without this, the thesis is vulnerable to a devastating two-word objection: "What about Russia?"

2. **How does open-source AI and model commoditization affect the thesis?** The proposal doesn't mention open-source AI models (Llama, DeepSeek, Mistral) or the trend toward running capable AI locally. If frontier AI capabilities become available without depending on the full hardware supply chain, the chokepoint logic weakens. Is AGI something that can be run on commodity hardware, or will it always require bleeding-edge chips? This is a potentially fatal objection that the essay needs to address.

3. **The integrate-everyone vs. democracies-only tension:** The essay simultaneously argues that (a) peace requires broad supply chain integration to align incentives, and (b) the supply chain should exclude autocracies. These pull in opposite directions. If you exclude China from the AI supply chain, you don't get the mutual dependency that deters China — which is the most important deterrence to secure. How do you resolve this?

4. **Is the "playbook" realistic?** Can nations actually manufacture chokepoint status on a competitive global supply chain? ASML and TSMC didn't achieve dominance through national security strategy — they achieved it through decades of engineering excellence and massive R&D investment. Can Ukraine replicate this deliberately, on a policy-relevant timeline, while fighting a war? The essay should be honest about the gap between the theoretical elegance of the playbook and the practical difficulty of execution.

5. **What makes this different from the liberal institutionalist argument that already exists?** The IPT shares DNA with liberal internationalism (interdependence promotes peace, institutions coordinate collective security). What's genuinely new here? The essay should clearly distinguish IPT from existing frameworks — otherwise it risks being read as "liberal peace theory, but for semiconductors."

6. **Can the thesis be tested or falsified?** If IPT is about a "long-term post-AGI steady state" that hasn't arrived yet, how would we know if it's wrong? The Silicon Shield is offered as an embryonic test case, but what would refute the thesis? FA editors will want to know this isn't just a speculative scenario but an argument with empirical grip.

## Scope Check

**Assessment: Too broad for a single 3,000-5,000 word FA article as currently structured.**

The proposal tries to accomplish too much in one piece:
1. Establish the theoretical framework (IPT)
2. Analyze the Taiwan/Silicon Shield case in detail
3. Address single-chain vs. multi-chain dynamics and AI autarky
4. Describe transitional dynamics
5. Propose a full policy playbook (NATO for AI, Ukraine strategy, democracy-only club, niche dominance doctrine)
6. Handle objections

Each of the playbook items (NATO for AI, Ukraine strategy, democracy-only supply chain) could be its own article. At 3-5k words, trying to do all of this produces a survey that argues nothing deeply.

**Recommended narrowing:** Center the essay on three things:
1. **Establish the IPT framework** — the core logic of why AI supply chain chokepoints create collective security (and why this is different from prior economic interdependence arguments)
2. **Ground it in the Silicon Shield** — Taiwan as proof of concept, including honest treatment of the counterarguments
3. **One marquee policy implication** — pick the single most compelling recommendation and develop it fully

The Ukraine playbook is the most novel and provocative policy application. If you develop it seriously (with evidence on feasibility), it gives FA readers something genuinely new. The NATO-for-AI and democracy-only-club arguments are important but more familiar — they could be a follow-up piece.

Alternatively: Split into two articles. Article 1 establishes IPT conceptually (framework + Silicon Shield evidence + key objections). Article 2 develops the policy playbook (NATO for AI, Ukraine strategy, democracy-only supply chain, managing AI autarky). Article 1 is the priority.
