# Essay Series Strategy: The Intelligence Peace Thesis

**Format:** Essay series (8 chapters), modeled on Drago & Laine's [Intelligence Curse](https://intelligence-curse.ai)
**Target length:** 2,000–4,000 words per chapter, ~20,000 words total
**Tone:** Authoritative, deductive, Bostrom-meets-Mearsheimer. Each chapter is self-contained but builds toward a unified theory.
**Publication:** Standalone site (like intelligence-curse.ai) + possible condensed versions later

---

## Argument Arc

The series builds the Intelligence Peace Thesis from the ground up. It starts by establishing the factual landscape (AI's importance, the supply chain's structure), then derives the peace mechanism, then stress-tests it against existing theories and failure conditions, and concludes with the institutional architecture needed to realize it.

**Emotional arc across the series:**
- Chapters 1–2: *Recognition* — "AI is transformative, and the supply chain is more fragile and integrated than I realized"
- Chapter 3: *Intellectual surprise* — "Wait — this structure could actually generate peace?"
- Chapters 4–5: *Engagement* — "How does this relate to what I already know about peace and power?"
- Chapter 6: *Honest reckoning* — "Under what conditions does this break?"
- Chapter 7: *Conviction* — "The democratic supply chain vision is both desirable and achievable"
- Chapter 8: *Urgency* — "We need an AI NATO, and the window is closing"

---

## Core Logic

**Two Conditions:**
1. The world develops an existential dependency on AI services
2. AI services are produced through a globally integrated supply chain with irreplaceable chokepoints

**Two Mechanisms:**
1. **Mutual Restraint** — Every node on the chain depends on every other node. Attacking any node is self-destructive. Taiwan can't attack the Netherlands (ASML) because TSMC needs EUV machines. The Netherlands can't attack Taiwan because ASML needs TSMC to buy its machines. No node can weaponize its position without destroying itself.
2. **Collective Security** — Every dependent nation has an overwhelming *individual* incentive to defend any node under attack. This is not a public good with free-rider problems — it's a private necessity for each nation.

**Result:** Nations playing critical roles in the AI supply chain are protected by structural position, not military strength. This is the Intelligence Peace.

**Key framing:** The supply chain should be globally integrated but rested within democratic systems. Autocracies are dependent consumers, not chokepoint suppliers. This is desirable and achievable.

### Chapter 2: Why the Chain Holds (~3,000 words)

**Purpose:** Establish that the AI supply chain will remain globally integrated in the long term. This is the foundation the entire peace argument rests on. If any country could onshore everything, the thesis collapses.

**Content:**

**The economics of integration:**
- Comparative advantage (Ricardo applied to semiconductors): different nations specialize because of genuine advantages — talent pools, accumulated expertise, infrastructure, cost structures
- Gains from trade in complex technology: a single country attempting the full stack sacrifices the specialization benefits that make frontier tech possible
- Winner-take-most dynamics: semiconductor manufacturing exhibits extreme economies of scale and learning curves. First movers compound their lead.

**Why frontier technology requires global collaboration:**
- ASML's EUV machine: components from dozens of countries, Zeiss optics (Germany), light source technology, precision engineering. No single country has all inputs.
- TSMC's process: depends on foreign chemicals, materials, equipment (Japanese photoresist, American EDA tools, Dutch lithography)
- AI research talent: what % of top researchers are immigrants or work internationally? Frontier research is inherently collaborative.

**The cost of onshoring:**
- TSMC Arizona: costs ~2x what equivalent fabs cost in Taiwan. 5+ years even with full cooperation.
- Intel's struggles with advanced nodes: delayed timelines, yield problems
- Samsung outside Korea: similar difficulties
- Why: fab construction requires local ecosystem (suppliers, talent, infrastructure) that takes decades to develop

**Historical precedents of attempted autarky:**
- Soviet semiconductor independence: how it went, why it failed
- China's "Made in China 2025" semiconductor targets: where they stand (equipment self-sufficiency 13.6%)
- Lessons: complex technology supply chains resist nationalization because the knowledge is distributed, tacit, and built on decades of iterative specialization

**Why specialization deepens:**
- Concentration in semiconductors has *increased* over time, not decreased
- Network effects and knowledge ecosystems
- The more advanced the technology, the more specialized the supply chain becomes

**Key claim:** The globally integrated supply chain is not an accident or a fragile arrangement — it is the natural result of economic forces that intensify over time. Autarky is not just expensive; it is economically irrational at the frontier.

### Chapter 3: The Intelligence Peace Thesis (~2,500 words)

**Purpose:** State the thesis. The core argument in its cleanest form. Two conditions, two mechanisms, one result. The reader should feel the logical force: if you accept the conditions (established in Chapters 1–2), the peace mechanism follows.

**Content:**

**State the thesis:** In a world where (1) AI is existentially important and (2) the AI supply chain is globally integrated with irreplaceable chokepoints, nations playing critical roles on the chain are protected from attack by structural position.

**Derive Mechanism 1 — Mutual Restraint:**
- Every node depends on every other node
- Attacking any node is self-destructive: you destroy what you need
- Taiwan can't attack the Netherlands; the Netherlands can't attack Taiwan; neither can attack Japan, South Korea, or the US — because each depends on what the others provide
- Even *capturing* a facility intact fails: it can't operate without its global supply chain. Other nations cut it off immediately.
- This is multilateral, not bilateral: the entire chain is locked into mutual dependency

**Derive Mechanism 2 — Collective Security:**
- Every dependent nation has an overwhelming individual incentive to defend any node under attack
- No free-rider problem: each nation is individually harmed
- No credibility problem: nations act in material self-interest, which is always credible
- No coordination problem: the incentive is individually rational

**State the result:**
- The attacker is deterred by self-harm (Mechanism 1) and faces a coalition of all dependent nations (Mechanism 2)
- Any nation hosting a chokepoint is "too important to fail"
- This is the Intelligence Peace — peace through structural position

**Temporal framing:** This is a prediction about the post-AGI steady state, not a description of the present. The essay series is explicit about this throughout.

**Evidence needed:** None — this is pure logical derivation. Evidence was established in Chapters 1–2.

### Chapter 4: Peace Theories and Their Track Record (~3,000 words)

**Purpose:** Situate IPT within the rich tradition of peace theories. Show similarities first — IPT builds on real intellectual foundations. Examine how well prior theories have held up. Then offer the distinctions.

**Content:**

**The liberal/commercial peace tradition:**
- Russett & Oneal: interdependence has "important pacific benefits"
- Gartzke: "capitalism, not democracy, leads to peace"
- Track record: broadly supported statistically, but with notable failures (WWI, Russia-Ukraine invoked as counterexamples)
- IPT similarities: shares the insight that economic integration constrains conflict
- IPT distinction: existential vs. substitutable dependency; chokepoints vs. diffuse trade; multilateral vs. bilateral harm; no exit option (2025 systematic review: exit options mitigate risk — AI supply chains have no exit)

**Nuclear deterrence:**
- Track record: no great-power war since 1945; widely credited
- IPT similarities: both based on costs too catastrophic to accept
- IPT distinction: structural not volitional (no willingness to act required); multilateral not bilateral; protects small states (Netherlands with 17M people is more important than most large nations because of ASML); no arsenal needed

**WWI — the perennial counterexample:**
- Gartzke & Lupu: WWI began in the *weakly integrated* portion of Europe (Balkans). Crises among highly integrated Western powers were resolved peacefully. Alliance entanglement dragged integrated states into a non-integrated conflict.
- Norman Angell was right that war was *futile* — he never said it was impossible. IPT argues something stronger: AI supply chain destruction is not just futile but self-defeating.

**Russia-Ukraine — the recent counterexample:**
- Copeland framing: Putin acted when expectations about future access turned negative
- Five structural reasons it's not analogous to IPT: fait accompli strategy, supplier vs. consumer leverage, energy fungibility, bilateral dependency, authoritarian echo chamber
- The structural comparison table (Energy vs. AI/Semiconductors)

**Key claim:** IPT is not a repudiation of these traditions — it is their culmination. The commercial peace insight is correct but was too weak because trade was substitutable. Nuclear deterrence works but is bilateral and volitional. IPT inherits their strengths while addressing their structural weaknesses.

**Evidence needed:**
- Commercial peace literature (Russett & Oneal, Gartzke, Barbieri)
- Nuclear deterrence comparison
- Gartzke & Lupu on WWI
- Copeland on Russia-Ukraine
- 2025 Cogent Social Sciences systematic review

### Chapter 5: Chokepoints, Power, and Restraint (~3,000 words)

**Purpose:** Engage deeply with the chokepoint literature — primarily Farrell & Newman. This is intellectually the most sophisticated chapter. It establishes why chokepoints create restraint (defensive protection) and not just coercion (offensive power).

**Content:**

**Farrell & Newman's framework:**
- *Weaponized Interdependence* (2019): hub-and-spoke networks concentrate power; panopticon + chokepoint effects
- *Underground Empire* (2023): SWIFT, semiconductors, EDA tools — empirical documentation of chokepoint power
- Their empirical work confirms IPT's foundational premise: chokepoints confer real, consequential power

**The critical distinction: selective denial vs. total destruction:**
- Farrell & Newman analyze *selective denial* — cutting specific actors off while the network continues running (Iran/SWIFT, Huawei export controls)
- IPT's deterrence depends on *total destruction* — invasion destroys fabs for everyone, including the attacker
- Selective denial is an offensive weapon. Total destruction is a collective defense trigger. These are categorically different.
- This is the essay's key theoretical contribution.

**The weaponized economy problem:**
- Current US policy uses chokepoints offensively (export controls on China)
- This generates the Copeland dynamic: negative expectations about future access → incentive for preventive countermeasures
- Farrell's own critique (2025): US is degrading its platform power, "enshittification of American hegemony"
- The *International Organization* (2025) decoupling dilemma: sanctions erode the interdependence they exploit

**The Silicon Shield — empirical case study:**
- Taiwan as the embryonic form of IPT: MAD (China needs TSMC) + collective security (US/allies depend on TSMC)
- The shield is real economically: Taiwan produces 90%+ of advanced chips, China bought 53.8% of Taiwan's chip exports
- The shield is incomplete politically: CFR, Defense Priorities, Horster critiques
- Escape attempts confirm the logic: TSMC Arizona ($165B), CHIPS Act, China's Big Fund III — both superpowers spending billions to exit the peace trap
- But escape is very slow: 5-10+ years, enormous cost, uncertain success
- IPT's contribution: the Silicon Shield is raw material that needs institutional architecture to function as stable deterrence

**Key claim:** Chokepoint power is real, but how it's used matters. Offensive weaponization (sanctions) generates instability. Defensive architecture (collective security) generates peace. The policy choice between these two uses is the central question of AI geopolitics.

**Evidence needed:**
- Farrell & Newman (2019, 2023, 2025)
- Silicon Shield assessments (CFR, Defense Priorities, Stimson, Horster)
- CHIPS Act progress, TSMC Arizona data
- Beaumier et al. on cross-network weaponization
- China's Big Fund III and autarky progress

### Chapter 6: When the Argument Breaks (~2,500 words)

**Purpose:** Map the failure conditions explicitly. Under what conditions does IPT hold, and when does it collapse? This is essential for intellectual credibility.

**Content:**

**Condition 1 fails — AI not yet existential:**
- This is the present situation. IPT doesn't describe today.
- If AI is important but not existential, the cost of supply chain disruption may not exceed geopolitical gains
- Trajectory is clear (Goldman/PwC/McKinsey convergence) but current measured impact is only 0.2-0.4%
- Hedge: even narrow AI is creating substrate dependency; AGI accelerates but isn't strictly required

**Condition 2 fails — autarky achieved:**
- If a nation builds a complete, independent supply chain, Mechanism 1 (mutual restraint) fails for that nation
- China's progress: Big Fund III ($47.5B), SMIC, EUV prototype
- Honest assessment: China is far from autarky (equipment self-sufficiency 13.6%, SMIC 5-7 years behind, EUV prototype years from production)
- Why autarky is the enemy of peace and should be actively prevented

**Condition 2 fails — replicability:**
- If destroyed chokepoints can be quickly rebuilt, the cost of disruption drops
- Current evidence: 17-30 years for EUV, 5+ years for a fab with full cooperation
- No evidence of fast replicability at the frontier

**Irrationality:**
- A determined adversary may accept devastation (Defense Priorities critique)
- Response: no deterrence system is absolute; this limitation applies to nuclear deterrence too
- IPT advantage: costs are automatic (invasion destroys fabs) not discretionary (someone must choose to retaliate)
- The bar for "irrational enough" rises dramatically — equivalent to voluntarily returning to pre-industrial era

**The Copeland Problem — the most dangerous failure mode:**
- What matters is not current dependency but *expectations about future access*
- Negative expectations + high dependency = incentive for preventive war
- Current US export controls are generating exactly this dynamic
- The transition period (before steady state) is the period of maximum danger
- Horster (2025): US diversification + Chinese perception of "closing window" = Silicon Trap

**Key claim:** The IPT has identifiable, testable failure conditions. The most urgent is the Copeland dynamic: exclusionary policies that flip expectations negative. The policy implication is clear: maintaining positive expectations about supply chain access is essential.

**Evidence needed:**
- Copeland (1996, 2015, 2023)
- China's autarky progress data
- Defense Priorities critique
- Horster on Silicon Trap
- IO 2025 decoupling dilemma

### Chapter 7: The Democratic Supply Chain (~3,000 words)

**Purpose:** Argue that the AI supply chain should be globally integrated but rested within democratic systems. Autocracies should be dependent consumers, not chokepoint suppliers. This is one of the essay's key claims.

**Content:**

**Why democracies should control the chokepoints:**
- Democratic peace tradition: democracies have a strong track record of not going to war with each other
- Trust and transparency: democratic institutions enable the credible commitments needed for supply chain cooperation
- Current reality: the major chokepoint nations are already democracies (Netherlands, Taiwan, Japan, South Korea, US, Germany)
- This is not an accident — democratic institutions foster the kind of open, collaborative innovation ecosystem that produces frontier technology

**Autocracies as dependent consumers, not suppliers:**
- Keeping autocracies dependent on the democratic supply chain is a feature, not a bug
- Dependency creates restraint: as long as China expects continued access, peace is likely (Copeland)
- But dependency without chokepoint status means autocracies cannot weaponize their position
- The asymmetry is intentional: democratic nations hold structural power; autocratic consumers are deterred by dependency

**The lock-in problem:**
- Once an autocracy becomes a chokepoint supplier, the world has incentive to keep it stable — locking in its political system
- Drago & Laine's "Intelligence Curse" (2025): resource curse analogy — the same structure enables peace *and* authoritarian concentration
- This is a real cost the essay must weigh honestly
- Resolution: this is precisely why chokepoint roles should be reserved for democracies
- The period before countries join the supply chain could be the last window for regime change

**Why integration with adversaries still matters:**
- Excluding autocracies from the supply chain *entirely* generates the Copeland dynamic (negative expectations → preventive action)
- The right policy: integrate as consumers/dependents, exclude from chokepoint roles
- Keep China buying TSMC chips (dependency = restraint) but prevent China from becoming a chokepoint supplier (autarky = danger)

**Key claim:** The Intelligence Peace works best when the supply chain is a democratic commons — controlled by democracies, accessible to all, weaponizable by none. This is both the most stable configuration and the most desirable.

**Evidence needed:**
- Democratic peace literature
- Drago & Laine Intelligence Curse
- Current distribution of chokepoint nations (all democracies)
- Copeland on expectations and integration
- Autocratic lock-in risks

### Chapter 8: An AI NATO (~2,500 words)

**Purpose:** The policy vision. A collective security organization for the AI supply chain. This is the essay's actionable conclusion and most radical recommendation.

**Content:**

**Why collective defense is necessary:**
- The Silicon Shield is incomplete because it lacks institutional architecture
- Raw economic dependency is not enough — it needs a formal security guarantee
- The current approach (diversify, onshore, hedge) is actively dismantling the peace mechanism
- An AI NATO: treat military or economic attack on any chokepoint nation as an attack on all

**What an AI NATO looks like:**
- Member states: all nations hosting critical AI supply chain nodes (Netherlands, Taiwan, Japan, South Korea, US, Germany, others)
- Article 5 equivalent: military or economic coercion against a member's AI infrastructure triggers collective response
- Intelligence sharing on supply chain threats
- Coordinated export control policy (defensive, not offensive)
- Joint investment in supply chain resilience (redundancy within the alliance, not diversification away from allies)

**Why you can't diversify away from Taiwan:**
- TSMC Arizona is progress but tiny: 10K wafers/month vs. 2M+ in Taiwan
- Even with $165B invested, the US won't have meaningful independence for 5-10+ years
- Intel 18A delayed, Samsung Taylor delayed, yields uncertain
- The realistic option is not replacing Taiwan — it's defending Taiwan
- Securing Taiwan is securing AI

**The supply chain as the new security architecture:**
- Geographic and population size become irrelevant: the Netherlands (17M people) matters more than most large nations
- National security strategy for vulnerable nations: become indispensable to the supply chain
- Nations facing threats should pursue chokepoint status as a rational security strategy
- The Pax Silica approach (economic integration) is laudable but insufficient — it needs a security layer

**Key claim:** The only way to secure the AI supply chain is to defend it collectively. Diversification is a fantasy on policy-relevant timescales. An AI NATO is not idealistic — it is the logical consequence of the Intelligence Peace Thesis.

**Evidence needed:**
- Silicon Shield limitations (CFR, Defense Priorities)
- TSMC Arizona vs. Taiwan capacity data
- CHIPS Act progress and timelines
- Victor Cha's collective resilience concept
- G-7 Hiroshima declaration on weaponized dependencies
- Pax Silica initiative details
- Historical precedents of collective security organizations
