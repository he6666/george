# Essay Strategy: The Intelligence Peace Thesis

**Target publication:** Foreign Affairs
**Target length:** 4,000–5,000 words
**Tone:** Authoritative, declarative, Bostrom-meets-Mearsheimer — a deductive framework delivered with FA confidence and policy urgency

## Argument Arc

The essay takes the reader from a familiar observation (AI supply chains are geopolitically important) through a clean game-theoretic derivation (two conditions + two mechanisms = collective defense of supplier nations) to a surprising conclusion (supply chain structure, not military alliances, will be the primary peace mechanism of the post-AGI era). The structure is **deductive**: state the argument first, then show it differs from existing frameworks, then ground it in evidence, then map its limits. The emotional arc moves from curiosity → intellectual surprise → conviction → urgency. The reader finishes believing that the Intelligence Peace Thesis is not speculative futurism but a logical consequence of trends already underway — and that the policy window to shape this outcome is closing.

## Method

The essay follows a **deductive method** — state the argument as a clean logical derivation from two conditions, then show how it differs from existing peace theories, then marshal empirical evidence for the conditions, then map the failure conditions. This is the structure of Mearsheimer's "Back to the Future" or Bostrom's *Superintelligence*: clear premises, rigorous derivation, explicit limits. FA publishes this deductive style when the thesis is bold enough to justify it.

**Two Conditions:**
1. The world has developed an existential dependency on AI services
2. AI services are produced through a single global supply chain with irreplaceable chokepoints

**Two Mechanisms:**
1. Mutually Assured Destruction — attacking a chokepoint destroys what the attacker itself depends on
2. Collective Security — every dependent nation individually has overwhelming reason to defend any chokepoint under attack (no free-rider problem, no credibility problem, no coordination problem)

**Result:** Chokepoint nations are "too important to fail" — protected not by military strength, alliances, or geography, but by structural position.

**This is a prediction about the post-AGI steady state, not a description of the present world.** The essay must be explicit about this temporal framing throughout.

### 2. The Core Argument (~800 words)

**Purpose:** The clean game-theoretic derivation — the essay's intellectual center of gravity. This section follows `game-theory-core.md` closely. The reader should feel the force of the logical derivation: if you accept the two conditions, the result follows necessarily.

**Content:**

**State the two conditions:**
- Condition 1: Existential dependency on AI. Loss of AI access is not a setback — it is civilizational regression. No geopolitical gain compensates for it.
- Condition 2: Single, integrated supply chain with chokepoints. Specific facilities in specific nations dominate irreplaceable niche markets. The chain breaks if any chokepoint is destroyed.

**Derive the two mechanisms:**

*Mechanism 1 — Mutually Assured Destruction:*
- If the attacker depends on the AI supply chain (and in the steady state, every nation does), then attacking a chokepoint destroys what the attacker itself needs
- This is not ordinary cost-of-war calculus — the cost is existentially large (Condition 1) and outweighs any conceivable gain
- Even capturing a facility intact fails: it can't operate without its global supply chain, other nations cut it off, the chain is a network not a single asset

*Mechanism 2 — Collective Security:*
- Every dependent nation has an overwhelming *individual* incentive to defend the chokepoint under attack
- No free-rider problem: each nation is individually harmed, not just the bilateral partner. Defense is a private necessity, not a public good
- No credibility problem: nations act in material self-interest, which is always credible (unlike nuclear retaliation — "would France really launch over Estonia?")
- No coordination problem: the incentive is individually rational and doesn't require prior agreement or institutional machinery

**State the result:**
- The attacker is deterred by self-harm (Mechanism 1) and faces a coalition of all dependent nations (Mechanism 2)
- No rational nation attacks a chokepoint nation
- Any nation hosting a chokepoint is "too important to fail" — protected by structural position, not military strength, alliances, or geography
- Frame explicitly: this is the Intelligence Peace — a prediction about the post-AGI steady state

**Evidence needed:**
- None in this section — this is pure logical derivation. Evidence comes in Section 4.

**Transition to next:** "This argument will sound familiar to students of international relations — but it is fundamentally different from any existing peace theory."

### 3. Why This Is Not Just Another Peace Theory (~500 words)

**Purpose:** Distinguish IPT from liberal peace, nuclear deterrence, and weaponized interdependence. This section earns the essay's claim to originality. Without it, the IR-literate FA reader will immediately file IPT under "commercial peace" and dismiss it.

**Content:**

**vs. Liberal peace (Russett & Oneal, Gartzke):**
- Existential vs. substitutable dependency: ordinary trade is sourced elsewhere, stockpiled, or done without. AI dependency is existential — no substitute, no stockpile, no doing without
- Chokepoints vs. diffuse trade: ordinary trade is distributed across many partners. AI supply chain has single points of failure. The relevant variable is not "how much trade" but "is there a single point of failure"
- Multilateral vs. bilateral harm: in liberal peace, third-party nations are weakly affected by a bilateral disruption. In IPT, all nations depend on the *same* chain — an attack on a chokepoint harms everyone
- The standard "pre-WWI" counterexample fails on all three distinctions

**vs. Nuclear deterrence:**
- Structural not volitional: nuclear deterrence requires the *willingness to act* (retaliation); IPT requires only the *existence of dependency*
- Multilateral not bilateral: nuclear deterrence is A-threatens-B; IPT protects chokepoint nations from *everyone*
- No credibility problem: self-interest is always credible; willingness to launch is not
- Protects small states: the Netherlands (17M people) is more important than most large nations because of ASML. No arsenal needed.

**vs. Weaponized interdependence (Farrell & Newman):**
- Critical distinction: Farrell & Newman analyze *selective denial* (cutting specific actors off while the network runs). IPT's deterrence depends on *total destruction* (invasion destroys fabs for everyone)
- Selective denial is an offensive weapon. Total destruction is a collective defense trigger. Categorically different.
- Their empirical work *confirms* IPT's foundational premise: if SWIFT chokepoints generate coercive power, AI supply chain chokepoints generate even more

**Evidence needed:**
- Must-have: Farrell & Newman's framework (2019 *International Security* + 2025 FA piece)
- Must-have: Overview of commercial peace literature (Oneal & Russett, Gartzke)
- Nice-to-have: Nuclear deterrence comparison data (bilateral vs. multilateral structure)

**Transition to next:** "The argument is logically sound. The question is whether its conditions hold in the real world."

### 4. The Empirical Picture (~1,000 words)

**Purpose:** Ground the abstract argument in evidence — show the two conditions are plausible. This section does what old Section 2 (Five Axioms) did, but now the evidence is marshaled *for* the two conditions rather than presented as independent premises.

**Content:**

**Evidence for Condition 1 — AI is becoming existentially important:**
- AI economic integration: Goldman Sachs $7T GDP impact, PwC 15 percentage points by 2035, McKinsey $4.4T annual profits, $23T by 2040. 78% of organizations using AI; 99% of Fortune 500.
- Military integration: Pentagon $13.4B FY2026 autonomy budget, renamed "Department of War." 343 PLA AI contracts (CSET). Both superpowers investing as if AI dominance = military dominance (revealed preference).
- AGI timelines: Grace et al. median 2047 (shifted 13 years earlier in one year); Metaculus 50% by 2033. MacAskill & Moorhouse: 10^7 to 10^14 multiplier on effective research capability.
- Caveat: current measured GDP impact is only 0.2–0.4%. Condition 1 is about trajectory, not present state. But the trend is unambiguous and the investment patterns show revealed preference.

**Evidence for Condition 2 — Chokepoints exist and are non-replicable:**
- ASML: 100% of EUV lithography, ~83% of all lithography sales. No competitor exists. 100,000 components, 180 tons, $200–380M per machine, 5,150+ supplier companies.
- TSMC: 64–67% overall foundry, ~90% at sub-7nm advanced nodes. CoWoS advanced packaging fully booked through 2026.
- SK Hynix + Samsung: 90%+ of global HBM memory.
- NVIDIA: 80% GPU market share, $49B AI-related revenue in 2025.
- Non-replicability: ASML's EUV represents 17–30 years of R&D, EUR 6B+ investment. Zeiss optics took 15 years alone. Leading-edge fabs: $15–40B, 3–5+ years (US costs ~2x Taiwan). TSMC Arizona: announced 2020, Fab 1 operational early 2025 — 5 years with full TSMC cooperation. China's SMEE: 90nm node, ~15–20 years behind ASML. Equipment self-sufficiency: 13.6%.
- China's distance from autarky: SMIC 5–7 years behind TSMC, yields 20–50% vs. TSMC's 92%. Big Fund III ($47.5B over 15 years) is serious but spread thin. EUV prototype can generate light but not produce chips; target 2028–2030. CSIS: breakthrough claims "project ambition but do not fundamentally alter the strategic reality."

**The Silicon Shield as embryonic, incomplete form:**
- Taiwan embodies both mechanisms: MAD (China needs TSMC chips, invasion destroys fabs) + collective security (US and allies depend on TSMC and would intervene)
- Taiwan produces 60% of all global semiconductors and 90%+ of the most advanced chips. China purchased 53.8% of Taiwanese semiconductor exports (2023).
- But incomplete because Condition 1 is partial (world depends on semiconductors but not yet existentially on AI) and Condition 2 is eroding (CHIPS Act, TSMC Arizona $165B, China's Big Fund III)
- Escape attempts confirm the logic: both great powers recognize the peace trap and are spending billions to escape it. The enormous cost and difficulty of escape is evidence for how powerful the mechanism is.
- Silicon Shield critique (CFR: "does not act as a shield"; Defense Priorities: "weak incentive"; Horster: "Silicon Trap?") — these critiques confirm IPT's analysis: the Shield is *raw material* that needs institutional architecture

**Evidence needed:**
- Must-have: ASML, TSMC, supply chain concentration data (current, 2024–2026)
- Must-have: AI economic integration data — GDP share, corporate adoption, military AI programs
- Must-have: China's semiconductor self-sufficiency status (honest assessment)
- Must-have: Silicon Shield strategic assessments (CFR, Stimson, Defense Priorities — both supporters and critics)
- Must-have: CHIPS Act progress, fab timelines, SMIC capabilities
- Nice-to-have: Chip border-crossing statistic, fab fragility examples

**Transition to next:** "The conditions are plausible. But every theory must face its failure conditions — and the IPT's are both identifiable and urgent."

### 5. When the Argument Breaks (~800 words)

**Purpose:** Failure conditions + the Copeland problem + transition danger. This is the most important section for intellectual credibility. It consolidates material from old Sections 5 and 6.

**Content:**

**Failure conditions (tied to condition breakdowns):**

*Condition 1 fails — AI not yet existential:*
- This is the present situation. IPT doesn't describe the present — it describes the steady state.
- If AI is important but not existential, the cost of losing the supply chain may not exceed geopolitical gains from attacking. The IPT's applicability depends on the trajectory toward Condition 1 being realized.

*Condition 2 fails — the single chain breaks:*
- Autarky: if a nation builds a complete, independent supply chain, it no longer depends on the global chain. Mechanism 1 (MAD) fails. Mechanism 2 (collective security) still holds but is weaker. This is why AI autarky is dangerous for peace and should be actively prevented.
- Replicability: if destroyed chokepoints can be quickly rebuilt, the cost of disruption drops. The argument depends on non-replicability on policy-relevant timescales.

*Irrationality:*
- If leadership ignores rational cost-benefit calculation, no deterrence works — nuclear, conventional, or supply chain. A limitation of all deterrence theory, not specific to IPT.
- But IPT is more robust: costs are *automatic* (invasion destroys fabs) not *discretionary* (someone must choose to retaliate). More resistant to miscalculation.
- Defense Priorities: a determined adversary may accept devastation. Pottinger: "China does not behave in ways which appear rational." Response: the bar for "irrational enough" rises dramatically — equivalent to voluntarily returning to pre-industrial era.

**The Copeland Problem — when peace traps trigger war:**
- Copeland's trade expectations theory: what deters war isn't current dependency but *expectations about future access*. Negative expectations + high dependency = incentive for preventive war.
- Current US export controls against China are generating exactly the negative trade expectations Copeland warns about. By weaponizing chokepoints offensively (Farrell & Newman's framework), Washington creates conditions for the preventive war it wants to avoid.
- Russia-Ukraine confirms Copeland, not interdependence failure: Putin acted when he believed the window was closing. But Russia-Ukraine is NOT an IPT counterexample (5 structural reasons: fait accompli strategy, supplier vs. consumer, energy fungibility, bilateral not multilateral dependency, authoritarian echo chamber).

**The structural comparison table (Energy vs. AI/Semiconductors):**

| Dimension | Energy (Russia-Europe) | AI/Semiconductors (Post-AGI) |
|---|---|---|
| Substitutability | High — Europe switched to LNG in months | Near-zero — no ASML/TSMC alternative; 5–10 years to replicate |
| Retaliation speed | Slow — sanctions need political will | Automatic — invasion destroys fabs instantly |
| Dependency breadth | Bilateral (Russia ↔ Europe) | Global — every tech economy depends on same nodes |
| Who holds leverage | Supplier (Russia) held offensive leverage | Consumer (aggressor) is the dependent party |
| Stockpilability | Strategic petroleum reserves hold years | Chips: ~1 month, heterogeneous, rapidly obsolete |
| Cost magnitude | High but survivable | Civilizational regression |

**The transition period — maximum danger:**
- Conditions not yet met, nations racing for position, exclusionary policies trigger preventive action
- The period before the steady state is when wars are most likely
- Horster (2025): US diversification + Chinese perception of "closing window" = Silicon Trap
- *International Organization* (2025): sanctions erode the interdependence they exploit

**Evidence needed:**
- Must-have: Copeland's trade expectations theory — core argument and evidence (1996 IS paper, 2015 book, 2023 FA piece)
- Must-have: Russia-Ukraine structural comparison — 5 reasons it's not analogous
- Must-have: The structural comparison table (Energy vs. AI/Semiconductors)
- Must-have: Farrell & Newman's framework — to show how offensive use generates Copeland spiral
- Nice-to-have: China's strategic anxiety about supply chain exclusion (CCP statements, SMIC investment rationale)

**Transition to next:** "If this analysis is correct, the policy implications are urgent — and they cut against the current direction of US strategy."

### 6. Policy Implications (~600 words)

**Purpose:** What follows logically from the argument — the "so what" for FA. Separated from failure conditions (old Section 6 combined both), giving each more room to breathe.

**Content:**

- **Stop offensive weaponization of the supply chain.** Current export controls generate exactly the negative trade expectations that trigger preventive war (Copeland). Use chokepoints for collective defense, not unilateral coercion. Farrell & Newman's own 2025 FA recommendation converges: move "toward detente and arms control."

- **Keep adversaries integrated, not excluded.** The counter-intuitive recommendation: China's dependence on TSMC and ASML is a *feature*, not a bug. Policies that push China toward AI autarky make the world more dangerous, not safer. As long as Beijing expects continued access, peace is likely (Copeland 2023 FA).

- **Build a collective defense layer on top of supply chain nodes.** An "Article 5 for the AI supply chain" — treat economic coercion or military action against any chokepoint nation as an attack on all dependent nations. Victor Cha's "collective resilience" concept: allies treat economic coercion against one as attack on all.

- **Nations facing threats should join the supply chain.** Becoming indispensable to the AI supply chain is a rational national security strategy. Not deterrence through weapons but deterrence through indispensability. The IPT playbook for vulnerable nations.

- **Geography and population become irrelevant.** The Netherlands (17 million people) is more strategically important than most large nations because of ASML. In the post-AGI steady state, small nations with chokepoint facilities are safer than large military powers without them.

- **Autarky is the enemy of peace — and should be actively prevented.** A second, independent supply chain allows a major power to attack without self-harm. The single-chain structure is not just economically efficient — it is a security asset.

- **Autocratic lock-in is a real cost.** Once an autocracy is on the supply chain, the world has incentive to keep it stable — locking in its political system. The essay must weigh this cost honestly.

**Evidence needed:**
- Must-have: Copeland 2023 FA on keeping China's expectations positive
- Must-have: Farrell & Newman 2025 FA recommendation (convergence with IPT policy)
- Must-have: China's progress toward autarky (honest assessment — SMIC, Huawei, DeepSeek)
- Nice-to-have: Ukraine energy/mineral resource data, feasibility of supply chain strategy
- Nice-to-have: Historical precedents of nations engineering economic indispensability

**Transition to next:** Conclusion

### 7. Conclusion (~300 words)

**Purpose:** Synthesize and leave the reader with the core insight and its urgency.

**Content:**
- The post-AGI world will be shaped by the structure of the AI supply chain more than by military alliances, nuclear arsenals, or institutional agreements
- The peace-keeping properties of supply chain chokepoints are not automatic — they must be deliberately maintained and protected
- The current trajectory (offensive weaponization, diversification, push toward autarky) is dismantling the very structure that could secure lasting peace
- The window for shaping this outcome is the transition period — before AGI arrives and before the supply chain structure is locked in
- MacAskill & Moorhouse's "century in a decade" — the timeline for preparation is compressed
- Final line: a forward-looking provocation about what's at stake if we get this wrong

**Evidence needed:** None — this is synthesis.

## Counterargument Map

### Counter 1: "Economic interdependence didn't prevent WWI or Russia's invasion of Ukraine"

- **Strength:** High — this is the immediate objection from any IR scholar
- **Addressed in:** Section 3 (vs. liberal peace: three structural distinctions) and Section 5 (Copeland Problem: Russia-Ukraine confirms Copeland, not interdependence failure)
- **Strategy:** Reframe. Russia-Ukraine is not a counterexample because the IPT's preconditions don't hold (5 structural reasons). WWI is addressed via three structural distinctions (existential vs. substitutable, chokepoints vs. diffuse, multilateral vs. bilateral) + Gartzke & Lupu showing WWI began in the *weakly integrated* portion of Europe.
- **Risk if ignored:** Fatal. The essay loses all credibility with the IR community.

### Counter 2: "Open-source AI and model commoditization eliminate chokepoints"

- **Strength:** Medium — compelling on the surface but misunderstands the physical layer
- **Addressed in:** Section 4 (empirical picture, briefly)
- **Strategy:** Rebut. Open-source AI (DeepSeek, Llama, Mistral) still runs on the same hardware supply chain. Software is commoditizing; hardware is not. DeepSeek showed compute-efficient training, but inference at scale still requires massive GPU clusters. The chokepoint is in the physical layer (fabs, lithography, HBM), not the model layer. Jevons paradox: as AI becomes more efficient, demand for compute grows faster than efficiency gains.
- **Risk if ignored:** Medium. Tech-literate readers will immediately think of this.

### Counter 3: "Chokepoints can be weaponized offensively, not just defensively" (Farrell & Newman)

- **Strength:** High — Farrell & Newman's weaponized interdependence framework is the most sophisticated challenge
- **Addressed in:** Section 3 (selective denial vs. total destruction distinction) and Section 5 (Copeland Problem: offensive use generates war-triggering expectations)
- **Strategy:** Partially concede, then distinguish. Yes, chokepoints are currently used offensively (US export controls). But IPT argues this is precisely the wrong strategy. Offensive use generates the negative trade expectations that trigger preventive war (Copeland). In the post-AGI world, the same chokepoints generate defensive collective security because *destruction* (as opposed to selective denial) is self-defeating. The policy recommendation follows: stop using chokepoints offensively; build collective defense around them instead.
- **Risk if ignored:** High. Farrell & Newman are the leading scholars on this exact topic.

### Counter 4: "Nations can achieve AI autarky, breaking the single-chain assumption"

- **Strength:** Medium — China is clearly trying, but far from succeeding
- **Addressed in:** Section 5 (Condition 2 failure: autarky) and Section 4 (China's distance from autarky, empirically)
- **Strategy:** Partially concede, then assess realistically. China's semiconductor self-sufficiency efforts are real but progress is slow (SMIC 5–7 years behind TSMC, equipment self-sufficiency 13.6%). Full AI autarky is not achievable on any near-term timeline. The policy implication: keep China integrated rather than pushing it toward autarky through aggressive export controls.
- **Risk if ignored:** Medium. China hawks will dismiss the thesis if this isn't addressed.

### Counter 5: "The IPT only protects supplier nations — it doesn't prevent suppliers from attacking non-AI powers"

- **Strength:** Medium — a genuine limitation of the framework
- **Addressed in:** Section 5 (failure conditions, briefly) and Section 6 (policy: nations should join the supply chain)
- **Strategy:** Concede honestly. IPT is not a theory of universal peace. It's a theory of why *supplier nations* are safe from invasion. The policy implication: integrate as many nations as possible into the supply chain to extend the protection.
- **Risk if ignored:** Low for FA audience (they expect theories with boundary conditions). But should be acknowledged for intellectual honesty.

## Rhetorical Approach

- **Opening:** Counterintuitive claim — "The discourse on AI is missing a potentially transformative positive outcome." Start with the gap, not the thesis. The reader's first reaction should be curiosity: "wait, how could AGI produce peace?"
- **Core argument first:** The essay's key structural move. Present the full game-theoretic derivation before any evidence. This signals confidence and gives the reader the framework before asking them to evaluate data. Follows Bostrom and Mearsheimer: state the theory, then show it survives scrutiny.
- **Evidence as confirmation, not foundation:** Section 4 marshals evidence *for* the conditions already stated, not as independent premises. The reader already knows the argument — now they see whether it could be true.
- **Opposition engagement:** Concentrated in Section 5 (When It Breaks) rather than scattered throughout. Build the positive case first (Sections 2–4), then show you can survive the strongest attacks. Weaker objections (open-source AI, autarky) handled in Sections 4 and 5.
- **Closing:** Policy urgency — the current trajectory is destroying the peace mechanism. The window is closing. This gives FA editors the "so what" they demand.
- **Voice notes:** No voice samples available. Default to FA institutional register with the deductive clarity of a physicist-turned-strategist. Confident, not hedging. First person sparingly ("I call this the Intelligence Peace Thesis"). No academic signposting.

## Structural Shift

| Old Structure | New Structure | What Changed |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Opening (500 words) | 1. Opening (400 words) | Smaller; previews two conditions, not five axioms |
| 2. Five Axioms (1,000 words) | 2. Core Argument (800 words) | Logic-first, not evidence-first. Two conditions + two mechanisms, not five empirical claims |
| 3. Theorem (600 words) | 3. Why This Is New (500 words) | Comparisons to existing frameworks become their own section |
| 4. Silicon Shield (800 words) | 4. Empirical Picture (1,000 words) | Silicon Shield folded into broader evidence section for both conditions |
| 5. Copeland Problem (700 words) | 5. When It Breaks (800 words) | Copeland + failure conditions + transition danger consolidated |
| 6. Failure + Policy (800 words) | 6. Policy Implications (600 words) | Policy separated from failure conditions |
| 7. Conclusion (300 words) | 7. Conclusion (300 words) | Same |

**The fundamental shift: the old structure was inductive** (evidence → theory → case → challenge → policy). **The new structure is deductive** (theory → differentiation → evidence → limits → policy). This matches the game-theoretic core driving everything.
