## 1. Research Summary

The research confirms that the Intelligence Peace Thesis rests on strong empirical and theoretical foundations — but faces one genuinely dangerous theoretical challenge and one structural limitation that the essay must address head-on. The strongest evidence supports the structural/technical claims: ASML's 100% EUV monopoly, TSMC's ~90% advanced logic share, and the 17–30 year non-replicability timelines are undisputed facts documented by multiple independent sources. The economic integration data (Goldman Sachs's $7T GDP impact, 78% corporate AI adoption, $13.4B Pentagon autonomy budget) establishes that AI is already becoming the substrate of economic and military power, with convergence across all major forecasters.

The most important theoretical finding is that Dale Copeland's trade expectations theory is both the essay's strongest antagonist *and* its most valuable ally. Copeland shows that negative expectations about future supply chain access trigger preventive war — which means current US export controls against China are generating exactly the destabilizing dynamic the IPT warns against. This transforms Copeland from a critic into a supporting argument for the essay's policy recommendations. The essay should embrace Copeland, not rebut him: the danger is not interdependence itself but *exclusionary signals* that flip expectations negative.

The structural limitation is that the Silicon Shield — the essay's primary proof of concept — is under active erosion. TSMC's $165 billion US investment (with no security guarantees for Taiwan), China's EUV prototype, and Defense Priorities' critique all complicate the case. The research recommends framing the Silicon Shield as "embryonic and incomplete" rather than as a working example — which is precisely what the IPT adds: the institutional architecture needed to make chokepoint dependency function as stable deterrence, rather than relying on the raw economic logic alone.

## Section 3: "Why This Is Not Just Another Peace Theory" (~500 words)

### Supporting Evidence

- **Liberal peace baseline (IPT is fundamentally different)**
  - [Tier 1] Russett & Oneal, *Triangulating Peace* (2001) — interdependence has "important pacific benefits"
  - [Tier 1] Gartzke, "The Capitalist Peace," *AJPS* (2007) — financial openness reduces disputes; "capitalism, not democracy, leads to peace"
  - [Tier 1] 2025 systematic review (*Cogent Social Sciences*) — reviews 45 years; finds exit options mitigate asymmetric dependency risks. Key for IPT: AI supply chains have *no* exit options (switching costs too high).
  - IPT's three structural distinctions: existential vs. substitutable dependency, chokepoints vs. diffuse trade, multilateral vs. bilateral harm

- **Nuclear deterrence comparison (IPT is stronger)**
  - IPT deterrence is multilateral (attacking A harms B, C, D, E, and the attacker), not bilateral. No weapons required — operates through economic necessity.
  - Structural not volitional: requires existence of dependency, not willingness to retaliate.
  - Victor Cha's "collective resilience" concept (in Drezner/Farrell/Newman eds., Brookings 2021): allies treat economic coercion against one as attack on all. This is the institutional form of what the IPT predicts will emerge organically.

- **Farrell & Newman validate that chokepoints confer real power (but IPT differs)**
  - [Tier 1] Farrell & Newman, "Weaponized Interdependence," *International Security* 44:1 (2019) — hub-and-spoke networks concentrate power; panopticon + chokepoint effects
  - [Tier 2] Farrell & Newman, *Underground Empire* (2023) — SWIFT, semiconductor chokepoints, EDA tool monopoly documented empirically
  - Key finding: Their empirical work confirms the IPT's foundational premise. If SWIFT chokepoints generate coercive power, AI supply chain chokepoints generate even more.
  - Quote: "States with political authority over the central economic nodes can weaponize these structural advantages for coercive ends."
  - **Critical distinction for the essay:** Farrell & Newman analyze *selective denial* (cutting specific actors off while the network runs). IPT's deterrence depends on *total destruction* (invasion destroys fabs for everyone). Selective denial is an offensive weapon. Total destruction is a collective defense trigger. These are categorically different.

### Counterarguments to Address

- **"Farrell & Newman show chokepoints are offensive, not defensive" (Counter #3)**
  - Best source: [Tier 1] Farrell & Newman 2019; [Tier 2] Farrell & Newman, "The Weaponized World Economy," *FA* (Sep/Oct 2025)
  - Steelman: Chokepoint power has been used offensively (Iran/SWIFT, Huawei, export controls). The same logic applies to AI — chokepoints enable coercion, not peace.
  - Response: Critical distinction — Farrell & Newman analyze *selective denial* (cutting specific actors off while network runs). IPT's deterrence depends on *total destruction* (invasion destroys fabs for everyone). Selective denial is an offensive weapon. Total destruction is a collective defense trigger. These are categorically different.
  - Strength of counter: HIGH — must be addressed explicitly. The distinction between selective denial and total destruction is the essay's key theoretical move.

### Context & Current Discourse

- Farrell & Newman's 2025 FA cover story recommends US should move "toward detente and arms control" — converges with IPT's policy direction
- Farrell's "enshittification of American hegemony" argument (2025): US is degrading its own platform power, driving allies away — IPT offers alternative

### Section-Level Assessment

Evidence strength: **STRONG**
Key risk: The selective denial vs. total destruction distinction must be crisp and convincing. If the reader doesn't see why this distinction matters, the entire theoretical contribution collapses.

## Section 5: "When the Argument Breaks" (~800 words)

### Supporting Evidence

**Failure conditions**

- **Condition 1 fails (AI not yet existential)**
  - This is the present situation — IPT doesn't describe the present
  - Unlikely to persist given trajectory; Goldman/PwC/McKinsey convergence
  - [Tier 1] McKinsey caveat: only 39% report enterprise EBIT impact; Goldman notes 0.2–0.4% current GDP impact
  - Hedge: even narrow AI is already creating substrate dependency; AGI accelerates but isn't required

- **Condition 2 fails — autarky**
  - CHIPS Act progress: TSMC Arizona operational but tiny fraction of Taiwan output (10K vs. 2M+ wafers/month)
  - Intel 18A: yields at 55–65%, delayed to 2026
  - Samsung Taylor: late 2026 at earliest
  - US won't have meaningful independence for 5–10+ years
  - Key tradeoff: "Policies that enhance supply chain resilience may inadvertently weaken the peace-keeping incentive" — the essay should name this explicitly

- **Condition 2 fails — replicability**
  - No evidence of fast replicability. China's equipment self-sufficiency: 13.6%. EUV prototype years from production. CSIS: breakthrough claims "project ambition but do not fundamentally alter the strategic reality."
  - Big Fund III ($47.5B/15 years) is serious but spread across entire equipment ecosystem

- **Irrationality**
  - [Tier 2] Defense Priorities: determined adversary may accept economic devastation
  - Pottinger: "China does not behave in ways which appear rational"
  - Response: No deterrence system is absolute. Nuclear deterrence faces the same problem. But the bar for "irrational enough" rises dramatically in post-AGI world — equivalent to voluntarily returning to pre-industrial era.
  - IPT advantage: costs are automatic (invasion destroys fabs) not discretionary (someone must choose to retaliate) — more robust against miscalculation

**The Copeland Problem**

- **Copeland's trade expectations theory**
  - [Tier 1] Copeland, "Economic Interdependence and War: A Theory of Trade Expectations," *International Security* 20:4 (1996)
  - [Tier 1] Copeland, *Economic Interdependence and War* (Princeton, 2015) — ISA Best Book 2017. Tests theory against great-power conflict 1790–1991.
  - [Tier 2] Copeland, "When Trade Leads to War," *Foreign Affairs* (June 2023) — applies theory to US-China. "Technology decoupling could shift this calculus."
  - Key finding: What deters war isn't current dependency but expectations about *future access*. Negative expectations + high dependency = preventive war.
  - Quote: "If the expected value for trade is lower than the expected value for invasion, war becomes the rational choice, and this is so even when the expected value of invasion is itself negative: war becomes the lesser of two evils."
  - Strength: VERY STRONG — the most important single source for the essay

- **Current US policy is generating negative expectations**
  - [Tier 2] Farrell & Newman, "The Weaponized World Economy," *FA* (2025): "the United States is discovering what it is like to have others do unto it"
  - [Tier 1] "The Decoupling Dilemma," *International Organization* (2025): each sanctions wave "intensifies incentives for targets to build alternative nodes," eroding interdependence
  - [Tier 4] Farrell, "Twilight of Tech Unilateralism" (2025): semiconductor-for-rare-earth deal demonstrates limits of unilateral chokepoint power
  - [Tier 4] Farrell, "America's Plan to Control Global AI" (2025): "strong reasons for skepticism" about export control efficacy

- **Russia-Ukraine confirms Copeland, not interdependence failure**
  - [Tier 1] Hari P. Shyam, *Strategic Analysis* (2023): "Expectations in Moscow that Russia might be losing its economic leverage over Ukraine contributed to Putin's sense that it was 'now or never'"
  - [Tier 1] Kulakevich, *Nationalities Papers* (2024): Russia weaponized Europe's energy dependence as shield against Western retaliation
  - [Tier 1] Vieira, *Contemporary Security Policy* (2023): "weaponized interdependence only works when there are no alternative networks" — Russia had alternatives (China, India, Turkey)
  - Session notes: 5 structural reasons Russia-Ukraine is not analogous (fait accompli strategy, supplier vs. consumer, energy fungibility, bilateral dependency, authoritarian echo chamber)

- **The structural comparison table**

  | Dimension | Energy (Russia-Europe) | AI/Semiconductors (Post-AGI) |
  |---|---|---|
  | Substitutability | High — Europe switched to LNG in months | Near-zero — no ASML/TSMC alternative; 5–10 years to replicate |
  | Retaliation speed | Slow — sanctions need political will, months to bite | Automatic — invasion destroys fabs instantly |
  | Dependency breadth | Bilateral (Russia ↔ Europe) | Global — every tech economy depends on same nodes |
  | Who holds leverage | Supplier (Russia) held offensive leverage | Consumer (aggressor) is the dependent party |
  | Stockpilability | Strategic petroleum reserves hold years | Chips: ~1 month, heterogeneous, rapidly obsolete |
  | Cost magnitude | High but survivable | Civilizational regression |

- **AI supply chains have distinctive features shifting expectations positive**
  - Suppliers profit enormously from continued sales (unlike zero-sum territorial disputes)
  - AI capabilities advance rapidly, creating ongoing demand (unlike static commodities)
  - Knowledge ecosystem is collaborative — cutting off access hurts supplier's own R&D
  - Multiple chokepoints distributed across allied nations (US design, Dutch lithography, Taiwanese fabrication, Japanese chemicals) — collective security incentives
  - Observable indicators of future access (ASML order books, TSMC capacity allocations) make expectations formation more tractable (addresses Shea's time-horizon critique of Copeland)

**The transition period**

- This is the most urgent danger. The theorem holds at equilibrium but breaks during transitions.
  - [Tier 1] Horster (2025): US diversification + Chinese perception of "closing window" = Silicon Trap
  - [Tier 1] "The Decoupling Dilemma," *International Organization* (2025): sanctions erode the interdependence they exploit
  - Current trajectory: US export controls → Chinese negative expectations → preventive countermeasures → spiral

### Counterarguments to Address

- **"Economic interdependence didn't prevent WWI" (Counter #1)**
  - Best source: [Tier 1] Gartzke & Lupu, "Trading on Preconceptions," *International Security* 36:4 (2012)
  - Steelman: Pre-WWI Europe was deeply integrated and went to war anyway.
  - Response: Gartzke & Lupu show WWI began in the *weakly integrated* portion of Europe (Balkans). Crises among highly integrated Western powers were resolved peacefully. Alliance entanglement dragged integrated states into a non-integrated conflict.
  - Strength of counter: HIGH on the surface, but the evidence actually supports the IPT when examined carefully.

- **"Norman Angell was wrong too"**
  - Response: Angell argued war was *futile* (economically irrational), not *impossible*. He was right about futility. The IPT argues something stronger: AI supply chain destruction is not just futile but *self-defeating* — the attacker loses its own AI infrastructure. + Gartzke & Lupu show WWI isn't a clean counterexample. + AI dependencies are qualitatively different (concentration, non-substitutability, switching costs).

### Section-Level Assessment

Evidence strength: **VERY STRONG** — Copeland + Russia-Ukraine + Farrell & Newman convergence
Key risk: The counter-intuitive policy conclusion (keep adversaries *integrated*) must be presented with enough nuance that FA readers don't dismiss it as naive. The Copeland engagement is what gives this credibility.

## 5. Thesis Health Check

- **Original thesis:** The global AI supply chain's chokepoint structure will generate collective security incentives powerful enough to protect supplier nations from foreign invasion — creating a durable, self-reinforcing peace mechanism.

- **Thesis still fully supported?** Yes, with one important qualification.

- **Recommended adjustment:** The essay should explicitly acknowledge that the peace mechanism is *conditional on maintaining positive expectations about future access* (Copeland's insight). The thesis should be stated as: "AI supply chain chokepoints generate collective security *provided that dependent states expect continued access.*" This makes the thesis stronger, not weaker — it gives it empirical grip, falsifiability, and a clear policy lever.

- **Biggest risk to the argument:** The Copeland-inspired "closing window" dynamic. If China perceives that it will be permanently locked out (via export controls and US onshoring), supply chain dependency becomes a war trigger rather than a peace mechanism. Current US policy is generating exactly this risk. The essay must make this the central policy message.

- **Biggest opportunity:** Copeland himself. The essay can position itself as extending Copeland's framework (which he has already published in *Foreign Affairs*) to the AI domain — showing that his theory supports the IPT's *policy recommendations* even while it challenges the IPT's structural optimism. This gives the essay instant theoretical credibility and a built-in "conversation partner" that FA readers will recognize.

## 6. Unresolved Issues

### Evidence Gaps

- **Pax Silica details:** Limited sources on what exactly the State Department's semiconductor diplomacy initiative covers. The policy recommendation for an "Article 5 for the AI supply chain" lacks a specific institutional model.
- **TSMC exact capacity by node:** Proprietary data; estimates only.
- **SMIC actual yields:** Wide range (20–50%) across sources; hard to verify.
- **China's true H100/A100 inventory:** Estimates range from 100K to 1M+.
- **Ukraine supply chain strategy feasibility:** No research conducted on whether Ukraine can realistically engineer chokepoint status. This is the boldest recommendation and the least evidenced. Consider dropping or qualifying heavily.

### Unresolved Contradictions

- **Is the Silicon Shield real?** The evidence is genuinely split. The economic incentives are clear (China can't use captured fabs); the political deterrence is uncertain (CFR and Defense Priorities say it won't stop a determined adversary). Resolution: frame the Shield as proving the *structural logic* while acknowledging the *institutional gap* the IPT addresses.
- **Should China be integrated or excluded?** The essay's theoretical argument (Copeland) says integrate; current US policy says exclude; the original hunch said "democracy-only club." Resolution: the session notes already resolved this — integration with adversaries, not exclusion. The essay should acknowledge the tension explicitly.

### Access Failures

- **Copeland's 2015 book:** Not read in full text. Key arguments derived from the 1996 article, the 2023 FA piece, the ISSF roundtable, and Snyder's review essay. The essay should cite the book as a primary source but not overclaim specific passages.
- **Farrell & Newman FA 2025 cover story:** Paywalled. Key arguments reconstructed from Farrell's Substack, Stimson Center event, and secondary coverage. Same caveat on quotation.

### Questions for the Author

1. **Ukraine playbook:** The research found no strong evidence for feasibility. Should this be kept as a brief speculative coda, or dropped? It's the most novel recommendation but the weakest evidentially.
2. **"Democracy-only" vs. "integrate adversaries":** The session notes resolved this toward integration. Does the essay want to explicitly walk back the original "democracy-only club" suggestion, or simply present the integration argument without referencing the earlier position?
3. **Tone on China:** The essay argues China should be kept integrated (Copeland logic) but also acknowledges China may be irrational (Pottinger). How hawkish should the essay be? Hoover affiliation suggests a certain realpolitik register.

## 7. Discourse Positioning

### What's Already Been Said

- **Farrell & Newman (FA 2025):** Weaponized interdependence is real, dangerous, and requires arms control. This is the most sophisticated existing treatment of chokepoint geopolitics. The IPT must add to, not repeat, this.
- **Copeland (FA 2023):** Trade expectations theory applied to US-China. Warns that technology decoupling could trigger preventive conflict.
- **Defense Priorities (2024):** Silicon Shield is overstated. Semiconductors shouldn't drive Taiwan defense policy.
- **Silicon Shield discourse (2024–25):** Multiple pieces (Stimson, MIT Tech Review, CSET, ISDP) document erosion of the Shield.
- **Aschenbrenner (2024):** AI as existential security race; government nationalization of AGI. Competitive, not cooperative framing.

### Our Unique Contribution

The essay brings three things nobody has said:

1. **The selective denial vs. total destruction distinction.** Farrell & Newman analyze chokepoints used for sanctions (selective denial). Nobody has systematically analyzed why *invasion and destruction* of chokepoint nodes generates a categorically different — and peace-promoting — dynamic.

2. **The deductive derivation from two conditions.** Nobody has formalized the supply-chain-as-peace-mechanism argument with explicit conditions, two mechanisms, and a clean logical result. This gives it the deductive rigor of Mearsheimer or Bostrom.

3. **The Copeland-IPT synthesis.** Nobody has shown that Copeland's trade expectations theory — usually cited as a *challenge* to interdependence-promotes-peace arguments — actually *supports* the IPT's policy recommendations when applied to AI supply chains.

### Who to Engage With

1. **Farrell & Newman** — confirm their framework, extend it, distinguish selective denial from total destruction
2. **Copeland** — embrace as the strongest challenge and show how IPT resolves it
3. **Defense Priorities** — engage the Silicon Shield critique honestly; show IPT addresses their objections
4. **Chris Miller** — cite *Chip War* for empirics but go beyond supply chain description to security theory
