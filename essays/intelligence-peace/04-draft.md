# The Intelligence Peace Thesis
*Locking In Peace In A Post-AGI World*

**By Liu He**

The discourse on artificial intelligence is dominated by catastrophic warnings: alignment failures, bioweapons, mass unemployment, and the collapse of democracy. Nearly every major analysis frames AI as an existential threat requiring containment, regulation, or international control regimes. But this catastrophist framing obscures a potentially transformative positive outcome that no one is discussing: the possibility that advanced AI could make the world fundamentally safer.

The argument I will present—the Intelligence Peace Thesis—is that the global AI supply chain's structure will generate collective security incentives powerful enough to protect supplier nations from foreign invasion, creating a durable, self-reinforcing peace mechanism. Unlike nuclear deterrence, this protection operates through economic necessity rather than military threat. Unlike liberal theories of commercial peace, it depends on existential rather than substitutable dependencies. The mechanism emerges from the supply chain's architecture: a few irreplaceable chokepoints that every advanced economy needs to access, creating a situation where attacking any supplier nation would be economically suicidal.

This argument rests on two conditions and proceeds through two mechanisms to a single conclusion. If the conditions hold—and the evidence suggests they increasingly will—then the post-AGI world will be structured around supply chain protection rather than military alliances, making geography and population size irrelevant to national security. The policy implications are urgent and counterintuitive: current attempts to weaponize AI supply chains or achieve technological autarky are dismantling the very structure that could secure lasting peace.

## The Core Argument

The Intelligence Peace Thesis derives from two conditions that together generate an unprecedented form of collective security:

**Condition 1: Existential AI dependency.** The world has developed a dependency on AI services so fundamental that losing access would not merely reduce economic growth or military capabilities—it would trigger civilizational regression. No conceivable geopolitical gain could compensate for such losses.

**Condition 2: Irreplaceable global integration.** AI services are produced through a single, globally integrated supply chain dominated by irreplaceable chokepoints. Specific facilities in specific nations control bottleneck technologies that cannot be replicated quickly. The entire global chain breaks if any critical node is destroyed.

Here is the crucial foundation: **global AI supply chain integration is permanent, not transitional.** It is impossible for any single nation to onshore the entire chain. The complexity spans thousands of companies across dozens of countries, from rare earth mining to chip design to fabrication to packaging to assembly. Even the world's two superpowers, despite spending hundreds of billions on supply chain independence, remain years away from meaningful self-sufficiency and may never achieve it at reasonable cost.

From these conditions flow two mechanisms that together create the Intelligence Peace:

**Mechanism 1: Mutually Assured Destruction through supply chain dependency.** In a world where every nation depends existentially on AI (Condition 1), any attack on a supply chain chokepoint destroys what the attacker itself needs to survive (Condition 2). This is not ordinary cost-of-war calculation—the cost is civilizational regression while the gain is at most territorial or political. Even capturing a facility intact fails because it cannot operate without its global supply network, other nations cut off inputs, and modern semiconductor fabrication is a network asset, not a standalone prize.

**Mechanism 2: Collective security without collective action problems.** Every AI-dependent nation has an overwhelming *individual* incentive to defend any chokepoint under attack. This eliminates the three classic problems of collective security: the free-rider problem (each nation is individually harmed, not just the bilateral partner), the credibility problem (nations act in material self-interest, which is always credible), and the coordination problem (the incentive is individually rational and requires no prior agreement).

The result is automatic: any nation attacking a supply chain chokepoint faces both self-inflicted economic devastation and a coalition of every other dependent nation. The attacker is deterred by self-harm and overwhelmed by collective resistance. Supplier nations become "too important to fail"—protected not by military strength, alliance relationships, or geographical barriers, but by their structural position in the global economy.

This is the Intelligence Peace: a prediction about the post-AGI steady state where supply chain structure, not military balance, determines which nations are safe from invasion.

## Why This Is Not Just Another Peace Theory

This argument will sound familiar to students of international relations, but it differs fundamentally from every existing framework in ways that determine whether it succeeds or fails.

**The Intelligence Peace is not the liberal peace.** The commercial peace tradition from Norman Angell to Bruce Russett argues that economic interdependence reduces the probability of conflict by raising its costs. But liberal peace depends on *substitutable* dependencies—trade partnerships that can be replaced, supply sources that can be diversified, economic relationships that create costs but not existential vulnerabilities. The Intelligence Peace depends on *existential* dependencies where alternatives do not exist and substitution is impossible on relevant timescales.

Consider three critical distinctions: First, ordinary trade involves substitutable goods that can be sourced elsewhere, stockpiled, or simply done without during wartime. AI infrastructure dependency is existential—there is no substitute for advanced semiconductors, no meaningful stockpile, and no "doing without" in a post-AGI world. Second, traditional commercial peace operates through diffuse trade relationships distributed across many partners. The Intelligence Peace operates through concentrated chokepoints—single points of failure that break entire supply networks. Third, in liberal peace theory, third-party nations are weakly affected by bilateral trade disruptions. In the Intelligence Peace, all nations depend on the same critical nodes, making any attack on a chokepoint a threat to everyone.

The standard counterexample—that deeply integrated pre-1914 Europe went to war anyway—fails on all three distinctions. As Erik Gartzke and Yonatan Lupu demonstrated, World War I began in the *weakly integrated* portion of Europe (the Balkans), not between the highly interdependent Western powers, who resolved their crises peacefully until alliance commitments dragged them into a conflict that started elsewhere.

**The Intelligence Peace is not nuclear deterrence.** Nuclear deterrence protects through threatened retaliation; the Intelligence Peace protects through structural necessity. Nuclear deterrence is bilateral (A threatens B with destruction if B attacks); the Intelligence Peace is multilateral (attacking any supplier harms everyone simultaneously). Nuclear deterrence depends on credibility—the willingness to follow through on threats—while the Intelligence Peace depends only on dependency structure. Nuclear deterrence protects major powers with arsenals; the Intelligence Peace protects any nation hosting critical supply chain infrastructure, regardless of size or military capability. The Netherlands, with 17 million people, becomes more strategically important than most large nations because ASML's extreme ultraviolet lithography machines are irreplaceable and cannot be produced elsewhere.

**The Intelligence Peace is not weaponized interdependence.** Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman's influential framework analyzes how states use chokepoint control for coercive leverage—cutting specific targets off from networks while keeping those networks running for everyone else. Their work confirms the Intelligence Peace's foundational insight about chokepoint power, but the mechanisms operate in opposite directions.

Farrell and Newman analyze *selective denial*: using control over network nodes to exclude particular actors while preserving access for others. This is an offensive weapon that creates asymmetric leverage. The Intelligence Peace depends on *total destruction*: military attacks that destroy production facilities for everyone, including the attacker. This triggers collective defense because no one benefits from the destruction.

The distinction is categorical. Selective denial allows a hegemon to maintain network benefits while excluding rivals—a form of economic coercion. Total destruction eliminates network benefits for everyone—a form of mutual suicide. Current U.S. export controls on semiconductors to China represent selective denial and generate the offensive dynamics Farrell and Newman describe. A Chinese military attack on TSMC fabrication facilities would represent total destruction and trigger the defensive dynamics the Intelligence Peace predicts.

## The Empirical Picture

The abstract argument is logically sound, but its validity depends on whether the two conditions actually hold. The evidence suggests they increasingly do.

**Evidence for Condition 1: AI is becoming existentially important.** Artificial intelligence is not merely another technological advance—it is becoming the substrate of economic and military power across all advanced societies. Goldman Sachs estimates AI could drive a $7 trillion increase in global GDP, representing a 7% boost to the world economy. PwC projects a 15 percentage point increase by 2035, calling the impact "on par with 19th century industrialization." McKinsey forecasts $4.4 trillion in annual corporate profits from AI, potentially reaching $23 trillion annually by 2040. These are not marginal improvements but civilizational-scale shifts.

Corporate adoption confirms the trajectory: 78% of organizations now use AI in some capacity, rising to 99% among Fortune 500 companies. AI investment already surpasses the dot-com boom's contribution to GDP growth, according to the St. Louis Federal Reserve. Military integration is equally dramatic: the Pentagon's FY2026 autonomy budget reaches $13.4 billion, while Georgetown's Center for Security and Emerging Technology has documented 343 People's Liberation Army contracts for AI equipment. Both superpowers are investing as if AI dominance equals military dominance—a revealed preference more telling than official doctrine.

The timeline for artificial general intelligence—systems that match or exceed human performance across all cognitive tasks—has compressed dramatically. The largest survey of AI researchers, conducted by Katja Grace and colleagues, found median expectations for AGI by 2047, a timeline that shifted 13 years earlier in just one year of technological progress. Metaculus, the forecasting platform, puts 50% probability on AGI by 2033. William MacAskill and Fin Moorhouse estimate that advanced AI systems could increase effective research capability by factors ranging from 10^7 to 10^14, meaning states without AI access would fall permanently and irreversibly behind.

Current measured GDP impact remains modest—Goldman Sachs notes only 0.2-0.4% contribution so far. But this reflects the early stage of deployment, not the trajectory. The investment patterns, corporate adoption rates, and military integration demonstrate revealed preferences: every major economy is betting that AI access will be existential, not optional.

**Evidence for Condition 2: Irreplaceable chokepoints exist and cannot be quickly replicated.** The global AI supply chain exhibits precisely the concentration and non-replicability that Condition 2 requires. The bottlenecks are not distributed across many potential suppliers but concentrated in specific companies in specific countries that cannot be replaced on any policy-relevant timeline.

ASML, the Dutch company, holds 100% of the market for extreme ultraviolet lithography machines required to manufacture the most advanced semiconductors. These machines contain over 100,000 components, weigh 180 tons, cost $200-380 million each, and depend on supply chains spanning 5,150+ companies across dozens of countries. No competitor exists anywhere in the world. ASML's technology represents 17-30 years of accumulated R&D and over EUR 6 billion in investment. The specialized optics alone, produced by Germany's Zeiss, took 15 years to develop.

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) controls 64-67% of global foundry capacity and approximately 90% of production at the most advanced process nodes (5nm and below) that enable cutting-edge AI systems. TSMC's advanced packaging technology, called CoWoS, is fully booked through 2026, with NVIDIA claiming over 60% of capacity. South Korea's SK Hynix and Samsung together control over 90% of global high-bandwidth memory production, the specialized chips that enable large-scale AI training and inference.

These chokepoints cannot be replicated quickly. Leading-edge semiconductor fabrication facilities cost $15-40 billion and require 3-5+ years to construct, with U.S. costs running approximately double those in Taiwan. TSMC's Arizona facility, announced in 2020 with full company cooperation and U.S. government support, only became operational in early 2025—a five-year timeline despite unprecedented investment and political priority.

China's efforts to achieve semiconductor independence illustrate the barriers to replication. Despite massive state investment, China's Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation (SMIC) remains 5-7 years behind TSMC technologically, with yields of 20-50% compared to TSMC's 92% at equivalent process nodes. China's equipment self-sufficiency rate stands at just 13.6% despite decades of effort. The country's domestically produced lithography equipment, made by Shanghai Micro Electronics Equipment, operates at 90-nanometer processes—approximately 15-20 years behind ASML's current capabilities.

China's recent EUV prototype represents genuine progress but confirms the timeline for true independence. The prototype can generate the required extreme ultraviolet light but cannot yet produce functional semiconductors, with realistic targets for production systems in the 2028-2030 timeframe. China's Big Fund III, announced with $47.5 billion over 15 years, represents serious commitment but is spread across an entire ecosystem of equipment manufacturers, materials suppliers, and design companies.

The Center for Strategic and International Studies concluded in 2025 that Chinese breakthrough claims "project ambition but do not fundamentally alter the strategic reality." The lack of access to advanced AI chips remains "the most significant challenge" facing Chinese AI companies, and the U.S.-China compute advantage represents "almost certainly the largest single advantage the United States enjoys" in strategic competition.

**Taiwan's Silicon Shield as embryonic Intelligence Peace.** Taiwan embodies both mechanisms in incomplete form, demonstrating the logic while revealing the institutional gaps the Intelligence Peace would fill. Taiwan produces 60% of all global semiconductors and over 90% of the most advanced chips required for AI systems. China, despite its ambitions for technological independence, purchased 53.8% of Taiwan's semiconductor exports in 2023, creating the mutual dependency that Mechanism 1 requires.

The collective security logic is also visible: the United States, Japan, South Korea, and European nations all depend on Taiwanese semiconductor production and would face economic catastrophe if it were disrupted. This creates powerful incentives for intervention that have nothing to do with democratic solidarity or alliance commitments—pure material self-interest in maintaining supply chain access.

But the Silicon Shield remains incomplete because neither condition is fully met. Condition 1 fails because the world depends on semiconductors for current technology but not yet existentially on AI for civilizational function. Condition 2 is eroding as both superpowers invest in supply chain diversification: TSMC's $165 billion U.S. investment represents the largest foreign direct investment in American history, while China's Big Fund III aims to reduce import dependence.

The escape attempts themselves confirm the underlying logic. Both great powers recognize the peace trap created by supply chain concentration and are spending enormous sums to escape it. The fact that escape requires such massive investment and extended timelines—five years for a single TSMC fabrication facility with full cooperation—demonstrates how powerful the mechanism could be if properly institutionalized and protected.

Current critiques of the Silicon Shield actually support the Intelligence Peace thesis. The Council on Foreign Relations concluded that Taiwan's semiconductor dominance "does not act as a shield that can protect Taiwan from an attack" because the deterrent effect is incomplete and unreliable. Defense Priorities argued that semiconductor dependency provides only a "weak incentive" and "weak deterrent" against a determined adversary. These assessments identify exactly the institutional gaps the Intelligence Peace would fill: the raw material of supply chain dependency exists, but it lacks the collective defense architecture to make deterrence reliable.

## When the Argument Breaks

Every theory must confront its failure conditions. The Intelligence Peace faces three categories of breakdown: condition failures, the transition period danger, and the fundamental challenge posed by trade expectations theory.

**When conditions fail.** If Condition 1 fails—if AI remains important but not existentially necessary—then the cost of losing supply chain access may not exceed the gains from successful conquest. This is largely the present situation. The Intelligence Peace describes a future equilibrium, not current reality. Its applicability depends on the trajectory toward existential AI dependency being realized, which the evidence suggests is underway but not complete.

If Condition 2 fails through successful autarky, a major power could attack supply chain nodes without harming itself. This is why AI autarky represents a threat to the Intelligence Peace mechanism and why policies that push adversaries toward self-sufficiency may be counterproductive. Current U.S. export controls on semiconductors to China, while intended to maintain American technological advantages, may ultimately undermine the supply chain dependencies that could prevent conflict.

Alternatively, if destroyed chokepoints can be quickly rebuilt or replaced, the disruption costs drop below the threshold needed for deterrence. The argument depends on non-replicability persisting on timescales relevant to political decision-making—years to decades, not months.

**The irrationality problem affects all deterrence theories equally.** If leadership ignores rational cost-benefit analysis, no deterrence mechanism works, whether nuclear, conventional, or economic. Critics like Defense Priorities note that a sufficiently determined adversary may accept economic devastation, while former National Security Council official Matt Pottinger observes that "China does not behave in ways which appear rational" to Western observers.

But the Intelligence Peace enjoys a structural advantage over nuclear deterrence: costs are automatic rather than discretionary. Nuclear retaliation requires someone to make the decision to launch, creating credibility problems ("would France really risk Paris over Estonia?"). Supply chain retaliation is automatic—invasion destroys fabrication facilities regardless of anyone's decision to respond. This makes the Intelligence Peace more robust against miscalculation, even if not immune to genuinely irrational actors.

**The Copeland Problem: when peace traps trigger wars.** The most serious challenge comes from Dale Copeland's trade expectations theory, which demonstrates that interdependence can cause wars under specific conditions. Copeland's insight, validated across great power conflicts from 1790 to 1991, is that what matters for peace is not current dependency but *expectations about future access to trade*. High current dependency combined with negative expectations about future access creates incentives for preventive war—better to fight now while still strong than accept inevitable decline.

Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine confirms Copeland's framework rather than disproving interdependence theory. As strategic analyst Shyam Hari P. documented, "expectations in Moscow that Russia might be losing its economic leverage over Ukraine contributed to Putin's sense that it was 'now or never.'" Russia weaponized Europe's energy dependence as a shield against Western retaliation, but declining Ukrainian dependence and expanding NATO integration created negative expectations about future leverage.

Current U.S. policy toward China's AI supply chain access is generating exactly the negative trade expectations that Copeland's theory warns against. Export controls, investment restrictions, and aggressive technology transfer limitations signal to Beijing that future access will be restricted or eliminated entirely. Combined with high current dependency on American chip design tools and Taiwan's foundry capacity, this creates the classic Copeland trap: fight now while still capable, or accept permanent technological subordination.

The structural comparison with Russia-Ukraine reveals why energy interdependence failed while AI supply chain interdependence might succeed:

| Dimension | Energy (Russia-Europe) | AI/Semiconductors (Post-AGI) |
|---|---|---|
| Substitutability | High—Europe switched to LNG in months | Near-zero—no ASML/TSMC alternative; 5-10 years to replicate |
| Retaliation speed | Slow—sanctions need political will, months to bite | Automatic—invasion destroys fabs instantly |
| Dependency breadth | Bilateral (Russia ↔ Europe) | Global—every tech economy depends on same nodes |
| Who holds leverage | Supplier (Russia) held offensive leverage | Consumer (aggressor) is the dependent party |
| Stockpilability | Strategic petroleum reserves hold years | Chips: ~1 month, heterogeneous, rapidly obsolete |
| Cost magnitude | High but survivable | Civilizational regression |

The key difference is that AI supply chains have distinctive features that tend to keep expectations positive rather than negative. Suppliers profit enormously from continued sales, unlike zero-sum territorial disputes. AI capabilities advance rapidly, creating ongoing demand unlike static commodities. The knowledge ecosystem is collaborative—cutting off access hurts suppliers' own research and development. Multiple chokepoints are distributed across allied nations (U.S. design, Dutch lithography, Taiwanese fabrication, Japanese specialty chemicals), creating collective security incentives. Observable indicators like ASML order books and TSMC capacity allocations make expectations formation more tractable than in the energy sector.

**The transition period represents maximum danger.** The Intelligence Peace describes an equilibrium state, but the pathway to that equilibrium poses significant risks. This is the period when conditions are not yet fully met, major powers are racing for strategic position, and exclusionary policies could trigger the preventive action they aim to prevent.

Tim Horster's analysis warns of the "Silicon Trap": U.S. diversification efforts combined with Chinese perceptions of a "closing window" could create incentives for immediate action rather than long-term accommodation. The 2025 study "The Decoupling Dilemma" notes that each wave of sanctions "intensifies incentives for targets to build alternative nodes," progressively eroding the interdependence that generated American leverage in the first place.

The transition period is when wars are most likely because the peace mechanism is incomplete but the strategic stakes are already high. Managing this transition requires exactly the opposite policies from those currently pursued: maintaining integration rather than forcing decoupling, keeping expectations positive rather than negative, building collective defense arrangements rather than unilateral offensive capabilities.

## Policy Implications

If this analysis is correct, the policy implications are urgent and cut directly against current strategic directions. The Intelligence Peace offers a path to lasting security, but only if the underlying structure is deliberately maintained and protected rather than dismantled through short-term competitive logic.

**Stop weaponizing the supply chain offensively.** Current U.S. export controls on semiconductors to China represent exactly the kind of selective denial that generates the negative trade expectations Copeland warns against. By using chokepoint control as an offensive weapon—cutting China off while maintaining access for allies—Washington creates conditions for the preventive war it seeks to avoid. This policy may provide temporary strategic advantages, but it undermines the long-term peace mechanism that could make such competition irrelevant.

Henry Farrell and Abraham Newman, the scholars who coined the term "weaponized interdependence," have themselves moved toward this conclusion. Their 2025 Foreign Affairs cover story recommends that the United States move "toward detente and arms control" rather than continued escalation of economic competition. They argue that America is discovering "what it is like to have others do unto it" as targeted nations develop counter-chokepoints and alternative networks.

**Keep adversaries integrated, not excluded.** The counter-intuitive but essential recommendation is that China's dependence on TSMC and ASML represents a *feature* of the current system, not a bug requiring correction. Policies that push China toward AI autarky make the world more dangerous by eliminating the mutual dependencies that generate peace incentives.

As Copeland argued in his own 2023 Foreign Affairs analysis of U.S.-China relations, "as long as Beijing expects continued access" to international technology networks, "peace is likely." The policy challenge is maintaining those positive expectations while managing legitimate security concerns about technology transfer and military applications.

This does not mean abandoning all export controls or technology transfer restrictions. But it means being selective and strategic rather than comprehensive, focusing on genuinely military applications rather than broad categories, and maintaining clear pathways for continued commercial integration. The goal should be managed competition within continued interdependence, not technological divorce.

**Build collective defense architecture around supply chain nodes.** The Intelligence Peace's mechanisms operate automatically through economic incentives, but they would be more reliable and credible with institutional reinforcement. An "Article 5 for the AI supply chain" would formalize what economic incentives already suggest: that attacks on critical supply chain infrastructure represent threats to all dependent nations, not just bilateral partners.

Victor Cha's concept of "collective resilience" points toward this architecture: allies should treat economic coercion against any supply chain partner as an attack on all members of the network. The G7's 2023 Hiroshima communique included language opposing "weaponized economic dependencies," representing embryonic movement toward collective defense principles.

**Nations facing security threats should pursue supply chain integration.** Becoming indispensable to the global AI supply chain represents a rational national security strategy, especially for smaller nations that cannot achieve security through military strength or alliance relationships. This is deterrence through indispensability rather than deterrence through punishment—making oneself too valuable to attack rather than too dangerous to attack.

The Netherlands provides the model: 17 million people, no nuclear weapons, modest conventional military, but strategically safer than most major powers because ASML's lithography monopoly makes Dutch territory essential to global technological civilization. Any attack on the Netherlands would immediately trigger economic catastrophe for every advanced economy, creating powerful intervention incentives that operate independently of NATO commitments or democratic solidarity.

**Geography and population become irrelevant to security.** In the post-AGI steady state, small nations hosting critical supply chain infrastructure will be safer than large military powers without such infrastructure. Traditional metrics of national power—population, territory, natural resources, military spending—matter less than structural position in essential supply networks.

This represents a fundamental shift in the international system comparable to the rise of nuclear weapons. During the Cold War, nuclear arsenals allowed relatively small powers like Britain and France to maintain strategic relevance despite declining conventional capabilities. The Intelligence Peace extends this logic: any nation can achieve effective security guarantees by hosting irreplaceable supply chain infrastructure.

**Autarky is the enemy of peace and should be actively prevented.** The existence of multiple, independent AI supply chains would allow major powers to attack without self-harm, eliminating Mechanism 1 entirely. A world with both U.S.-allied and Chinese-led supply chains would be fundamentally less stable than a world with a single, globally integrated chain that all powers depend on.

This implies that current efforts to build "friend-shoring" or "ally-shoring" networks, while understandable from a competitive perspective, may be counterproductive from a peace perspective. The most dangerous outcome would be successful technological bipolarity—two complete, independent supply chains that enable conflict without economic self-destruction.

**Acknowledge the autocratic lock-in problem honestly.** Once an authoritarian regime becomes integrated into critical supply chain infrastructure, the world develops economic incentives to maintain its stability regardless of its domestic political practices. The Intelligence Peace may lock in existing political systems by making their disruption economically catastrophic for other nations.

This represents a genuine cost that must be weighed against the benefits of reduced military conflict. Luke Drago and Rudolf Laine's analysis of the "Intelligence Curse" draws parallels to resource curse theory: the same structural position that generates peace incentives can also enable authoritarian consolidation by making external pressure economically self-defeating.

But the alternative—a world of competing technological blocs armed with artificial general intelligence—may pose even greater risks to human freedom and security. The policy challenge is designing institutions that preserve peace benefits while creating space for internal political development and human rights advocacy.

## Conclusion

The post-AGI world will be shaped by the architecture of artificial intelligence supply chains more than by military alliances, nuclear arsenals, or international institutions. The peace-keeping properties of supply chain chokepoints are not automatic—they emerge from specific structural conditions and must be deliberately maintained through policy choices that often run counter to immediate competitive instincts.

The current trajectory moves in exactly the wrong direction. Offensive weaponization of supply chains generates the negative expectations that trigger preventive conflicts. Diversification efforts aimed at achieving technological autarky eliminate the mutual dependencies that could prevent such conflicts. The institutional architecture needed to make supply chain deterrence reliable and credible remains underdeveloped.

The window for shaping this outcome is the transition period—after the strategic importance of AI supply chains has become clear but before their structure has crystallized into permanent form. As William MacAskill and Fin Moorhouse observe, we may be experiencing a "century in a decade" of compressed technological and political change, making the timeline for institutional adaptation shorter than traditional diplomacy assumes.

The Intelligence Peace Thesis offers a path toward lasting security based on structural economic logic rather than military competition or arms control agreements. But realizing this outcome requires abandoning the comfortable assumptions that competition requires separation, that security requires independence, and that peace can be achieved through strength alone. In the post-AGI world, peace may depend instead on making everyone too interdependent to fight—and too valuable to destroy.
