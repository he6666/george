# Chapter 1: The AI Substrate

---

In 2021, Holden Karnofsky — co-founder of GiveWell, co-CEO of Open Philanthropy, one of the most methodical thinkers in effective altruism — published a series of essays arguing that we are living in the most important century in human history. His argument was disarmingly simple. For 99% of human existence, the global economy grew at less than 0.1% per year. Since the Industrial Revolution, it has grown at over 2%. Run that growth rate forward for ten thousand years and you need to convert every atom in the Milky Way into economic output. The math does not work. Either growth slows back to its historical crawl, or something happens — something that changes the trajectory of civilization permanently. Karnofsky's claim was that artificial intelligence is the most likely candidate for that something, and that the window in which humans can shape its trajectory is closing fast.

He was not alone in this view. Nick Bostrom's *Superintelligence* had laid the philosophical groundwork a decade earlier: intelligence is substrate-independent; it can run on carbon neurons or silicon transistors; what matters is the computational architecture, not the biological form. If machine intelligence crosses a critical capability threshold, it may improve itself faster than humans can respond — creating what Bostrom called a "decisive strategic advantage" for whoever controls it. William MacAskill and Fin Moorhouse, writing in March 2025, went further. They argued that even without recursive self-improvement, even if compute scaling stalls, AI that can substitute for human researchers creates a feedback loop potent enough to compress "a century's worth of progress into a decade." MacAskill stated publicly that he takes seriously the possibility of human-level AI within three to five years — and takes the idea of an intelligence explosion even more seriously.

These are not fringe positions. They represent a tradition of thinking — rooted in effective altruism, developed over two decades at Oxford, Cambridge, Berkeley, and the research organizations they spawned — that has been thinking about transformative AI longer and more rigorously than any other intellectual community. What this tradition understands, and what mainstream institutions are only beginning to grasp, is that AI is not a sector. It is not an industry the way semiconductors or pharmaceuticals are industries. It is becoming the substrate — the foundational layer on which economic production, military power, scientific discovery, and eventually governance itself will run.

---

The mainstream institutions are catching up. Goldman Sachs projects that generative AI could raise global GDP by 7% — roughly $7 trillion — and lift productivity growth by 1.5 percentage points over a decade. PwC estimates AI's total economic contribution at $15.7 trillion by 2030. McKinsey puts the long-run figure at $15.5 to $22.9 trillion annually by 2040, with generative AI alone adding $2.6 to $4.4 trillion per year. These are staggering numbers. They converge on the same conclusion from three independent analytical traditions: AI is projected to become the single largest driver of global economic growth in human history.

But intellectual honesty demands acknowledging the gap between projection and reality. As of early 2026, AI's measured contribution to GDP growth is essentially zero. Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Jan Hatzius said as much in January: AI investment contributed "basically zero" to US GDP growth in 2025. A follow-up Goldman analysis in March found "no meaningful relationship between productivity and AI adoption at the economy-wide level." Only 10% of S&P 500 management teams could quantify AI's impact on specific use cases. A mere 1% could quantify its impact on earnings.

This gap is not evidence against the substrate thesis. It is, in fact, its signature. General-purpose technologies — technologies so fundamental that they reshape the entire economy — follow a well-documented pattern of delayed impact. Commercial electricity became available in 1882. Measurable productivity gains did not appear until the 1920s, more than thirty years later. The reason was not that electricity was overhyped. It was that the entire industrial infrastructure — factory layouts, power distribution networks, management practices, worker training — had to be rebuilt around the new technology before its potential could be realized. The economist Paul David called this the "dynamo and the computer" analogy. The internet followed the same trajectory: dismissed as economically irrelevant through most of the 1990s, it became the backbone of the global economy within a decade of its apparent failure to move the needle.

The trajectory evidence for AI is unmistakable. Global venture capital investment in AI surged from $114 billion in 2024 to $211 billion in 2025 — an 85% year-over-year increase. AI now captures 53% of all global VC deal value. The five largest hyperscalers — Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta, and Oracle — spent $443 billion on capital expenditure in 2025, with roughly three-quarters going directly to AI infrastructure. Goldman Sachs projects cumulative hyperscaler capex of $1.15 trillion between 2025 and 2027. Companies are not investing at this scale because they are confused about the technology's potential. They are investing because they believe — with good reason — that falling behind in AI adoption will be an existential business risk within the decade.

The current GDP impact is small. The trajectory is vertical. Karnofsky's framework explains this precisely: the Most Important Century thesis does not require AI to be transformative today. It requires that the trajectory is clear and the stakes are civilizational. By that standard, the evidence is overwhelming.

---

The clearest evidence that states take AI seriously is not what their economists project. It is what their militaries do.

In January 2026, the United States executed Operation Absolute Resolve — the military capture of Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro. It was the first major military operation in which AI played a central, publicly acknowledged role. More than 150 aircraft and drones were deployed, integrated with space-based assets and cyber operations. Anthropic's Claude, operating through Palantir's defense platform, was used for mission planning and analysis. The Maven Smart System provided AI-powered image processing and tactical support. RQ-170 Sentinel stealth drones maintained near-continuous AI-assisted surveillance over Caracas, mapping facilities, movement patterns, and air defense layouts in real time. The operation succeeded. A head of state was captured. AI was not an accessory to the mission. It was infrastructure.

Weeks later, the scale escalated dramatically. A joint US-Israeli offensive against Iran delivered nearly 900 strikes on Iranian targets in the first twelve hours. AI systems accelerated the kill chain — target identification, legal review, strike execution — to speeds that, as Fortune reported, operated "faster than the speed of thought." Nature published an article titled "Iran war heralds era of AI-powered bombing." Secretary of War Pete Hegseth confirmed that the United States was deploying autonomous systems and AI-enabled technologies across the campaign.

These are not future scenarios. They are facts. AI-enabled warfare is now the norm.

The Pentagon's FY2026 budget makes the institutional commitment explicit. For the first time, the Department of Defense created a dedicated budget line for AI and autonomy: $13.4 billion. Of this, $9.4 billion goes to unmanned aerial vehicles, $1.7 billion to maritime autonomous systems, $1.7 billion to DARPA, $734 million to underwater capabilities, and $210 million to autonomous ground vehicles. The Chief Digital and Artificial Intelligence Office awarded $200 million in contracts to commercial AI providers — Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and xAI — in 2025 alone.

China's approach is structurally different but equally revealing. Beijing's official defense budget is approximately $230 billion, but real military expenditure — accounting for off-budget spending — is estimated at $330 to $450 billion. More significant than the headline number is the Military-Civil Fusion strategy, which has blurred the line between civilian AI companies and defense procurement. Analysis of PLA procurement contracts reveals that the majority of suppliers for AI-related military capabilities are now civilian companies and universities, not traditional state-owned defense enterprises. China is not building a parallel military AI ecosystem. It is fusing its civilian AI industry directly into its military apparatus.

The United Kingdom's 2025 Strategic Defence Review positions AI as a "core enabler" of operational effectiveness. Israel has been the most operationally aggressive adopter, deploying AI-assisted targeting systems in Gaza and creating a dedicated Mafat research unit for AI and autonomy. Every major military power — and many mid-tier ones — is restructuring around AI. The revealed preference is unambiguous: states that bet their security on AI believe, at the most consequential level of national decision-making, that AI is becoming the substrate of military power.

---

The question of *when* AI reaches transformative capability has been the subject of the most rigorous forecasting effort in the history of technology assessment, driven largely by the effective altruism research ecosystem.

The most concrete scenario is AI 2027, published by Daniel Kokotajlo, a former OpenAI governance researcher, and Eli Lifland, who ranks first on the RAND Forecasting Initiative's all-time leaderboard. Their scenario traces, period by period, how current capability trends could produce AI systems that match and then exceed human cognitive performance by 2027. It is not a prediction. It is a detailed, quantitative scenario — informed by trend extrapolation, wargaming, and expert consultation — that makes near-term transformative AI concrete and testable rather than vague.

The empirical backbone of the timeline debate is provided by Epoch AI, which tracks AI capability trends with the rigor of an econometrics institute. Their data shows that the training compute used in frontier AI models has grown at 5x per year since 2020. The cost of training frontier models has grown at 3.5x per year. By 2027, the largest models could cost over $1 billion to train. As of June 2025, more than 30 publicly announced models from 12 developers have exceeded 10^25 FLOP of training compute. The trend is exponential and, so far, unbroken.

METR — Model Evaluation and Threat Research — provides the most direct measure of what these capability gains mean in practice. METR measures the length of software tasks that AI agents can complete independently. This "time horizon" metric has been doubling roughly every seven months for the past six years. As of February 2026, the best-performing model achieved a 50% time horizon of 14 hours and 30 minutes — meaning it can autonomously complete software engineering tasks that take experienced human developers about 14.5 hours, at a 50% success rate. If the doubling trend continues, AI agents will be able to complete multi-day autonomous tasks within one to two years.

Ajeya Cotra's biological anchors framework, developed at Open Philanthropy in 2020 and updated in 2022, remains the most methodologically rigorous timeline forecast. Using multiple empirical anchors — evolutionary compute, lifetime learning compute, neural network scaling — she estimates a 15% chance of transformative AI by 2030, 35% by 2036, and 50% by 2040. The largest survey of AI researchers, conducted by Grace et al. in 2024 with 2,778 respondents from top venues including NeurIPS and ICML, produced a median estimate of 50% probability of human-level AI by 2047 — thirteen years earlier than the same cohort estimated in 2022.

Benjamin Todd, co-founder of 80,000 Hours, has identified four drivers of current AI progress — larger base models, learned reasoning, increased inference-time compute, and agent scaffolding — all of which are set to continue through at least 2028. His analysis suggests that "bottlenecks will likely start around 2030, meaning we'll either reach AGI by around 2030 or see progress slow significantly." Rob Wiblin, also at 80,000 Hours, documented in January 2026 how AGI timelines swung dramatically over the course of 2025 — compressing sharply after reasoning models appeared, then expanding again as scaling plateaus became apparent. Peter Wildeford — co-founder of the Institute for AI Policy and Strategy, first-place finisher in the 2025 Astral Codex Ten forecasting tournament out of nearly 3,000 competitors — has called 2025 a "make-or-break year" for interpreting AI's trajectory.

The Metaculus community forecast, aggregating thousands of forecasters, puts the median date for AGI at 2033 — with a 25% chance by 2029. Over the past four years, the community's mean estimate has compressed from fifty years to five.

The details of these forecasts differ. The direction does not. Whether AGI arrives in five years or twenty-five, every serious forecasting effort has shortened its timeline. And the substrate thesis does not require AGI. It describes what is already happening: AI as the foundation of economic production and military power. The possibility of near-term AGI simply raises the stakes.

---

There is a peculiar silence in the global conversation about AI.

The dominant frames of discussion are well-established. There is the existential risk frame — pioneered by Bostrom, advanced by researchers at Anthropic, MIRI, and the Future of Life Institute, and given institutional weight by the International AI Safety Report of 2025. There is the disruption frame — focused on job displacement, inequality, bias, deepfakes, and the social consequences of automation. And there is the geopolitical competition frame — the US-China AI race, export controls, semiconductor supply chains, the struggle for technological supremacy.

What is almost entirely absent is a fourth question: could AI make the world *safer*?

A Cambridge University paper published in *Data & Policy* in 2024 noted that "peace remains a missing piece in conversations about AI" — a "blind spot that grows more urgent in the context of rising conflict and violence." The UN Security Council's open debates on AI and international peace have focused almost exclusively on risks and regulation. SIPRI's 2025 Yearbook chapter on AI frames the question through the lens of "how AI could create or exacerbate threats." Even MacAskill and Moorhouse, who identify opportunities to "dramatically improve quality of life and collective decision-making," do not systematically explore whether AI could reduce the probability of great-power war.

This series asks that question. It takes the EA community's understanding of AI's trajectory seriously — the substrate thesis, the intelligence explosion, the civilizational stakes — and applies it to a domain that the EA community itself has largely neglected: international security and the conditions for peace.

The premise is straightforward. If AI is becoming the substrate of civilization — the foundation on which economic production, military capability, and scientific progress increasingly depend — then the physical infrastructure that produces AI becomes the most strategically important industrial system in human history. The question is not whether that infrastructure matters. The Maduro operation, the Iran strikes, the $13.4 billion Pentagon budget line, the $443 billion hyperscaler capex surge — these have already answered that question. The question is whether the *structure* of that infrastructure — who builds it, where it is located, how it is governed — could itself be a source of stability and peace.

That is the question this series will answer.
