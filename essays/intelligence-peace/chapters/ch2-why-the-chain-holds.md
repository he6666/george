# Chapter 2: Why the Chain Holds

---

Consider a single machine. It weighs 150 metric tons — more than a Boeing 757. It contains over 100,000 individual components and two kilometers of internal cabling. Shipping one requires forty freight containers, twenty trucks, and three Boeing 747 cargo planes. It costs upward of $370 million. And it is the most important machine in the world.

ASML's extreme ultraviolet lithography system is the only device on Earth capable of printing the transistor patterns required for cutting-edge semiconductors — the chips that train and run frontier AI models. ASML, headquartered in Veldhoven, the Netherlands, is the sole manufacturer. But ASML cannot build the machine alone. The mirror optics system comes from Carl Zeiss SMT in Oberkochen, Germany — mirrors polished to sub-atomic precision, where the largest imperfection, if scaled to the size of Germany, would be a fraction of a millimeter. Zeiss is the sole supplier. The CO2 laser system that powers the EUV light source comes from Trumpf, also in Germany. The light source technology itself — tin droplets struck by a laser 50,000 times per second to produce extreme ultraviolet photons — was developed by Cymer, a San Diego company ASML acquired in 2013. Japanese firms supply precision stages and measurement systems. The EUV program took more than twenty years of development and billions of dollars in R&D distributed across companies, universities, and national laboratories in three countries.

No single country can build this machine. And this machine is the bottleneck through which every advanced AI chip must pass.

Zoom out from the machine to the supply chain it anchors, and the picture becomes more extraordinary. The United States dominates chip design — Nvidia, AMD, Qualcomm, Apple, Broadcom — and the electronic design automation software that makes design possible, where Synopsys and Cadence hold roughly 74% of the global market. Taiwan, through TSMC, fabricates approximately 90% of the world's most advanced semiconductors. South Korea, through Samsung and SK Hynix, produces over 70% of DRAM memory. Japan supplies more than half of the world's semiconductor materials — the ultra-high-purity silicon wafers from Shin-Etsu and SUMCO, the photoresists from JSR and TOK, the specialty chemicals without which fabrication is impossible. Germany provides the optics and lasers. The United Kingdom, through ARM, provides the instruction set architecture that dominates mobile and is increasingly important in data centers.

According to Accenture, the inputs to a typical integrated circuit cross more than seventy international borders before a final product reaches consumers. Manufacturing involves over a thousand processing steps across a dozen or more countries, takes four to six months, and passes through five hundred discrete production stages. A chip is not made in a country. It is made by the world.

Even TSMC — the most important manufacturer on the planet, a company that single-handedly fabricates the silicon brains of the AI revolution — cannot function without the rest of the chain. More than 80% of its equipment comes from non-Taiwanese sources. Its process design kits are built for American EDA software. Its wafers come from Japan. Its lithography machines come from the Netherlands. Its customers' designs rely on ARM's architecture from the UK. TSMC is not an island of self-sufficiency. It is a node in a network, and the network has no center.

This is the phenomenon that requires explanation. The most strategically important industrial system in human history — the system that produces the substrate of AI — is distributed across a dozen democracies, with extreme concentration at every layer and no single country controlling more than one or two segments. This is not an accident. It is not a policy choice that a sufficiently determined government could reverse. It is the natural result of economic forces that have been operating for decades and are intensifying with every new generation of technology.

The numerous points of failure are a feature, not a bug. They are the structural foundation of the argument this series will make.

---

Why does the supply chain look this way? Three economic forces — each well-established in theory and confirmed by decades of empirical evidence — explain the pattern.

The first is comparative advantage, applied to the most complex manufacturing process humans have ever devised. Different countries dominate different segments because of genuine, deep, path-dependent advantages that cannot be purchased or decreed into existence. Taiwan's fabrication dominance arose from deliberate industrial policy — Morris Chang founding TSMC in 1987 with government backing — combined with a large, highly trained engineering workforce and decades of accumulated process knowledge. But Chang himself embodies the global nature of the ecosystem: born in China, educated at MIT and Stanford, a twenty-five-year career at Texas Instruments before returning to Taiwan. The Netherlands dominates lithography because Philips Research Laboratories in Eindhoven had been working on optical lithography since the 1960s, building an ecosystem of precision engineering firms and optics expertise that ASML inherited and scaled. Japan dominates materials because of a chemical engineering tradition stretching back a century. These are not interchangeable capabilities. They are the product of specific institutional histories, talent pools, educational systems, and industrial cultures that developed over generations.

The second force is increasing returns to scale. Semiconductor manufacturing exhibits extreme learning curves — what economists call Wright's Law, after Theodore Wright's 1936 observation that the cost of producing airplanes fell by a fixed percentage with each doubling of cumulative production. The same dynamic operates in chip fabrication, but with far greater intensity. Each new process node requires cumulative knowledge from all previous nodes. TSMC's cost advantage over competitors is not primarily a matter of capital investment — it is a matter of cumulative production volume. TSMC has fabricated more leading-edge wafers than any competitor, and each wafer taught its engineers something that cannot be learned from a textbook or replicated in a laboratory. This is what Brian Arthur, in his landmark work on increasing returns and path dependence, called dynamic increasing returns: the more you produce, the better you get, and the better you get, the more you produce. The result is natural oligopoly. Paul Krugman's new trade theory explains the international dimension: when production exhibits increasing returns, trade between nations generates welfare gains far beyond what classical Ricardian models predict, because each country's specialization allows it to operate further along the learning curve than it could if it tried to produce everything domestically.

The third force is winner-take-most dynamics at the leading edge. A single leading-edge fabrication facility costs $15 to $20 billion. TSMC's total investment in its Arizona complex has reached $165 billion across six planned fabs. The critical variable is yield — the percentage of functional chips per wafer — and yield is determined by accumulated process refinements that compound over years of production. Higher yields mean lower costs per chip, which mean more revenue, which mean more R&D investment, which mean higher yields. This is John Sutton's endogenous sunk costs creating natural oligopoly: the very structure of the industry's cost function guarantees that only a handful of firms can survive at the frontier. The number of companies capable of leading-edge fabrication has collapsed from approximately twenty-five in 2001 to two — TSMC and Samsung — in 2024, with Intel attempting to rejoin after years of delays. Barriers to entry do not merely persist. They increase with every generation.

BCG and the Semiconductor Industry Association estimated in 2021 that building fully self-sufficient national supply chains would require over $1 trillion in incremental investment and increase chip prices by 35 to 65 percent. Their 2024 follow-up report, incorporating post-CHIPS Act data, confirmed that the fundamental economics had not changed. The cost of autarky is not merely high. It is prohibitive — and it grows with each new process node.

---

If the economics are so clear, why do nations keep trying?

Every major attempt at semiconductor autarky in the modern era has either failed outright or fallen dramatically short of its targets. The pattern is consistent enough to constitute an empirical law.

The most instructive case is the Soviet Union. Soviet scientists developed transistor and early integrated circuit technology roughly in parallel with the West through the 1950s and 1960s. The gap opened in the 1970s, as Western semiconductor firms — competing fiercely for commercial markets — drove rapid innovation while Soviet enterprises, insulated from competitive pressure, stagnated. The Soviet response combined domestic R&D with industrial espionage: reverse-engineering Intel processors, importing equipment through front companies to circumvent COCOM export controls. It failed, for four reasons that remain relevant today. First, without market competition, there was no incentive to optimize for yield, cost, or performance. Second, isolation from the global knowledge ecosystem — conferences, publications, the tacit knowledge embedded in supplier relationships — meant Soviet engineers were working blind. Third, the Soviets could not manufacture precision lithography equipment, and reverse-engineering the physical artifacts failed because tacit manufacturing knowledge cannot be extracted from the artifact itself. Fourth, the domestic market was too small to achieve the cumulative production volumes needed for learning-curve cost reductions.

By the mid-1980s, Soviet microprocessors were ten to fifteen years behind Western equivalents. The gap contributed directly to the broader technological stagnation that undermined Soviet economic and military competitiveness. The lesson was not that autarky was expensive. It was that autarky was impossible at the frontier — because the frontier is defined by the integration of knowledge, equipment, and talent distributed across the global ecosystem.

China's "Made in China 2025" initiative is the contemporary test case — the most heavily funded attempt at semiconductor self-sufficiency in history, and it is failing to meet its own targets. The original goals, set in 2015, called for 40% semiconductor self-sufficiency by 2020 and 70% by 2025. As of March 2026, China has achieved approximately 40% overall self-sufficiency — impressive growth from roughly 20% a decade ago, but far short of the 70% target, and much of that figure represents mature-node production and output from foreign firms operating in China rather than frontier capability.

The most important data point is SMIC, China's most advanced domestic foundry. SMIC achieved 7-nanometer production in 2023 using DUV multi-patterning — an ingenious workaround that avoids the EUV machines China cannot obtain. By December 2025, SMIC had pushed to 5-nanometer, producing Huawei's Kirin 9030 processor. This is a genuine achievement. But the details reveal its limits. SMIC's yields on the 7-nanometer Ascend 910C improved from 20% to roughly 40% — compared to TSMC's yields exceeding 90% on comparable nodes. The DUV multi-patterning approach makes production approximately 50% more expensive per wafer than TSMC's EUV equivalent. SMIC has begun research on 3-nanometer technology, but it remains two or more generations behind TSMC, and the gap in cost and yield shows no sign of closing.

China's equipment self-sufficiency has surged to approximately 35% by late 2025, up from 13.6% in early 2024 — strong progress in etching, cleaning, and chemical-mechanical planarization. But the critical gap remains: EUV lithography. China has assembled a prototype, but functional EUV chips are not expected before 2028 at the earliest, measured against ASML's twenty-year development timeline. EDA tools are similarly constrained — domestic alternatives like Empyrean are estimated to be ten to fifteen years behind Synopsys and Cadence. The talent gap persists: China is short an estimated 200,000 to 300,000 experienced semiconductor engineers.

The investment has been enormous. China's Big Fund has deployed over $100 billion across three phases — $21 billion in 2014, $29 billion in 2019, and $47.5 billion in 2024 — supplemented by provincial and local funds. The returns have been disappointing, and the program has been plagued by corruption scandals, with multiple executives arrested in 2022.

Other autarky attempts reinforce the pattern. India's most advanced project — a Tata-PSMC fab in Gujarat — targets mature 28-nanometer nodes, with no leading-edge ambitions. Japan's Rapidus, backed by an IBM partnership, has demonstrated working 2-nanometer transistors and secured $1.7 billion in funding, with mass production targeted for 2027. If successful, Rapidus would add a new node to the democratic supply chain — a development that strengthens global integration rather than breaking from it. The European Chips Act committed €43 billion to semiconductor sovereignty, but its centerpiece — an Intel fab in Magdeburg, Germany — was cancelled in July 2025 after Intel's $16.6 billion net loss made the project untenable. The EU's target of 20% global market share by 2030 is now in serious doubt.

The pattern across seven decades and five continents is consistent. The Soviet Union fell ten to fifteen years behind. China hit 40% against a 70% target. India is decades from the frontier. Europe's flagship project collapsed. Japan's most promising initiative would, if it succeeds, join the global network rather than replace it. The common thread is that the semiconductor supply chain's complexity exceeds what any single nation can replicate within a strategically relevant timeframe — and that complexity is increasing, not decreasing.

---

The supply chain is not merely integrated today. It is becoming more integrated, more specialized, and harder to replicate with each passing year. Four forces drive this deepening.

The first is accelerating concentration. The number of companies capable of leading-edge fabrication has collapsed from roughly twenty-five at the 130-nanometer node in 2001, to ten at 32-nanometer in 2010, to four at 10-nanometer in 2017 — when GlobalFoundries abandoned the leading edge, leaving three — to two at 3-nanometer in 2024. Each new node requires more capital, more specialized knowledge, and more precise equipment. The barriers to entry do not merely persist. They compound.

The second is Wright's Law operating at civilizational scale. TSMC's cumulative production volume so far exceeds any competitor's that its cost advantage compounds with every wafer it ships. This is not a lead that can be closed by spending. It can only be closed by producing — and producing at the frontier takes decades of learning-by-doing that no amount of capital can substitute for.

The third is ecosystem lock-in. TSMC's process design kits represent billions of dollars in customer-side engineering investment. Apple, Nvidia, and AMD have spent years optimizing their chip designs for TSMC's specific manufacturing processes. Switching foundries requires not months but years of re-engineering, re-validation, and re-qualification. TSMC co-develops its manufacturing processes with ASML, Applied Materials, and other equipment makers through deep intellectual property sharing and joint R&D that newcomers cannot replicate. The knowledge ecosystem itself — the international conferences (IEDM, ISSCC, VLSI Symposia) where cutting-edge research is disseminated — reinforces integration. Isolation from these forums means falling behind.

The fourth, and deepest, is tacit knowledge. Much of what makes semiconductor manufacturing work is not written down. It is embedded in the skills and experience of individual engineers and teams, transmitted through apprenticeship and practice, not through documents or blueprints. This is Michael Polanyi's insight, articulated in *The Tacit Dimension*: "we can know more than we can tell." It explains why TSMC's yield advantages persist even though the basic physics of its processes are published in academic journals. It explains why TSMC's own Arizona fab, using identical equipment and the same process recipes, produces wafers at 30 to 50% higher cost than its facilities in Taiwan. The knowledge is in the people and in the accumulated refinements of millions of production runs. It cannot be purchased. It cannot be stolen. It can only be built, slowly, over years.

AnnaLee Saxenian documented the related phenomenon in *Regional Advantage* and *The New Argonauts*: the way knowledge circulates through regional clusters — Silicon Valley, Hsinchu Science Park, the Eindhoven high-tech campus — creating self-reinforcing ecosystems of innovation that are extraordinarily difficult to replicate elsewhere. These ecosystems are not barriers that can be overcome with sufficient investment. They are the accumulated output of specific histories, institutions, and human networks that took decades to form.

The implication is counterintuitive but inescapable. The more advanced the technology becomes, the more globally specialized the supply chain must be. This is not a temporary condition that onshoring programs will resolve. It is the equilibrium outcome of increasing returns, learning-by-doing, agglomeration, tacit knowledge, and ecosystem lock-in — forces that intensify over time. The AI supply chain is not drifting toward fragmentation. It is locking into deeper integration with every new generation of chips.

---

There are serious counterarguments, and intellectual honesty requires engaging with them.

The strongest is that China could achieve "good enough" self-sufficiency without matching the frontier. Most semiconductors are not leading-edge. Mature-node chips — 28-nanometer and above — serve automotive, industrial, IoT, and many military applications, and China has growing capacity in these segments. For AI inference, as opposed to training, less-than-cutting-edge chips may eventually suffice. This is legitimate. But frontier AI training — the capability that determines who leads the intelligence explosion — requires the most advanced chips available. The peace thesis applies most directly to frontier capabilities, where SMIC's 50% cost penalty and two-generation lag are not inconveniences but structural barriers.

A second counterargument holds that technological breakthroughs — quantum computing, photonic chips, neuromorphic architectures — could bypass the current supply chain entirely. This is plausible on a fifteen- to thirty-year horizon. But history offers a consistent lesson: new technological paradigms create new specializations and new chokepoints, not a return to autarky. The transition from planar transistors to FinFETs did not democratize fabrication. It concentrated it further. There is no reason to believe that the next paradigm shift will be different.

A third points to the CHIPS Act. With $52.7 billion in US incentives and leading-edge fabs under construction by TSMC, Samsung, and Intel, won't the United States achieve meaningful self-sufficiency? The answer is instructive. The CHIPS Act increases US production capacity, but it cannot achieve independence. Total global semiconductor capital expenditure runs $150 to $180 billion per year; $52.7 billion over five years is significant but not transformative. More fundamentally, the new American fabs still depend on ASML equipment from the Netherlands, Zeiss optics from Germany, and materials from Japan. Onshoring fabrication moves one link of the chain while leaving all others in place. It redistributes a node. It does not break the network.

A fourth invokes wartime mobilization — the Manhattan Project, the Apollo program — as evidence that governments can achieve seemingly impossible technological goals when sufficiently motivated. The parallel has surface appeal but breaks down on examination. The Manhattan Project required physics breakthroughs but relatively simple manufacturing by modern standards. Semiconductor manufacturing requires the opposite: well-understood physics but extraordinarily complex, globally distributed manufacturing. You cannot build an ASML EUV machine on a wartime footing if you do not have Zeiss optics, and Zeiss optics require years to produce.

A fifth, the most speculative but intellectually honest, suggests that AI itself could compress the catch-up timeline — accelerating materials science, chip design, and manufacturing optimization to the point where autarky becomes feasible. Two responses: AI also accelerates the leader's progress, so the gap may not close. And the very technology that could enable catch-up is the technology the supply chain produces — a chicken-and-egg problem for would-be autarkists who lack access to the frontier chips needed to train the AI that might help them build frontier chips.

---

The weight of evidence points in one direction. The AI supply chain will remain globally integrated for at least the next ten to fifteen years, and probably longer. Six reinforcing forces converge on this conclusion. Deepening specialization and increasing returns — concentration from twenty-five companies to two in two decades, with barriers still rising. No single country controlling all layers — the US leads design, Taiwan fabrication, the Netherlands lithography, Japan materials, Germany optics. Tacit knowledge that cannot be purchased — TSMC's own American fabs cost 30 to 50% more with identical equipment. Historical autarky that has universally failed — the Soviet Union fell a generation behind; China hit 40% against a 70% target. Cost penalties that are prohibitive — full onshoring would require over $1 trillion and raise chip prices by 35 to 65%. A talent pool that is inherently global — over 65% of top AI researchers in the United States are foreign-born.

This does not mean the supply chain is invulnerable. Determined autarky efforts, while failing at full independence, could partially degrade interdependence — and partial decoupling partially undermines the peace mechanism. That is a failure condition this series will examine honestly. Nor does the thesis apply with equal force everywhere. It is strongest for frontier AI — the training of large models on leading-edge chips — and weaker for mature-node production and inference workloads, where national self-sufficiency is more feasible. It is strongest for the period from 2025 to 2040, and less certain thereafter.

But within those bounds, the conclusion is clear. Global integration is not a fragile historical accident. It is not a policy choice that a sufficiently ambitious government can reverse. It is the equilibrium outcome of economic forces — increasing returns, learning-by-doing, agglomeration, tacit knowledge, ecosystem lock-in — that intensify over time. The supply chain does not merely happen to be global. It must be global at the frontier, because no single nation can replicate the full stack of knowledge, equipment, materials, talent, and accumulated process refinements within any strategically relevant timeframe.

This is the long-term steady state. And it is on this structural foundation that the Intelligence Peace Thesis rests.
