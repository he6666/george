# Chapter 3: The Intelligence Peace Thesis

---

The previous two chapters established two empirical conditions. The first: artificial intelligence is becoming the substrate of civilization — the foundational layer on which economic production, military power, and scientific progress increasingly depend. The second: the supply chain that produces AI hardware is globally integrated, with irreplaceable chokepoints distributed across multiple nations, and the economic forces driving this integration are intensifying over time.

If you accept these conditions — and the evidence for both is, at this point, overwhelming — then what follows is not a speculation. It is a deduction.

---

Begin with a thought experiment. Suppose a nation decides to invade Taiwan.

Set aside, for the moment, the conventional military calculus — the hundred-mile strait, the amphibious logistics, the US security commitment. Focus only on the supply chain.

TSMC fabricates roughly 90% of the world's most advanced semiconductors. These chips train frontier AI models, power the data centers that run them, and increasingly operate the military systems that defend the nations that depend on them. An invasion of Taiwan would not merely risk damaging TSMC's fabrication facilities. It would, with near certainty, destroy them. Modern semiconductor fabs are among the most fragile industrial installations ever built. They require vibration-free environments, ultra-pure cleanrooms, continuous power, and thousands of precisely calibrated machines operating in sequence. An artillery shell landing within a kilometer of a fab renders it inoperable. A cyberattack on its environmental controls could contaminate months of work-in-progress. TSMC's own contingency planning reportedly includes the ability to remotely disable its most advanced equipment — a "silicon scorched earth" option that would deny an invader the very prize it sought.

But suppose, against all odds, the invader captures the fabs intact. What then?

The fabs cannot operate without ASML's EUV lithography machines, which require continuous maintenance, software updates, and replacement parts that only ASML can provide. They cannot operate without Zeiss optics, Trumpf lasers, Japanese photoresists, American EDA software, and a global supply of ultra-high-purity materials. The moment an invasion begins, every supplier nation cuts off the occupied facilities. Not out of solidarity — out of self-interest. The Netherlands does not need a treaty obligation to stop shipping EUV parts to an invader occupying its most important customer. Japan does not need a UN resolution to halt photoresist exports. The United States does not need a formal alliance commitment to embargo its EDA software. Each nation acts individually, because each nation's own AI infrastructure depends on the supply chain continuing to function — and an occupied Taiwan, cut off from its suppliers, produces nothing.

The invader has captured a building. It has not captured a capability. The capability is distributed across the world, and no military operation can seize it all.

This is the first mechanism of the Intelligence Peace: **mutual restraint through structural interdependence**.

---

The logic of mutual restraint extends far beyond the Taiwan scenario. Consider it from the perspective of every major node on the chain.

Taiwan cannot attack the Netherlands — because TSMC needs ASML's EUV machines, and there is no alternative supplier on Earth. The Netherlands cannot attack Taiwan — because ASML's commercial viability depends on TSMC being its largest customer. Neither can attack Japan — because both depend on Japanese materials and equipment. Japan cannot attack South Korea — because the memory chips fabricated by Samsung and SK Hynix are essential components of the AI systems that Japan's economy increasingly requires. South Korea cannot attack the United States — because Korean chip designs depend on American EDA tools and many of their end customers are American technology companies. The United States cannot attack any of them — because American AI companies depend on chips fabricated in Taiwan, using equipment from the Netherlands, with materials from Japan and memory from South Korea.

Every node depends on every other node. Attacking any node is attacking yourself.

This is not bilateral deterrence, the way nuclear weapons create a two-party standoff between the United States and Russia, or between India and Pakistan. It is multilateral and structural. The entire chain is locked into a web of mutual dependency where no single actor can weaponize its position without destroying its own access to the network. A country that controls one chokepoint — say, the Netherlands with EUV lithography — could in theory withhold its product from a specific customer, as the US-led export controls on China demonstrate. But it cannot survive the destruction of the broader network, because its own economy depends on the products that the rest of the network produces.

The critical distinction is between *selective denial* and *total destruction*. Selective denial — cutting a specific actor off from the network while the network continues to function — is an offensive capability that chokepoint nations can and do exercise. Total destruction — an invasion or attack that disables a major node for everyone — is categorically different. It triggers not retaliation but collapse. The aggressor does not face punishment from a vengeful adversary. It faces the cessation of its own AI capability, because the supply chain on which that capability depends has been broken.

This is deterrence without a deterrer. No one needs to threaten retaliation. The cost is inherent in the act itself.

---

But mutual restraint alone is not sufficient for peace. A deterrence mechanism that relies solely on self-harm has an obvious weakness: a sufficiently irrational or desperate actor might accept the cost. History offers examples — Japan's attack on Pearl Harbor, knowing it would bring the full weight of American industrial power into the war, is perhaps the most famous. Mutual restraint raises the cost of aggression. It does not guarantee that no actor will ever pay it.

This is where the second mechanism operates. And it is, in some ways, the more powerful of the two.

Consider again the Taiwan invasion scenario — but now from the perspective of every other nation that depends on the AI supply chain. The United States depends on TSMC for the chips that train its frontier AI models and power its military systems. If Taiwan's fabs go offline, the United States loses access to the most advanced semiconductors in the world for a period measured in years, not months. American AI companies — the engines of what Goldman Sachs, McKinsey, and PwC project to be the largest driver of economic growth in human history — would be crippled. The Pentagon's $13.4 billion AI and autonomy programs would lose their hardware foundation. The economic and military consequences would be catastrophic.

The same calculus applies to every other dependent nation. Japan depends on TSMC to fabricate the chips designed by its automotive and electronics industries. South Korea depends on the broader supply chain for the equipment and materials its own semiconductor firms require. Germany depends on the chips that increasingly run its industrial automation and automotive systems. The United Kingdom, France, Canada, Australia — every advanced economy that has integrated AI into its economic and military infrastructure depends, directly or through one intermediary, on the continued functioning of the AI supply chain.

This means that an attack on Taiwan is not an attack on Taiwan. It is an attack on every nation that depends on what Taiwan produces. And every one of those nations has an overwhelming, individually rational incentive to prevent it.

This is the second mechanism: **collective security through individual necessity**.

The distinction from traditional collective security arrangements is fundamental. Traditional collective security — the kind enshrined in the NATO charter's Article 5, or attempted by the League of Nations and the United Nations — suffers from three well-known problems.

The first is the free-rider problem. In a traditional alliance, each member has an incentive to let others bear the costs of collective defense. Why should Belgium spend 2% of GDP on defense when the United States will defend it regardless? This logic, which has plagued NATO since its founding, weakens the credibility of the collective commitment.

The second is the credibility problem. Would the United States really risk nuclear war to defend Estonia? Would France really go to war over a Baltic state? The entire theory of extended deterrence rests on adversaries believing that the answer is yes — but rational analysis suggests the answer might be no, and adversaries know this.

The third is the coordination problem. Even when all members agree that collective action is necessary, organizing a response takes time, and delay can be fatal. The UN Security Council's inability to act in the face of vetoes is the canonical example.

The Intelligence Peace resolves all three.

There is no free-rider problem because the cost of inaction is borne individually by each dependent nation. The United States does not defend Taiwan's fabs out of alliance solidarity. It defends them because losing access to TSMC's output would cripple its own AI industry and military capability. Japan does not contribute to Taiwan's defense because of a treaty obligation. It contributes because its own economic survival depends on the supply chain remaining intact. Each nation acts in its own material self-interest. There is no public good to free-ride on — only a private necessity that each nation must secure for itself.

There is no credibility problem because the incentive is material, not promissory. A treaty commitment can be broken. A verbal guarantee can be walked back. But material self-interest is always credible, because it does not require anyone to keep a promise — only to act rationally. No adversary needs to wonder whether the United States "really would" defend the AI supply chain. The question answers itself: of course it would, because the alternative is the collapse of its own AI capability.

There is no coordination problem because the incentive is individually rational. Each nation independently concludes that defending the supply chain is in its own interest. Formal coordination helps — shared intelligence, joint military planning, interoperable systems — but it is not required for the basic deterrence to function. Even without a single phone call between capitals, every dependent nation would independently move to protect a threatened chokepoint, because each one's own survival demands it.

---

Put the two mechanisms together and the result is a deterrence structure unlike anything in the history of international relations.

An attacker contemplating aggression against a chokepoint nation faces two simultaneous barriers. The first is self-harm: the attack destroys the attacker's own access to the AI supply chain, imposing costs that — in a world where AI is the substrate of economic production and military power — are equivalent to voluntary civilizational regression. The second is collective opposition: every dependent nation has an individually rational, materially grounded incentive to defend the target, creating an implicit coalition that does not require formal organization to be effective.

The combined deterrent is stronger than either mechanism alone. Mutual restraint might fail against an irrational actor willing to absorb enormous self-inflicted costs. But that actor would also face the combined opposition of every major economy on Earth — not because those economies are altruistic, but because they are self-interested. Collective security might fail if the costs of intervention exceeded the costs of inaction. But the costs of inaction — permanent loss of access to frontier AI — are so high that intervention is the individually rational choice for every dependent nation.

The result is that any nation hosting an irreplaceable node on the AI supply chain is, in effect, too important to attack. Its security derives not from its military strength, not from its alliance memberships, not from its geographic position, but from its structural role in the production of the world's most important technology. The Netherlands, with seventeen million people and a modest military, becomes one of the most strategically protected nations on Earth — because ASML's EUV monopoly makes the Netherlands indispensable. Taiwan, perpetually vulnerable in conventional military analysis, becomes structurally defended — because TSMC's fabrication capacity makes Taiwan's destruction equivalent to a global AI shutdown.

This is the Intelligence Peace. Peace through structural position. Peace not because nations choose to be peaceful, but because the architecture of production makes war self-defeating.

---

Three clarifications are essential.

First, the Intelligence Peace Thesis is a prediction about a steady state, not a description of the present. The two conditions — existential AI dependency and an irreplaceable, globally integrated supply chain — are not yet fully realized. AI is becoming the substrate, but it has not yet become existentially important to the degree the thesis requires. The supply chain is globally integrated, but determined autarky efforts could partially erode that integration over time. The thesis describes the world as it is becoming, not the world as it is. The transition period — the years between now and the steady state — carries its own risks, and those risks are the subject of a later chapter.

Second, the thesis does not claim that war becomes impossible. No deterrence mechanism makes war impossible. Nuclear deterrence has prevented great-power war for eighty years, but no serious analyst claims nuclear war is impossible. The Intelligence Peace Thesis claims that war against chokepoint nations becomes *structurally irrational* — that the costs, both self-inflicted and externally imposed, exceed any plausible gain. Sufficiently irrational actors may still choose war, just as they may choose nuclear war. The thesis raises the bar for aggression; it does not eliminate it.

Third, the thesis protects chokepoint nations — nations that play irreplaceable roles in the AI supply chain. It does not, by itself, protect nations that are merely consumers of AI. A nation that depends on the supply chain but contributes nothing irreplaceable to it receives the benefits of the collective security mechanism — other nations will defend the supply chain on which it depends — but does not receive the direct protection of the mutual restraint mechanism. This asymmetry is real, and it has policy implications that later chapters will explore. For now, the key point is that the Intelligence Peace is not a universal peace theory. It is a structural theory about the conditions under which specific nations — those hosting critical nodes — are protected by their position in the production network.

---

The argument can be stated in a single paragraph.

If AI becomes the substrate of civilization, then access to the AI supply chain becomes an existential necessity for every advanced nation. If that supply chain is globally integrated with irreplaceable chokepoints, then attacking any chokepoint is self-defeating (because the attacker depends on what it destroys) and triggers collective defense (because every other dependent nation is individually motivated to prevent the disruption). Nations hosting chokepoints are therefore protected by structural position — not by military power, not by alliances, not by good intentions, but by the architecture of production itself. This is the Intelligence Peace.

The next four chapters test this thesis against the most demanding scrutiny available: the track record of prior peace theories, the literature on chokepoint power and coercion, the conditions under which the argument breaks, and the question of whether the supply chain's democratic structure is an accident or a feature. Only after surviving that scrutiny does the series conclude with what the thesis implies for policy — and why the window to act on it is closing.
