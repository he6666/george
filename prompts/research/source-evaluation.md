# Source Evaluation Rubric

This rubric is included in every research agent's context. Tag every source you encounter with its tier and a one-line justification.

---

## Tier 1 — Gold Standard
**Use freely. Cite with confidence.**

- Peer-reviewed academic papers in reputable journals
- Meta-analyses and systematic reviews
- Official government statistics and census data
- Reports from major international organizations (UN, World Bank, WHO, OECD, IMF)

## Tier 2 — Strong
**Use as primary evidence. Note provenance.**

- Working papers from recognized institutions (NBER, Brookings, RAND, CEPR)
- Books from academic or major trade presses
- Reports from established think tanks (note ideological orientation)
- Investigative journalism from major outlets (NYT, FT, WSJ, Economist, etc.)
- Congressional/parliamentary testimony and official hearing records

## Tier 3 — Useful
**Use for framing, context, and expert perspective. Corroborate key factual claims with Tier 1-2 sources.**

- Policy reports from advocacy organizations (note potential bias)
- Op-eds and essays by recognized domain experts
- Quality journalism and feature reporting
- Conference presentations and lecture notes
- Doctoral dissertations

## Tier 4 — Supporting
**Use for color, examples, and current context only. Never as the sole source for a factual claim.**

- Blog posts by domain experts (including Substack, personal sites)
- Newsletter essays
- Wikipedia (as a starting point for finding citations — never cite Wikipedia itself)
- Social media posts by relevant figures
- Interviews and podcast transcripts

## Tier 5 — Avoid
**Do not cite. Flag if other sources rely on these.**

- Unattributed claims
- Sources with clear undisclosed conflicts of interest
- Preprints that have been contradicted by subsequent peer review
- Aggregator sites that repackage others' work without original analysis
- Press releases without independent verification
- Self-published work with no editorial review and no established author reputation

---

## Exceptions (use judgment)

- A Tier 4 blog post by a Nobel laureate or recognized field leader is more authoritative than a Tier 1 paper by an unknown graduate student on the same topic.
- A Tier 2 think tank report may be compromised on topics where the think tank has known funding conflicts. Note the conflict.
- Recency matters: a Tier 1 paper from 2005 may be superseded by a Tier 2 working paper from 2025 with better data or methods.
- Pre-publication status is not disqualifying — an NBER working paper may be more current and relevant than a published paper from years earlier. But note that it hasn't been through peer review.
- In fast-moving fields (AI, crypto, pandemic response), Tier 3-4 sources may be the only timely ones available. Note this limitation explicitly.

---

## Tagging Format

For every source you list, include:

```
[Tier X] Author(s), "Title," Publication/Venue, Year. URL
  → Justification: One sentence on why this tier and what makes it useful/limited.
```

Example:
```
[Tier 1] Acemoglu & Johnson, "Power and Progress," MIT Press, 2023.
  → Justification: Peer-reviewed book from major academic press by two MIT economists; directly argues against techno-optimism with historical evidence.
```
