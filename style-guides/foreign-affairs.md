# Style Guide: Foreign Affairs

Foreign Affairs is the flagship publication of the Council on Foreign Relations. It has published serious analysis of international relations and US foreign policy since 1922. Writing for FA means joining a conversation that includes heads of state, cabinet officials, and the global policy elite.

---

## Tone & Voice

- **Authoritative and declarative.** State positions with confidence. Hedging should be rare and purposeful.
- **Institutional register.** The vocabulary is diplomatic and policy-oriented: "strategic interests," "multilateral frameworks," "balance of power," "policy implications."
- **Not academic.** FA is not a journal. Avoid jargon, excessive caveating, and academic signposting ("In this essay, I will argue..."). Instead, argue.
- **Not journalistic.** FA is not a newspaper. No inverted pyramid structure, no pull quotes, no "breaking news" framing. The assumption is that readers already know the basic facts.
- **Educated generalist audience.** The reader is a senior government official, a diplomat, a corporate strategist, or an informed citizen. They're smart but not specialists. Explain specialized concepts without being condescending.

## Structure

- **Length:** 3,000-5,000 words
- **No numbered sections.** Subheadings are optional and understated.
- **Opening:** Start with the stakes. Why does this matter? What's at risk? The first paragraph should make the reader understand why they need to read this now.
- **Historical framing:** FA essays are expected to situate arguments in historical context. Show that this problem has precedent — or show that it's genuinely unprecedented and explain why.
- **Analytical body:** The middle sections build the argument through evidence, comparison, and reasoning. Each section should advance a specific point.
- **Policy implications:** FA readers want to know "so what?" Every essay should include concrete implications — what should policymakers do? This is not optional.
- **Conclusion:** End with a clear recommendation or a sobering assessment of what happens if the recommendation is ignored.

## Evidence & Citation

- **Endnotes, not inline citations.** Sources are cited in numbered endnotes at the end of the piece.
- **Evidence style:** Weave evidence into the prose. Don't list studies; deploy them as supporting moments in the argument.
- **Authoritative sourcing expected.** Government reports, established think tanks, peer-reviewed research, and recognized experts. FA editors will check your sources.
- **Data should be concrete and current.** "GDP grew by 4.2% in 2024" > "economic growth has been strong."
- **Historical examples carry weight.** FA readers respect argument by precedent — "The last time a great power did X, the result was Y."

## What FA Editors Want

- **Original argument.** Not a summary of what others think. What is YOUR claim?
- **Policy relevance.** Pure theory without policy implications will be rejected. The question is always: what should be done?
- **Engagement with counterarguments.** Show you've considered the objections. FA readers are too sophisticated for one-sided arguments.
- **Timeliness without being reactive.** The essay should be relevant to current events but not enslaved to the news cycle. It should still be worth reading in six months.
- **A clear throughline.** The reader should be able to state the essay's main argument in one sentence after reading it.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing an academic paper in FA's format (too many caveats, too little conviction)
- Writing a news analysis (too reactive, no original argument)
- Failing to answer "so what?" (interesting analysis with no policy upshot)
- Overusing acronyms without explanation
- Being too US-centric without acknowledging it (FA has a global readership)
- Treating allies as monolithic (e.g., "Europe wants X" — which European country? In what context?)

## Example Opening Patterns

- **Stakes-first:** "The post-World War II international order is under greater strain than at any point since its creation. [Why this is happening and what's at stake.]"
- **Counterintuitive claim:** "The conventional wisdom about X is wrong. [Here's what's actually happening.]"
- **Historical parallel:** "In 1947, the United States faced a choice that would define the next half-century. Today, a similar choice presents itself. [What it is and why it matters.]"
