# Style Guide: LessWrong & EA Forum

LessWrong and the EA Forum share an intellectual culture rooted in Bayesian reasoning, explicit epistemics, and a commitment to taking ideas seriously. This guide covers both venues, with notes where they diverge.

---

## Tone & Voice

- **Intellectual conversation among peers.** Formal but not stiff. Imagine you're presenting to a room of smart, skeptical people who will challenge you in Q&A.
- **Precision over eloquence.** Say exactly what you mean. If a sentence is beautiful but ambiguous, rewrite it to be clear.
- **First person is fine.** "I think," "I believe," "My model is..." — intellectual honesty about the authorial perspective is valued.
- **Humor is welcome** if it serves the argument. Irony, wit, and the occasional aside are part of the culture. Just don't let it undermine serious points.
- **Avoid status games.** Don't name-drop. Don't appeal to credentials. The argument stands or falls on its own.

## Epistemic Standards

This is the defining feature of rationalist writing.

- **Epistemic status header.** Open every essay with a brief statement of your confidence level:
  > *Epistemic status: Moderately confident. This is based on 6 months of research and 3 prior essays on the topic, but I haven't run formal models and I'm uncertain about the causal mechanism in Section 3.*

- **Explicit uncertainty.** Use probability language where appropriate: "I'd put this at ~70% likely" or "I think this is probably true (65-80%)" or "I'm genuinely uncertain about this (40-60%)."
- **Distinguish levels of confidence.** Make it clear which claims you'd bet on and which are speculative.
- **Update openly.** If you changed your mind during research, say so. "I started this essay expecting to argue X, but the evidence convinced me that Y is closer to the truth."
- **Bayesian framing welcome.** Prior probabilities, likelihood ratios, and Bayesian updating are part of the native vocabulary. Use them when they clarify.

## Structure

- **Epistemic status** at the top
- **TL;DR or summary** (3-5 sentences capturing the full argument — not a teaser, the actual conclusion)
- **Numbered or titled sections** — the community expects clearly delineated structure
- **Length:** 2,000-6,000+ words. Depth is valued over brevity. A 10,000-word deep dive will get read if it earns every paragraph.
- **Flexible structure.** No rigid template. The structure should follow the argument. Common approaches:
  - Build from first principles → develop framework → apply to specific question → implications
  - Present puzzle → eliminate wrong answers → arrive at best explanation
  - State thesis → steelman objections → respond to each → synthesize

## Evidence & Citation

- **Inline links.** Cite by linking to the source in the text: "as [Author argues](URL)..." or "[This study](URL) found that..."
- **Quantitative where possible.** Numbers, estimates, and calculations are expected. If you claim X is important, readers will want to know how important (order of magnitude at minimum).
- **Fermi estimates are valued.** Even rough quantification (back-of-envelope calculations) is better than pure qualitative assertion.
- **Link to prior work.** If someone has written about this before on LessWrong or EA Forum, engage with their post. Don't reinvent the wheel.
- **Cost-effectiveness framing** (especially EA Forum): If arguing for a cause area or intervention, include cost-effectiveness estimates or at least comparative analysis.

## Counterarguments

- **Steelman everything.** This is non-negotiable. If you present a counterargument, it must be the strongest version. Readers will immediately notice strawmanning.
- **Engage substantively.** Don't just mention the counterargument — respond to it with evidence and reasoning.
- **Concede when appropriate.** Partial concession is respected. "This objection is partially valid — it reduces the expected impact by roughly 30%, but the argument still holds because..."
- **Name the cruxes.** What would change your mind? What evidence would make you abandon your position? Identifying cruxes is central to productive rationalist discourse.

## LessWrong-Specific Notes

- **Engage with the sequences** if relevant. Concepts from Eliezer Yudkowsky's sequences, Scott Alexander's writing, and the broader rationalist canon can be referenced without extensive explanation.
- **Original thinking valued.** LessWrong rewards novel frameworks, new analyses, and fresh perspectives. Summarizing existing work without adding insight will not land well.
- **Technical depth welcome.** If the argument benefits from formal models, mathematical notation, or code, include them.

## EA Forum-Specific Notes

- **Cause-area awareness.** If your essay relates to an EA priority area (global health, animal welfare, existential risk, AI safety), connect it to the broader cause prioritization framework.
- **Explicit about assumptions.** EA readers want to see your model — what are the key assumptions, and how sensitive are your conclusions to changes in those assumptions?
- **Sensitivity analysis.** "Even if my estimate of X is off by 2x, the conclusion still holds because..." — this kind of robustness check is expected.
- **Worldview diversification.** Acknowledge that reasonable people with different moral frameworks might weigh the considerations differently.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing with false confidence (the community will call you on it)
- Strawmanning opposing views (immediate credibility loss)
- Failing to engage with relevant prior work on LW/EA Forum
- Being vague when you could be quantitative
- Treating uncertainty as weakness rather than intellectual honesty
- Writing for a general audience rather than a rationalist one (unnecessary explanation of concepts the audience already knows)
- Neglecting to identify cruxes

## Example Epistemic Status Headers

- *Epistemic status: High confidence in the empirical claims (based on multiple RCTs), moderate confidence in the causal model, low confidence in the policy recommendations (limited evidence on implementation).*
- *Epistemic status: Speculative. This is a hypothesis I find promising (60-70% that the core mechanism is real), but I haven't found strong enough evidence to be confident. Writing this partly to get feedback and identify holes in my reasoning.*
- *Epistemic status: This is a literature review + synthesis, not original research. I'm fairly confident the summary is accurate but may have missed important work.*
