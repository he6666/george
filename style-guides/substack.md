# Style Guide: Substack

Substack is where the best writers of the internet era have built audiences. The best Substack essays are personal, substantive, and narrative-driven — they combine the rigor of long-form journalism with the voice and freedom of a personal blog. The author's personality is the product.

---

## Tone & Voice

- **Conversational but substantive.** Write like you're talking to a smart friend over coffee — except you've done serious homework.
- **First person freely.** "I," "we," "you" — the reader is in conversation with a person, not an institution.
- **The author's personality is the product.** Asides, humor, tangents, speculation — all fine if they serve the piece. Don't be boring in the name of objectivity.
- **Strong opinions, loosely held.** Take a clear position. Readers subscribe to Substack writers for their perspective, not for balanced on-the-other-hand coverage.
- **Accessible without dumbing down.** Explain complex ideas clearly, but don't condescend. Trust the reader to follow a sophisticated argument if you write it well.

## Structure

- **Length:** 1,500-4,000 words typical. Can go longer if the writing sustains it, but every paragraph past 4,000 words needs to justify its existence.
- **Strong first sentence.** This is the most important sentence in the essay. Substack essays arrive in email inboxes — the reader decides in the first 2 sentences whether to keep reading. Open with a hook:
  - An arresting claim
  - A vivid scene or anecdote
  - A question the reader didn't know they had
  - A counterintuitive observation
  - A personal moment that connects to a bigger idea
- **Short paragraphs.** 2-4 sentences typically. White space is your friend. Substack essays are read on screens, often on phones.
- **Subheadings for scanability.** Break the essay into clearly labeled sections. A reader who skims the subheadings should get the gist.
- **Flexible structure.** No rigid formula. Common approaches:
  - Anecdote → Zoom out to the big idea → Evidence/analysis → So what → Personal close
  - Question → Exploration → Surprising answer → Implications
  - "Here's what everyone thinks" → "Here's why they're wrong" → "Here's what's actually going on"
- **Bold for emphasis.** Use **bold** to highlight key phrases. In a sea of text, bold acts as a scannable outline.
- **Pull-quotes or callouts** for key points (sparingly — not every paragraph needs one).

## Evidence & Citation

- **Inline links.** Casual citation style: "as [Name argues in this piece](URL)..." or "The data, [from this study](URL), shows that..."
- **Narrative evidence.** Stories, examples, and case studies carry more weight than abstract data — but the best Substack essays combine both.
- **Personal experience counts.** If you have relevant firsthand experience, use it. "I worked in X industry for 5 years, and here's what I saw" is powerful on Substack.
- **Don't over-cite.** This isn't an academic paper. Link when the reader might want to follow up, not to prove you did your homework.
- **Credit generously.** If someone else's idea shaped your thinking, link to them. Substack writers support each other — and it builds credibility.

## The Email-First Rule

Substack essays arrive in inboxes. This has structural implications:

- **The opening must work as an email preview.** The first 2-3 sentences appear in the email notification. They must be compelling enough to get the click.
- **Don't bury the lede.** The main idea should be clear within the first few paragraphs — some readers won't scroll past the email preview.
- **Images help in email.** A good header image or visual early in the piece breaks up the text and catches the eye in an inbox.
- **Formatting must survive email.** Complex layouts, embedded videos, and interactive elements don't render consistently in email clients. Keep it simple: text, images, links, bold.

## What Works on Substack

- **Original thinking.** Readers pay for perspectives they can't get elsewhere.
- **Personal voice.** The writing should sound like a specific human, not a content mill.
- **Narrative hooks.** Start with a story, a scene, or a moment. Then expand to the bigger idea.
- **"I changed my mind" essays.** Intellectual honesty about updating your views is magnetic on Substack.
- **Connecting dots.** The best Substack essays connect ideas from different domains in ways readers haven't seen before.
- **Practical takeaways.** "Here's what this means for you" — readers appreciate when abstract ideas are made actionable.

## What Doesn't Work

- **Academic tone.** Too formal, too cautious, too many caveats. Substack readers want conviction and clarity.
- **Hot takes without substance.** Opinions need to be backed by evidence and reasoning. "I think X" is boring. "Here's why X, and here's the evidence" is compelling.
- **SEO-driven writing.** Substack isn't a blog competing for Google traffic. Write for your subscribers, not for algorithms.
- **Clickbait without payoff.** Provocative titles are fine if the essay delivers. If it doesn't, you'll lose subscribers.
- **Excessive length without pacing.** A 5,000-word essay needs variation in pace — moments of intensity, moments of reflection, moments of humor. Unrelenting density will lose readers.

## Common Mistakes

- Opening with throat-clearing instead of a hook ("In recent years, there has been growing interest in...")
- Writing for fellow experts instead of smart generalists
- Forgetting that the reader is on their phone, in their inbox, with 47 other unread newsletters
- Being boring in the name of being thorough
- Failing to have a clear, memorable takeaway
- Not using the first person when a personal angle would strengthen the piece

## Example Openings

- **Anecdote hook:** "Three years ago, I sat across from a venture capitalist who told me, with complete sincerity, that [surprising claim]. I laughed. I shouldn't have."
- **Counterintuitive claim:** "Everything you've read about [topic] is wrong. Not slightly wrong — directionally wrong."
- **Question hook:** "What would you do if you discovered that the thing you'd spent your career building was actively making things worse?"
- **Scene-setting:** "It's 2:30 AM in [place] and [vivid detail]. This is where the story of [topic] begins."
